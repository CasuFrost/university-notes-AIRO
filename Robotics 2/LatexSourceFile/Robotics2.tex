\chapter{Introduction}
\section{An Overview in Dynamics}
Previously, in the \href{https://github.com/CasuFrost/university-notes-AIRO/raw/main/Robotics%201/LatexSourceFile/Robotics1.pdf}{Robotics 1} course, we described the kinematic behavior of a robotic manipulator, assuming that no forces are involved, and that we can control directly the angular velocity of each joint, without any kind of resistance.
We consider the following example:\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth ]{images/no_dyn_example.eps}
\end{center}
It is clear that, considering the positive angular velocity $v>0$, in the next configuration the second link will rotate counter-clockwise, while the first joint remains fixed. If we have to take into account the dynamics, we should also consider\begin{itemize}
    \item the center of mass of the first and the second link
    \item the mass of the first and the second link
    \item the inertia of the first and the second link
\end{itemize}
and instead of describing the control through angular velocities on the two joints, we define the torques $\tau_1,\tau_2$ applied on them. In this case (we ignore the effects of gravity), by applying a zero torque $\tau_1=0$ on the first joint, and a positive torque $\tau_2>0$ on the second joint, both joints will move, according to the laws of rigid body dynamics.\bigskip

Let's consider the following example of a 2R planar robot, with the joint axis orthogonal with respect to the direction of gravity.
\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth ]{images/2R_example.eps}
\end{center}
where $I_1,I_2$ are the moment of inertia of the links around their center of mass, $d_{c_{1}},d_{c_{2}}$ are the distances along the links between the joint and the center of mass, $m_1,m_2$ are the mass of the links, and $l_1,l_2$ the lengths. The initial configuration at $t=0$ is \begin{equation}
    \mathbf q_0 = \begin{pmatrix}
        0\\\frac{\pi}{2}
    \end{pmatrix} \ \ \ \  
    \dq_0 = \begin{pmatrix}
        0\\0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
Consequently, also $\dot {\mathbf p}=\mathbf 0$ (the end effector velocity). We want to know, what will be $\ddot {\mathbf q}$ (and consequently $\ddot {\mathbf p}$) in the initial configuration at $t=0$, by considering the dynamics and the forces. The Jacobian matrix of this simple manipulator is\begin{equation}
    J=\begin{pmatrix}
        -l_1s_1-l_2s_{12} & -l_2s_{12}\\ 
        l_1c_1+l_2c_{12} & l_2c_{12}
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
in the initial configuration $\mathbf q(0)=\mathbf q_0$ we have\begin{equation}
    J(\mathbf q_0)=\begin{pmatrix}
        -l_2&-l_2\\ 
        l_1&0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
from the differential relation\begin{equation}
    \ddot{\mathbf p}=J\ddot{\mathbf q}+\dot J\dq
\end{equation}
since $\dq_0=\mathbf 0$
\begin{equation}
    \ddot{\mathbf p_0}=\begin{pmatrix}
        -l_2&-l_2\\ 
        l_1&0
    \end{pmatrix}\ddot{\mathbf q_0}
\end{equation}
we know that, by finding the initial joint acceleration $\ddot{\mathbf q_0}$ we can predict the next configuration and where the end effector will move. The full dynamic model is a system of differential equation and is the following\begin{equation}
    M(\mathbf q)\ddot{\mathbf q}+\mathbf c(\mathbf q,\dq)+\mathbf g(\mathbf q)=\mathbf 0
\end{equation} 
where $M$ is the  $2\times 2$ inertia matrix, and depends only on the current configuration, $\mathbf c$ is a term representing the centrifugal and coriolis forces, and depends also on the velocities $\dq$. The last term $\mathbf g$ is the gravity term, and depends on the current configuration. We are not explaining in details how the model is derived, since this is an introductory section, and all these topics will be described and expanded in the following chapters.  

Since the robot is at rest, the $\mathbf c$ vector is null, so\begin{equation}
    \ddot{\mathbf q}=M^{-1}(\mathbf q)\mathbf g(\mathbf q)
\end{equation}
the inertia matrix in the initial configuration (that for now we get for granted) is\begin{equation}
    M(\mathbf q_0)=\begin{pmatrix}
        I_1+m_1d_{c_1}^2+I_2+m_2d_{c_2}^2+m_2l_1^2 &I_2+m_2d_{c_2}^2\\ 
        I_2+m_2d_{c_2}^2&I_2+m_2d_{c_2}^2
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
\begin{boxtheorem}{Steiner}{}
    If $I$ is the moment of inertia around an axis of a rigid body, and $d$ is the distance between this axis and the center of mass, the inertia around the parallel axis passing through the center of mass will be $\bar I = I+md^2$, where $m$ is the mass of the body.
\end{boxtheorem}
In the inertia matrix we see the terms $I_i+m_id_{c_i}^2$, that is exactly the moment of inertia around the parallel axis passing through the center of mass, that we denote $\bar I_i$.
\begin{equation}
    M(\mathbf q_0)=\begin{pmatrix}
        \bar I_1+\bar I_2+m_2l_1^2 &\bar I_2\\ 
       \bar I_2&\bar I_2
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
This inertia matrix is \textit{always} positive definite, so it is invertible, in particular\begin{align}
    &M^{-1}(\mathbf q_0)=\frac{1}{\det M(\mathbf q_0)}\begin{pmatrix}
        \bar I_2&-\bar I_2\\ 
       -\bar I_2&\bar I_1+\bar I_2+m_2l_1^2 
    \end{pmatrix}=\\ &\frac{1}{(\bar I_1+m_2l_1^2)\bar I_2}\begin{pmatrix}
        \bar I_2&-\bar I_2\\ 
       -\bar I_2&\bar I_1+\bar I_2+m_2l_1^2 
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{align}
the gravity term is the following\begin{equation}
    \mathbf g(\mathbf q_0)=\begin{pmatrix}
        m_1d_{c_1}g_0+m_2l_1g_0\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        (m_1d_{c_1}+m_2l_1)g_0\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
the first component $ m_1d_{c_1}g_0+m_2l_1g_0$ represents the total gravitational torque acting on the first joint.\begin{itemize}
    \item $m_1 d_{c_1} g_0$: This is the torque generated by the weight of the first link.
    \item $m_2 l_1 g_0$: This is the torque generated by the weight of the second link acting at the end of the first link.
\end{itemize}
due to the vertical alignment of the initial configuration, the gravity does not exerts any torque on the second joint. By putting together the equations we get:\begin{equation}
    \ddot{\mathbf q_0}=\frac{(m_1d_{c_1}+m_2l_1)g_0}{(\bar I_1+m_2l_1^2)\bar I_2}\begin{pmatrix}
        -\bar I_2\\ \bar I_2
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        \star<0\\ \star > 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
so the first joint will rotate clockwise while the second counter-clockwise. We want to know if the end effector will fall to the left or to the right. We compute \begin{align}
    &\ddot{\mathbf p_0}=\begin{pmatrix}
        -l_2&-l_2\\ 
        l_1&0
    \end{pmatrix}\ddot{\mathbf q_0}=\\ 
    &\ddot{\mathbf p_0}=\begin{pmatrix}
        -l_2&-l_2\\ 
        l_1&0
    \end{pmatrix}\frac{(m_1d_{c_1}+m_2l_1)g_0}{(\bar I_1+m_2l_1^2)\bar I_2}\begin{pmatrix}
        -\bar I_2\\ \bar I_2
    \end{pmatrix}=\\ 
    &\ddot{\mathbf{p}}_0 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ -\frac{l_1 (m_1 d_{c_1} + m_2 l_1)g_0}{\bar{I}_1 + m_2 l_1^2} \end{pmatrix}
\end{align}
so the end effector will accelerate downward without moving on the $x$ axis.
\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.27\textwidth ]{images/2R_example2.eps}
\end{center}
Now, we consider a simpler example from a dynamic point of view, but we will show the process to derive the dynamic model. We consider a planar 2P robot, where the joints are orthogonal, in this simple case the Jacobian matrix is the identity. For this manipulator there will be no angular terms, since all the joints are prismatic, we still refers with torques to the linear forces $\tau_1,\tau_2$ applied on the prismatic joints.\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.35\textwidth ]{images/2P_example.eps}
\end{center}
We consider two different methods to derive the dynamic model (both these methods we will further explained in details in the their relative sections).
\subsubsection{Newton-Euler}
This methods perform a balance of forces acting on the system of rigid bodies. We start by the last link, backtracking towards the first. The forces acting on the second link are the torque $\tau_2$ (the commands) and the gravity, so the dynamic equation for the second link (according to the Newton laws) is:\begin{equation}
    \tau_2-m_2g_0=m_2\ddot q_2
\end{equation}
on the first link, there is no force exerts from the gravity (since this link is constrained to move only along the $x$ axis), so is affected only by the command torque $\tau_1$ and the inertia of the second link (which opposes its motion):\begin{equation}
    \tau_1-m_2\ddot q_1=m_1\ddot q_1
\end{equation}
by putting the two equations together we obtain the following system\begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        m_1m_2&0\\ 0&m_2
    \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
        \ddot q_1\\ \ddot q_2
    \end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}
        0\\ m_2g_0
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        \tau_1\\\tau_2
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
the first matrix $M$ is the inertia matrix while the vector $\begin{pmatrix}
        0\\ m_2g_0
    \end{pmatrix}$ is the gravity term.
\subsubsection{Euler-Lagrange}
This methods is based on the energy of the system. The kinetic energy $T$ is given by the sum of the kinetic energies of the two links $T_1+T_2$, the same applies for the potential energy $U=U_1+U_2$ (that in this case, is given by the gravity field). The kinetic energy of the first link (that can move only along the $x$ axis) is\begin{equation}
    T_1=\frac{1}{2}m_1\dot q_1^2 
\end{equation}
since the second link can move in both directions, and their velocity is given by $\mathbf v_{c_2}=\begin{pmatrix}
    \dot q_1\\ \dot q_2
\end{pmatrix}$, the kinetic energy is\begin{equation}
    T_2=\frac{1}{2}m_2\|\mathbf v_{c_2}\|^2=\frac{1}{2}m_2(\dot q_1^2+ \dot q_2^2)
\end{equation}
the potential energy of the first link is constant, since it can't move along the $y$ axis, we denote this constant energy just $U_1$. The potential energy of the second link, given by the gravity field, is $U_2=m_2g_0q_2$.\bigskip 

The \textit{Lagrangian} is a function describing the dynamics of the system (will be explained in the next chapters), is given by the difference between the kinetic and the potential energy\begin{equation}
    \mathcal L=T-U
\end{equation}
depends on the current configuration $\mathbf q$ and the velocity $\dq$. In this case we have\begin{equation}
    \mathcal L(\mathbf q,\dq)=\frac{1}{2}m_1\dot q_1^2 +\frac{1}{2}m_2(\dot q_1^2+ \dot q_2^2)-U_1-m_2g_0q_2
\end{equation}
there is a simple principle in Physics, the trajectories of the system (the evolution over time of $\mathbf q$ and $\dq$) are the one that \textit{minimizes the action over time}, that is the following scalar function\begin{equation}
    \mathcal S=\int_0^t \mathcal L(\mathbf q,\dq)dt
\end{equation}
this is a problem of Calculus of Variations (the minimization of a functional over a set of functions) and is proved to be solved by the trajectories that satisfies the \textbf{Euler-Lagrange Equations}:\begin{equation}
    \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot q_i}-\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial q_i}=\tau_i, \ \ \ 1\le i\le n
\end{equation}
for our 2P robotic system, we have the two following equations\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        (m_1+m_2)\ddot q_1=\tau_1\\ 
        m_2\ddot q_2+m_2g_0=\tau_2
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
That fully describes the model.
\section{Planning vs Feedback Control}
We review some concepts already explored in the course of Robotics 1 about the differences between planning and feedback control. We recall that a good controller in every robotics application, should incorporate the offline planning procedure with an online closed loop system.\bigskip

We take as an example a simple body with mass $m$ that lies on a plane with zero friction. This body can move only along the direction parallel to the plane, and it's position is defined by the variable $x$, and it is applied an horizontal force $F$, the equation that describes the dynamics of the object is of course $F=m\ddot x$.

We want to plan a motion, starting from the initial condition \begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        x\\\dot x
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        0\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
and reaching the final condition  \begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        x\\\dot x
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        x_d\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
We can plan the motion by integrating over time $\ddot x = \frac{F}{m}=u$. We already seen the simple bang-bang profile, in that case, the displacement in position reached in $T$ seconds by applying a command input of amplitude $u$ is \begin{equation}
    x=\frac{T^2}{4}u
\end{equation}
if $u$ is limited by $F_{max}$ the minimum time to reach $x=x_d$ starting from $x=0$ is\begin{equation}
    T=\sqrt{\frac{4 m x_d}{F_{max}}}
\end{equation}
where $F_{max}=mu_{max}$.
\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth ]{images/bang_bang.eps}
\end{center}
This approach execute the desired motion in nominal condition in the minimum time, but it requires some information about the dynamical model (in this case, the value of $m$). Of course, in real situations, there are always some disturbance forces that we can't control or predict, so this approach will eventually lead to a configuration that is not exactly the desired one, in some cases, with a high error.

The feedback approach consists in using the state variables in the commands, it doesn't necessary requires the dynamic model, but some sensors to measure the position, velocity, and other quantities. In this simple example, the general approach is the following\begin{equation}
    m\ddot x = u(x,\dot x)
\end{equation}
where $u$ is the control rule. We consider the following PD law:\begin{equation}
    m\ddot x=k_P(x_d-x)-k_D \dot x
\end{equation}
the system is still linear. We track the error and it's derivatives\begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        e=x_d-x\\ 
        \dot e = -\dot x\\ 
        \ddot e = -\ddot x
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
We express the dynamics in term of the errors and we get the second order equation:\begin{equation}
    m\ddot e+k_Pe+k_D\dot e = 0
\end{equation}
this homogeneous equation can be solved analytically by knowing $m,k_P,k_D$ and the initial condition. The value of $e(t)$ converges to zero if $m,k_D$ and $k_P$ have the same sign, since $m$ is the mass (and is always positive), it is sufficient to choose positive values for $k_P,k_D$. This law works even if there are some disturbance, we can combine the feed forward action with the feedback loop by choosing a force command\begin{equation}
    F=F_f+k_P(x_f-x)+k_D \dot (\dot x_f-\dot x)
\end{equation}
where $F_f(t)$ realizes the desired trajectory in nominal condition and $x_f(t)$ is the evolution over time of $x$ in the nominal condition.\bigskip

This simple PD control law doesn't work in situations where the system is subject to undamped persistent forces. Let's consider a body of mass $m$ subject to the gravity, that can move only along the axis orthogonal to the ground. We call $y$ the position of the body, and we can apply a force $F$ as the command input. The dynamic model is described by the equation\begin{equation}
    m\ddot y = F-mg_0.
\end{equation}
If we choose a PD control law for $F$ the dynamic equation is \begin{equation}
    m\ddot y = k_P(y_d-y)-k_D\dot y-mg_0.
\end{equation}
where $y_d$ is the target position and 0 is the target velocity. The error is $e=y_d-y$, the temporal evolution of the error is described by the equation\begin{equation}
     k_Pe+k_D\dot e+m\ddot e -mg_0=0
\end{equation}
Will $e(t)$ converge to zero? Let's decide some values for the data just to give an example, let $m=k_P=k_D=1$.
\begin{equation}
   e+\dot e+\ddot e -9.8=0
\end{equation}
if the initial conditions are $e(0)=y_d, \ \dot e(0)=0$ the solution is 
\begin{equation}
    e(t) = \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}t\right) \left[ (y_d - 9.8) \cos\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}t\right) + \frac{y_d - 9.8}{\sqrt{3}} \sin\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}t\right) \right] + 9.8
\end{equation}
since $\lim_{t\rightarrow \infty}\exp(-\frac{1}{2}t)=0$ we have \begin{equation}
    \lim_{t\rightarrow \infty} e(t)=9.8\ne 0 
\end{equation}
so the desired state will be never reached. It is sufficient to add an integral term in the control law by making it a PID controller:\begin{equation}
    k_I\int ed\tau
\end{equation}
the final dynamic equation with the inclusion of this term is \begin{equation}
    k_Pe+k_D\dot e+k_I\int_0^t ed\tau+m\ddot e -mg_0=0
\end{equation}

     \caption{Discrete time simulation with $m=1,y_d=1,k_P=k_D=2,k_I=1, \Delta t = 0.0001$.}

\chapter{Advanced Topics on Kinematics}
\section{Calibration}
A measurement system has a high repeatability if different measurements of the same quantity provides similar results, and is accurate if the measured quantity is near to the ground truth. A robot can be seen as a measurement system, the DH parameters are used in the direct kinematics to compute the task vector from a given configuration. If the predicted position of the end effector is far from the real position, the parameters are wrong, there is a lack in accuracy and the \textbf{calibration procedure} is needed. 

For simplicity, we consider a manipulator with $n$ (all revolut) joints. The  DH parameters are\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\alpha }= \begin{pmatrix}
        \alpha_1\\\vdots \\\alpha_n
    \end{pmatrix}, \ \ 
    \mathbf a=\begin{pmatrix}
        a_1\\\vdots \\a_n
    \end{pmatrix}, \ \ 
    \mathbf d=\begin{pmatrix}
        d_1\\\vdots \\d_n
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
from these parameters we can compute the \textit{nominal} direct kinematics\begin{equation}
    \mathbf y_{e,nom}=k_e(\boldsymbol\alpha,\mathbf a,\mathbf d,\btheta)
\end{equation}
$\btheta$ is the set of joint angles measured by the encoders. The calibration procedure is essential to have a perfect direct kinematics, especially in the industrial context. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item due to the tolerances in the mechanical construction and in assembly of the links and the joints, there is a small error in the actual end effector pose respect to the one given by the direct kinematics.
    \item the errors distributed along the arm are amplified,  due to
the open kinematic chain structure of most robots
    \item the goal of the calibration procedure is to recover as much as possible this end effector position error by adjusting the DH parameters.
\end{itemize}
The calibration procedure needs a sampling operations, where the robot is set in different configuration, when an external device measure the real end effector pose, in such way we can compare this ground truth with the pose predicted by the direct kinematics.

Let $\mathbf y_e$ to be the real end effector pose measured by the external system, and $\mathbf y_{e,nom}$ the one given by the direct kinematics, we consider the error\begin{equation}
    \Delta \mathbf y=\mathbf y_e-\mathbf y_{e,nom}
\end{equation}
from the first order Taylor expansion of the direct kinematics, we can write the following linear relation
\begin{equation}
    \Delta \mathbf y=
    \frac{\partial k_e}{\partial\boldsymbol \alpha}\Delta\boldsymbol \alpha+
     \frac{\partial k_e}{\partial\mathbf a}\Delta\mathbf a+
     \frac{\partial k_e}{\partial\mathbf d}\Delta\mathbf d+
     \frac{\partial k_e}{\partial \btheta}\Delta\btheta
\end{equation}
The small vectors $\Delta\boldsymbol \alpha,\Delta\mathbf a,\Delta\mathbf d,\Delta\btheta$ are the small errors in the DH parameters. The relation can be rewritten in matrix form\begin{equation}
    \Delta\mathbf y_e=\Phi\Delta\boldsymbol\varphi
\end{equation}
where\begin{align}
    &\Phi=\begin{pmatrix}\displaystyle
         \frac{\partial k_e}{\partial\boldsymbol \alpha}&
         \displaystyle\frac{\partial k_e}{\partial\mathbf a}&
     \displaystyle\frac{\partial k_e}{\partial\mathbf d}&
     \displaystyle\frac{\partial k_e}{\partial \btheta}
    \end{pmatrix}\in Mat(6\times 4n)\\
    &\Delta\boldsymbol\varphi=\begin{pmatrix}
        \Delta\boldsymbol \alpha\\ 
        \Delta\mathbf a\\ 
        \Delta\mathbf d\\
        \Delta\btheta
    \end{pmatrix}\in \R^{4n}
\end{align}
notice how $ \Delta \mathbf y$ is given by the measurement, $\Phi$ is evaluated with the nominal parameters and $\Delta\boldsymbol\varphi$ is the unknown error in the DH parameters. 

For example, with a 2R planar robot, where the direct kinematics is \begin{equation}
    k(\mathbf a,\btheta)=\begin{pmatrix}
        a_1\cos\theta_1+a_2\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2)\\ 
        a_1\sin\theta_1+a_2\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2)
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
if the we assume that the parameters $\mathbf \alpha,\mathbf d$ are correct, the $\Phi$ matrix to consider is\begin{equation}
    \Phi=\begin{pmatrix}
        \displaystyle\frac{\partial k}{\partial \mathbf a}&   \displaystyle\frac{\partial k}{\partial \btheta}
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        c_1&c_{12}&-a_1s_2-a_2s_{12}&-a_2s_{12}\\ 
        s_1&s_{12}&a_1c_1+a_2c_{12}&a_2c_{12}
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
Instead of considering one simple point to measure the error, one can sample $l>>n$ points, to get $l$ matrices $\Phi_i$ and $l$ cartesian errors $\Delta\mathbf y_{e,i}$. In this way we can construct the matrix\begin{equation}
    \overline \Phi = \begin{pmatrix}
        \Phi_1 \\ \vdots \\ \Phi_l
    \end{pmatrix}\in Mat(6l\times 4n)
\end{equation}
and the vector \begin{equation}
        \overline{\Delta\mathbf y}_e=\begin{pmatrix}
            \Delta\mathbf y_{e,1} \\ \vdots \\ 
            \Delta\mathbf y_{e,l}
        \end{pmatrix}\in \R^{6l}
\end{equation}
the system of equation to solve in order to find the errors in the DH parameters is \begin{equation}\label{CalSys}
    \overline \Phi \Delta\boldsymbol\varphi= \overline{\Delta \mathbf y}_e 
\end{equation}
$\overline\Phi$ is called \textbf{regressor matrix}. The system in \eqref{CalSys} is an overdetermined system of equations, and can be solved by considering the pseudo inverse of $\overline\Phi$\begin{align}
    &\Delta\boldsymbol{\varphi}=\overline\Phi^{\#}\overline{\Delta \mathbf y}_e \\
    & \overline\Phi^{\#}=(\Phi^T\Phi)^{-1}\Phi^T
\end{align}
Instead of finding the pseudo inverse, an iterative algorithm can be applied to find $\Delta\boldsymbol\varphi$ that considers sequentially the measurements.\bigskip

\section{Kinematic Redundancy}
The direct kinematics $\mathbf y=k(\mathbf q)$ is the map between the task space and the configuration space\begin{equation}
     k:\mathcal C\rightarrow\mathcal T
\end{equation}
we usually denote $n=\dim\mathcal C$ the dimension of the configuration space and $m=\dim\mathcal T$ the dimension of the task space. A robot is \textbf{kinematically redundant} if $n>m$ (there are more degrees of freedom than strictly needed for executing the task), and $n-m$ is the \textbf{redundancy degree}. 
The redundancy of a robot is a relative concept, doesn't depend on the number of joints, but on the number of dimension of the task space, a 6R robot is not redundant for the task of positioning and orientating the end effector, but is redundant for the task of just positioning.

A redundant robot has an infinite number of solution for the inverse kinematics in the regular case, the redundancy can be used in various ways, for example\begin{itemize}
    \item avoid collision with obstacles in the cartesian space 
    \item avoid kinematic singularities in the joint space 
    \item increase manipulability in specified directions
    \item uniformly distribute/limit joint velocities and/or accelerations
    \item optimize energy consumption or needed motion torques
    \item \dots
\end{itemize}\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth ]{images/redundancy.eps}
\end{center}
We define a \textbf{self motion} of a redundant robot, a motion in the joint space (arm reconfiguration) that doesn't change the task vector $\mathbf y$. In a given configuration, one may perform a self motion to get to a configuration that improve/optimize in
some way the behavior of the robotic system.

The solution to the inverse kinematics to perform a self motion are mainly sought at differential level, by considering the Jacobian matrix, due to the linearity, we recall that the Jacobian is the linear mapping between the tangent spaces of $\mathcal C$ and $\mathcal T$.\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth ]{images/tangent_space2.eps}
\end{center}
In practice, the configuration that are in the self motion on a given position $\mathbf p$ satisfies\begin{equation}
    \mathbf q \in \mathcal C \  : \ k(\mathbf q)=\mathbf p
\end{equation}
the following image represents the points on the configuration space of a planar 2R that are mapped on the same task vector.\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth ]{images/selfmotion.png}
\end{center}

Without entering in the details (for now), the \textit{general redundancy resolution} is an optimization problem that can be solved offline (globally) or online (locally). In both methods, the objective function is a general function of the state space $H(\mathbf q,\dq)$, and the goal is to find a trajectory for $\mathbf q(t)$ and $\dq(t)$, thus, redundancy resolution is a calculus of variations problem.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Global methods}: Given a desired trajectory $\mathbf y(t)$ and an initial configuration $\mathbf q(t_0)$, to find an evolution over $[t_0,t_0+T]$, is involved a functional of the type\begin{equation}
     \int_{t_0}^{t_0+T}H(\mathbf q,\dq)dt   
    \end{equation}
    that have to be minimized (or maximized) over $\mathbf q(t), t\in [t_0,t_0+T]$. This problem is usually too difficult to solve. 
    \item \textbf{Local methods}: Usually at each time step $t=kT_s$ ($T_s$ is the sampling time) given a task velocity $\dot{\mathbf y}$ to realize we solve the problem of finding the joint velocity $\dq$ that realizes such $\dot{\mathbf y}$ while optimizing a given function $H(\mathbf q,\dq)$. Then the discrete step of update is \begin{equation}
        \mathbf q((k+1)T_s)=\mathbf q(kT_s)+T_s\dq(kT_s)
    \end{equation}
    this is more easy to solve and usually requires the resolution of a linear quadratic problem.
\end{itemize}
There are three classes of methods to solve $J\dq=\dot{\mathbf y}$:\begin{itemize}
    \item Jacobian-based: Among the infinite solutions, the one that minimizes a suitable norm is chosen 
    \item null-space methods: a term is added to the previous solution so as not to affect execution
of the task trajectory, i.e., belonging to the null-space $\mathcal N(J)$
    \item task augmentation: redundancy is reduced/eliminated by adding $s\le n-m$ further auxiliary tasks to make the problem ''squared''.
\end{itemize}
\subsection{Jacobian Based Methods}
We have to solve the redundant problem from a differential level\begin{equation}
    J\dq=\dot{\mathbf y}
\end{equation}
where $J$ have $m$ rows and $n$ columns, with $n>m$. We will consider solutions of the form\begin{equation}
    \dq = K\dot{\mathbf y}
\end{equation}
where $K$ is a matrix with $n$ rows and $m$ columns. We want $K$ to satisfy the following condition\begin{equation}
    JKJ=J
\end{equation}
such a matrix is called a \textbf{generalized inverse} of $J$ (depends on the current configuration $\mathbf q$), there are infinite matrix that satisfies that condition. A generalized inverse can realize the desired task velocity $\dot{\mathbf y}$ if it is in the range of $J$.\begin{align}
    \forall \dot{\mathbf y}\in\mathcal R(J) \ : \ J(K\dot{\mathbf y})=JKJ\dq=J\dq=\dot{\mathbf y}
\end{align}
An example of a generalized inverse is the \textit{right inverse}, let $J$ to be a Jacobian of the form\begin{equation}
    J=\begin{pmatrix}
        J_a&J_b
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
where $\det J_a\ne0$, then, the right inverse is \begin{equation}
    K_r=\begin{pmatrix}
        J_a^{-1}\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
this is equivalent to ''freezing'' some degrees of freedom. \bigskip 

Another example of a generalized inverse is the \textbf{pseudo inverse matrix}, that is the matrix $J^\#$ that satisfies the four following properties:\begin{align}
    &JJ^\#J=J\\ 
    &J^\#JJ^\#=J^\#\\ 
    &(JJ^\#)^T=JJ^\#\\ 
    &(J^\#J)^T=J^\#J
\end{align}
if the matrix $J$ is full rank, and $n>m$, then the pseudo inverse can be computed in closed form:\begin{equation}
    J^\#=J^T(JJ^T)^{-1}.
\end{equation}
\textbf{Example}: if $$J=\begin{pmatrix}
    1&2&3
\end{pmatrix} $$
the pseudo inverse is 
$$ 
\begin{pmatrix}
    1\\2\\3
\end{pmatrix}\left(
\begin{pmatrix}
    1&2&3
\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
    1\\2\\3
\end{pmatrix}
\right)^{-1}=\frac{1}{14}\begin{pmatrix}
    1\\2\\3
\end{pmatrix}.
$$
\textbf{Example}: Now we consider a square singular matrix. First of all, let $J_\epsilon$ to be the following parametrized matrix
$$J_\epsilon=\begin{pmatrix}
    3&0\\ 0&\epsilon
\end{pmatrix}
$$
we know that the inverse of this matrix is$$
    J_\epsilon^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix}
        \frac{1}{3}&0\\0&\frac{1}{\epsilon}
    \end{pmatrix}
$$
with $\epsilon\rightarrow 0$ (the matrix becomes singular), the right-bottom element of the inverse $\frac{1}{\epsilon}$ goes to infinity. So the matrix 
$$J=\begin{pmatrix}
    3&0\\ 0&0
\end{pmatrix}
$$
isn't invertible and the pseudo inverse just replace the ''infinite-becoming'' term with 0:$$
J^\#=\begin{pmatrix}
        \frac{1}{3}&0\\0&0
    \end{pmatrix}.
$$
\textbf{Example}: Let's consider the following redundant full rank matrix $$J=\begin{pmatrix}
    1&0&2\\ 
    2&1&-1
\end{pmatrix}$$
we consider a generalized inverse, in particular, the right inverse:\begin{align*}
    &J=\begin{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
    1&0\\ 
    2&1
\end{pmatrix}& \begin{pmatrix}
    2\\-1
\end{pmatrix}
\end{pmatrix}\\ 
&K_r=\begin{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
    1&0\\ 
    2&1
\end{pmatrix}^{-1}\\\begin{pmatrix}
    0&0
\end{pmatrix}
\end{pmatrix}\\
&K_r=\begin{pmatrix}
    1&0\\
    -2&1\\
    0&0
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{align*}
We already seen in the Robotics 1 course that the pseudo inverse realizes the desired task velocity by choosing the joint velocities $\dq$ of minimum norm. In particular, given the problem $J\dq=\dot{\mathbf y}$, even if $\dot{\mathbf y}$ is not in the range space of $J$, the pseudo inverse finds the minimum norm solution among the $\dq$ that minimizes\begin{equation}
    \|\dot{\mathbf y}-J\dq\|^2.
\end{equation}
If $J$ is singular and $JJ^T$ cannot be inverted, the formula $J^\#=J^T(JJ^T)^{-1}$ can't be used, and to find $J^\#$ we need numerical methods that relies on the \textit{singular value decomposition} of $J$.

\begin{boxdefinition}{SDV}{}
    For any matrix $J$, there exists a decomposition\begin{equation}
        J=U\Sigma V^T 
    \end{equation}
    where $U$ and $V$ are orthonormal matrices, and $\Sigma$ is a diagonal matrix.
\end{boxdefinition}
This decomposition have special properties, the columns of $U$ are the eigen vectors of $JJ^T$, and the columns of $V$ are the eigen vectors of $J^TJ$. The matrix $\Sigma$ have the following form:\begin{equation}
    \Sigma=\begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_1 & & & \\ 
        & \ddots & & 0_{m\times (n-m)}\\ 
        & & \sigma_m & 
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
it follows that\begin{align}
    &\sigma_1\ge\sigma_2\dots \ge \sigma_\rho>0\\
    &\sigma_{\rho+1}= \sigma_{\rho+2}\dots = \sigma_{m}=0
\end{align}
where $\rho$ is the rank of $J$. The numbers $\sigma_1\dots ,\sigma_\rho$ are the \textbf{singular values} of $J$. The eigen vectors of $JJ^T$ (columns of $U$) are associated to the eigen values $\sigma_1^2\dots,\sigma_\rho^2$. The last $n-\rho$ columns of $V$ are a basis for the null space of $J$.\begin{align}
    &J\mathbf v_i=\sigma_i\mathbf u_i, \ \ \ (i=1\dots, \rho)\\ 
    &J\mathbf  v_i=0, \ \ \ (i=\rho+1\dots n)
\end{align}
we denote $\mathbf u_i$ the $i$-th column vector of $U$ and $\mathbf v_i$ the $i$-th column vector of $V$. 
\begin{boxproposition}{}{}
    The column vectors of $U$ are the eigenvectors of $JJ^T$ associated to the eigen values $\sigma_1^2\dots,\sigma_\rho^2$.
\end{boxproposition}
\textit{Proof}: We want to show that a  vector $\mathbf u_i$ is an eigen vector of $JJ^T$. Since $J=U\Sigma V^T$ we have\begin{equation}
    JJ^T=U\Sigma V^T V \Sigma^T U^T
\end{equation}
since $V$ is orthonormal, we have that $V^T=V^{-1}$ so $V^TV=I$:\begin{align}
    &JJ^T=U\Sigma \blueText{V^T V}\Sigma^T U^T\\
    &JJ^T=U\Sigma \blueText{I}\Sigma^T U^T\\
    &JJ^T=U\Sigma\Sigma^T U^T
\end{align}
by considering the structure of $\Sigma$, we can easily see how $\Sigma\Sigma^T$ will be:\begin{equation}
    \Sigma\Sigma^T=\begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_1 & & & \\ 
        & \ddots & & 0_{m\times (n-m)}\\ 
        & & \sigma_m & 
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_1 & &  \\ 
        & \ddots & \\ 
        & & \sigma_m \\
        &0_{m\times (n-m)}&
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_1^2 & &  \\ 
        & \ddots &   \\ 
        & & \sigma_m^2 
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
so\begin{equation}
JJ^T=U\begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_1^2 & &  \\ 
        & \ddots &   \\ 
        & & \sigma_m^2 
    \end{pmatrix} U^T
\end{equation}
now we consider a vector column of $U$ and we multiply $JJ^T$ by that:\begin{equation}
    JJ^T\mathbf u_i=U\begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_1^2 & &  \\ 
        & \ddots &   \\ 
        & & \sigma_m^2 
    \end{pmatrix} U^T\mathbf u_i
\end{equation}
the result of $U^T\mathbf u_i$ is the column vector where each entry is the dot product between the column vectors of $U$ and $\mathbf u_i$:\begin{equation}
    U^T\mathbf u_i=
        \begin{pmatrix}
            \mathbf u_1^T\\ 
            \mathbf u_2^T\\ 
            \vdots \\ 
            \mathbf u_m^T
        \end{pmatrix}\mathbf u_i=\begin{pmatrix}
            \mathbf u_1^T\mathbf u_i\\ 
            \vdots \\ 
             \mathbf u_i^T\mathbf u_i\\ 
             \vdots \\ 
            \mathbf u_m^T\mathbf u_i\\ 
        \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
since $U$ is orthonormal, the column vectors are orthogonal and have unitary norm so\begin{equation}
    U^T\mathbf u_i=\begin{pmatrix}
        0 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}=\boldsymbol\delta_i
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol\delta_i$ is the column vector with $m$ component, all zeros except for the $i$-th entry that is 1. Now we can go back to  $JJ^T$:\begin{align}
    &JJ^T\mathbf u_i=U\begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_1^2 & &  \\ 
        & \ddots &   \\ 
        & & \sigma_m^2 
    \end{pmatrix} \boldsymbol\delta_i=
    JJ^T\mathbf u_i=U\sigma_i^2\boldsymbol\delta_i\\=\sigma_i^2 U \boldsymbol\delta_i
\end{align}
clearly $U \boldsymbol\delta_i$ is the $i$-th column vector of $U$ so\begin{equation}
    JJ^T\mathbf u_i=\sigma_i^2\mathbf u_i
\end{equation}
\hfill$\blacksquare$\bigskip

\noindent As we already mention, the pseudo inverse (that we can compute with the SVD) is important since it gives the solution that minimizes the norm of the joint velocity $\dq$, this is a good property from an energetic point of view, since we always pursue the same task velocity by imposing less torque (hence, less electrical current).
\begin{boxtheorem}{}{}
    Consider the system $J\dq=\dy$ in the variables $\dq$, where $J$ is a full rank matrix with $n$ columns and $m$ rows ($n>m$). The solution given by $J^\#\dy=J^T(JJ^T)^{-1}\dy$ denoted $\dq_{PS}$ is the minimum norm solution that realizes $\dy$. 
\end{boxtheorem}
\textit{Proof}: let $\dq^*$ to be any other solution to $J\dq^*=\dy$. Since $J\dq^*=J\dq_{PS}$ it follows:\begin{align}
    &J\dq^*=J\dq_{PS}\implies \\
    &J\dq^*-J\dq_{PS}=\mathbf 0\implies \\
    &J(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})=\mathbf 0.
\end{align}\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth ]{images/PS_solution_1.eps}
\end{center}
Now we show that the vector $\dq^*-\dq_{PS}$ is orthogonal to $\dq_{PS}$:
\begin{align}
    &(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})^T\dq_{PS}=\\ &(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})^TJ^\#\dy=\\
    &\blueText{(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})^TJ^T}(JJ^T)^{-1}\dy=\\
    &\blueText{(J(\dq^*-\dq_{PS}))^T}(JJ^T)^{-1}\dy=\\
    &\blueText{(J\dq^*-J\dq_{PS})^T}(JJ^T)^{-1}\dy=\\
    &\blueText{(\dy-\dy)^T}(JJ^T)^{-1}\dy=\\
     &\blueText{\mathbf 0^T}(JJ^T)^{-1}\dy=0.
\end{align}
\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth ]{images/PS_solution_2.eps}
\end{center}
Now let's compute the norm of $\dq^*$:\begin{equation}
    \|\dq^*\|^2=\|\dq_{PS}+(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})\|^2
\end{equation}
since $\dq^*$ and $(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})$ are orthonormal, the norm of the sum is the sum of the two norms:\begin{equation}
    \|\dq_{PS}+(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})\|^2=\|\dq_{PS}\|^2+\|(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})\|^2\ge\|\dq_{PS}\|^2 
\end{equation}
so it is always grater or equal than the norm of $\dq_{PS}$. The proof is complete.\hfill$\blacksquare$\bigskip

We mentioned how the SVD is useful to compute the pseudo inverse when the matrix is not full rank. This is true due to the following relation:\begin{equation}
    J=U\Sigma V^T\implies J^\#=V\Sigma^\# U^T 
\end{equation}
and $\Sigma^\#$ is easy to compute:\begin{equation}\label{SigmaDiecis}
    \Sigma^\#=\begin{pmatrix}
        \nicefrac{1}{\sigma_1}& & &\\ 
        &\ddots &  & 0_{(m-\rho)\times (m-\rho)}\\ 
        & &  \nicefrac{1}{\sigma_\rho} &\\ 
         & &  0_{(n-m)\times m} &
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
so to compute $J^\#$:\begin{enumerate}
    \item We use numerical methods to compute the singular value decomposition of $J$ by finding $U,V,\Sigma$
    \item we compute $\Sigma^\#$ by using the form in equation \eqref{SigmaDiecis}
    \item we compute $J^\#=V\Sigma^\# U^T$.
\end{enumerate}

There is another way to solve the problem $J\dq=\dy$ by finding a solution that realizes $\dy$ but not minimize the norm of $\dq$, but a weighted norm given by a positive definite matrix $W$.
\begin{boxdefinition}{Weighted Pseudo Inverse}{}
    Given the problem $J\dq=\dy$, and a positive definite matrix $W$, the solution \begin{equation}
        \dq_W=J^\#_W\dy
    \end{equation}
    where\begin{equation}
        J^\#_W=W^{-1}J^T(JW^{-1}J^T)^{-1}
    \end{equation}
    minimizes the weighted norm\begin{equation}
        \|\dq\|^2_W=\dq^TW\dq.
    \end{equation}
\end{boxdefinition}
This kind of solution is used to give more emphasis to some joints, for example, if $W$ is diagonal\begin{equation}
    W=\begin{pmatrix}
        W_1&\dots & 0\\ 
        \vdots &\ddots&\vdots \\ 
        0&\dots & W_n
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
a large weight $W_i$ will impose to find a solution where $\dot q_i$ is small. This can be used to find a solution that uses more joint velocity for the last joints that are near the end effector, since they have to support a smaller weight (and not that of the entire structure) and therefore less torque is required to move them compared to the joints at the base of the structure.\bigskip

There is a last Jacobian based method, called \textbf{Damped Least Squares (DLS)}. It can be proved that, the solution given by the pseudo inverse $J^\#$ is equivalent to the solution $\dq$ of the following optimization problem:\begin{equation}
    \begin{matrix}
        \displaystyle\min_{\dq} \|\dq\|^2\\ \\
        \text{such that }\|\dy-J\dq\|^2=0
    \end{matrix}
\end{equation}
this problem can be \textit{relaxed}: We might add a weighting term $\mu\ge 0$ that creates a compromise between large joint velocity and task accuracy: In practice, we allow a solution that achieves a task velocity that is not identical to the desired one, but with a lower norm. The optimization problem becomes unconstrained:\begin{equation}
    \displaystyle\min_{\dq} \frac{\mu}{2}\|\dq\|^2+\frac{1}{2}\|\dy-J\dq\|^2
\end{equation}
this induces a robust behavior when crossing singularities and applies equally to square and non-square matrices. Since it is a quadratic form, it admits an analytical solution:\begin{equation}
    \dq=J_{DLS}\dy=J^T(JJ^T+\mu I_m)^{-1}\dy
\end{equation}
the more the factor $\mu$ is big, the more importance will be given to the norm respect to the task error. The task error is\begin{equation}
    \dot{\mathbf e}=\mu(JJ^T+\mu I_m)^{-1}\dy
\end{equation}
of course, $\mu$ can be variable: Since when crossing a singularity the required joint velocity $\dq$ grows, so we can make $\mu$ inversely proportional to the minimum singular value $\sigma_\rho$ of $J$, that can be seen as a measure of distance from a singularity.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth ]{images/DLS}
    \caption{DLS solution near a singularity for a $2\times 2$ Jacobian.}
\end{figure}
We have seen four different Jacobian based methods:\begin{itemize}
    \item pseudo inverse: $\dq=J^\#\dy$
    \item weighted pseudo inverse: $\dq=J_W^\#\dy$
    \item right side inverse: $\dq=\begin{pmatrix}
        J_a^{-1}\\0
    \end{pmatrix}\dy$
    \item DLS: $\dq=J_{DLS}\dy$, this last one is not using a generalized inverse of $J$.
\end{itemize}
These solutions locally tries to avoid singularities, but there is no guarantees that  singularities are globally avoided, despite we are trying to minimize the joint velocity norm. Another issue of these local methods, is that leads to non repeatable motion: A periodic trajectory in the task space is not necessary mapped in the joint space as a periodic evolution of the joint trajectories. This makes harder to predict if a given task movement will leads to a collision of the manipulator with obstacles in the workspace.
\subsection{Null Space Methods}\label{NullSpaceMethods}
This method will exploit joint movements that lies in the null space of the Jacobian matrix. Before entering in the details, let's recall the following theorem.
\begin{boxtheorem}{}{}
    Given a system of linear equations $A\mathbf x=\mathbf b$, any solution (if any) can be written in the form:\begin{equation}
        \mathbf x_p+\mathbf x_h 
    \end{equation}
    where $\mathbf x_p$ is a particular solution, and $\mathbf x_h$ is a solution of the homogeneous system $A\mathbf x=\mathbf 0$.
\end{boxtheorem}
since we are trying to solve $\dy=J\dq$, we will consider a general solution of the following form:\begin{equation}
    \dq = J^\#\dy + (I-J^\#J)\dq_N
\end{equation}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $J^\#\dy$ is a particular solution (we know that is the solution with minimum norm)
    \item $(I-J^\#J)\dq_N$ is a solution to the system $J\dy=\mathbf 0$.
\end{itemize}
Notice how $(I-J^\#J)$ is the \textbf{projection operator} on the null space of $J$. $\dq_N$ is an arbitrary joint velocity vector, that will be projected on the null space, in such way, this term will not influence the resulting task vector $\dy$.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth ]{images/qN.eps}
\end{figure}
\noindent In particular, $(I-J^\#J)$ is the \textit{orthogonal} projection operator, and has the following properties:\begin{itemize}
    \item is symmetric
    \item is idempotent: $(I-J^\#J)^2=(I-J^\#J)$
    \item $(I-J^\#J)^\#=(I-J^\#J)$
    \item $J^\#\dy$ is orthogonal to $(I-J^\#J)\dq_N$.
\end{itemize}
This kind of solution can be generalized by considering any couple of generalized inverse $K_1,K_2$:\begin{equation}
    \dq=K_1\dy+(I-K_2J)\dq_N 
\end{equation}
but will not have all of the four properties just presented. In this way, $\dq_N$ can be chosen arbitrarily as a ''preferred'' direction, and the null space projection of this desired velocity will be accomplished. Before entering in the details of how to choose this $\dq_N$, we will give a general presentation (not only related to Robotics) of an optimization problem.
\begin{boxdefinition}{Linear Quadratic Problem}{def:LQ}
    A \textbf{LQ problem} is the following optimization problem\begin{align}
        &\min_{\mathbf x\in \R^n}H(\mathbf x)=\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf x-\mathbf x_N)^TW(\mathbf x-\mathbf x_N)\\
        &\text{subject to } \ J\mathbf x=\mathbf b 
    \end{align}
    where\begin{itemize}
        \item $W\in Mat(n\times n)$, symmetric
        \item $J\in Mat(m\times n)$
        \item $\mathbf b\in \R^m$
        \item $\mathbf x_N\in \R^n$.
    \end{itemize}
\end{boxdefinition}
In our Robotics context, the matrix $W$ is also positive definite\begin{equation}
    \mathbf x^T W \mathbf x >0, \ \ \forall \mathbf x.
\end{equation}
This condition is sufficient to make the problem to have a minimum solution. A general LQ problem can be solved by adding $m$ additional variables $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ called \textbf{Lagrangian multipliers} and by considering the following function called \textbf{Lagrangian}:\begin{equation}
    \mathcal L(\mathbf x,\boldsymbol{\lambda})=H(\mathbf x)+\boldsymbol{\lambda}^T(J\mathbf x-\mathbf b).
\end{equation}
These Lagrangian multipliers works as a way to ''violate'' the constraints to find a solution. To find a solution, we have to find the critical points of $\mathcal L$, by considering the gradient respect to $\mathbf x$ and $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ and putting it equals to zero.\begin{align}
    &\nabla_{\mathbf x}\mathcal L=\begin{pmatrix}
        \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial x_1}&\dots & \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial x_n}
    \end{pmatrix}=W(\mathbf x-\mathbf x_N)+J^T\boldsymbol{\lambda}=\mathbf 0\\ 
    &\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\lambda}}\mathcal L=\begin{pmatrix}
        \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial \lambda_1}&\dots & \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial \lambda_m}
    \end{pmatrix}=J\mathbf x-\mathbf b=\mathbf 0
\end{align}
The sufficient condition to have a minimum is to have the Hessian matrix respect to $\mathbf x$ to be positive definite, as we already said, since:\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{\mathbf x}^2\mathcal L=W\succ 0
\end{equation}
We can solve the system of equations given by \begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        \nabla_{\mathbf x}\mathcal L=\mathbf 0\\ 
        \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\lambda}}\mathcal L=\mathbf 0
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
the first equation can be solved for $\mathbf x$:\begin{equation}\label{LQ1}
    \mathbf x=\mathbf x_N-W^{-1}J^T\boldsymbol{\lambda}
\end{equation}
by substituting in the second equation we get\begin{equation}
    J(\mathbf x_N-W^{-1}J^T\boldsymbol{\lambda})-\mathbf b=\mathbf 0
\end{equation}
hence\begin{equation}\label{LQ2}
    \boldsymbol{\lambda}=(JW^{-1}J^T)^{-1}(J\mathbf x_N-\mathbf b)
\end{equation}
By combining \eqref{LQ1} and \eqref{LQ2} we can find the general solution for $\mathbf x$:\begin{equation}
    \mathbf x=\mathbf x_N+W^{-1}J^T(JW^{-1}J^T)^{-1}(\mathbf b-J\mathbf x_N).
\end{equation}
here $\mathbf x_n$ acts as a preferred solution, in fact, if it satisfies the constraint $J\mathbf x_n=\mathbf b$, will be the solution. Let's switch back to the Robotics context. The problem to find a solution to $J\dq=\dy$ that minimizes the weighted squared norm $\|\dq\|_W^2$ is exactly an LQ problem.\begin{align*}
    &\min_{\dq} \frac{1}{2}(\dq-\dq_N)W(\dq-\dq_N)\\ 
    &\text{s.t. } J\dq=\dy
\end{align*}
and $\dq_N$ is the preferred solution. We know that the solution is exactly\begin{equation}
   \dq= \dq_N+W^{-1}J^T(JW^{-1}J^T)^{-1}(\dy-J\dq_N)
\end{equation}
notice how $W^{-1}J^T(JW^{-1}J^T)^{-1}$ is the weighted pseudo inverse $J^\#_W$:
\begin{equation}
   \dq= \dq_N+J^\#_W(\dy-J\dq_N)
\end{equation}
can be reorganized as follows
\begin{equation}
   \dq= J^\#_W\dy+(I-J^\#_WJ)\dq_N 
\end{equation}
that is exactly the sum of a particular solution ($J^\#_W\dy$) and the projection (not orthogonal) of $\dq_N$ on the null space of $J$.
\bigskip 

\noindent At this point, we have to define a way to choose (at each time step) the preferred direction $\dq_N$.\begin{quote}
    \sapText{Idea}: We can choose $\dq_N$ as the gradient of some performance metrics in function of the configuration.
\end{quote}
We define a scalar function $G(\mathbf q)$ that measures how desirable a configuration is, the gradient of this function will push the configuration towards the maximization of that function. So we define:\begin{equation}
    \dq_N=\nabla G
\end{equation}
In this way, while executing the desired trajectory $\mathbf y(t)$, we will let the robot to perform a self motion by trying to increase $G(\mathbf q)$. Note how, in the configuration space $\mathcal C$, that is a differentiable manifold, there is a \textbf{self motion manifold}, an embedded subspace of the configurations that are mapped in the same task vector $\mathbf y$. This manifold is given by all the configurations $\mathbf q$ that satisfies $k(\mathbf q)=\mathbf y$, where $ k$ is the direct kinematics.\bigskip

We know that $\mathcal C$ is a $n$-manifold, and $\mathcal T$ (the task space) is a $m$-manifold. When $n>m$ the dimension of the self motion manifold embedded in $\mathcal C$ is of course $n-m$.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth ]{images/nablaG.eps}
    \caption{The vector $\nabla G$ is pointing where $G$ is greater than the actual configuration value $G(\mathbf q^*)$. In particular, the preferred direction will be the projection of the gradient on the null space of $J$.}
\end{figure}
\begin{boxdefinition}{Projected Gradient}{}
    The \textbf{projected gradient} method is the procedure to use the gradient of the performance metric $G$ as the preferred direction $\dq_N$.
\end{boxdefinition}
Now let's see some example of metrics. One important choice, is to consider the \textit{distance from singularities} defined as follows\begin{equation}
    G_{man}(\mathbf q)=\sqrt{\det(J(\mathbf q)J^T(\mathbf q))}
\end{equation}
this measure is related to the volume of the manipulability ellipsoid, so, by trying to maximize it, the robot will go away from singular configurations.\bigskip 

$G_{man}(\mathbf q)$ is quite difficult to compute at each time step, since it require to compute the determinant of $JJ^T$, when dealing with a specific kinematic structure, we can find other metrics that are suitable and less expensive to compute.

For example, consider a 3R planar robot, we know that a singular configuration occurs when $q_2\in \{0,\pi\}$ or $q_3\in \{0,\pi\}$. A good choice of metrics can be\begin{equation}
    G(\mathbf q)=\sin^2q_2+\sin^2q_3
\end{equation}
it is true that $G$ and $G_{man}$ (for the 3R planar robot) shares the zeros: $G(\mathbf q)=0\iff G_{man}(\mathbf q)=0$, but $G$ is easier to compute.

Another useful choice for $G$ comes from the midpoints of the joints ranges, let \begin{align}
    &q_i\in[q_{im},q_{iM}]\\
    &\bar q_i=\frac{q_{im}+q_{iM}}{2}
\end{align}
to be the ranges for each joint $i$. The following function will be greater when the value of each joint is near to their mid point $\bar q_i$:\begin{equation}
    G_{mid}(\mathbf q)=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(\frac{q_i-\bar q_i}{q_{iM}-q_{im}}\right)^2
\end{equation}
These choice of $G$ are all differentiable, so it's always possible to compute the gradient $\nabla G$. The following function is not differentiable.\begin{boxdefinition}{Clearance}{}
    The \textbf{clearance} is the minimum distance between a point of the robot and an obstacles in the workspace.
\end{boxdefinition}
Let $robot\subset\R^3$ to be the set of all points on the surface of the robot structure, and let $obstacles\subset\R^3$ to be the set of points on the surface of all the obstacles in the workspace. Clearly, the points in $robot$ change with the configuration $\mathbf q$ according to the direct kinematics.

The clearance function is defined as follows:\begin{equation}
    H_{cle}(\mathbf q)=\min_{\begin{matrix}
    \mathbf p\in robot\\ \mathbf p' \in obstacles
    \end{matrix}}\|\mathbf p(\mathbf q)-\mathbf p'\|^h
\end{equation}
where $h$ is usually 1 or 2. Of course, this function is not practically computable if we consider the real shapes of the objects, that are composed by an infinite and continuous number of points displaced in a non formally describable way. To exploit this, usually the robot structure and the obstacles are modeled with simple shapes like cubes,parallelepiped or spheres, which surround the true shapes of the objects, so as to be conservative in terms of coverage (if such shapes do not collide, then the true objects do not collide either). \begin{boxproposition}{Decidability of the Distance between Two Polytopes}{}
    For any couple of convex shapes (polytopes) in the three dimensional space, it is possible to find the minimum distance between them.
\end{boxproposition}
Given these results, if we model the robotic structure and the obstacles as convex shapes, we can always compute $H_{cle}(\mathbf q)$ in any configuration.

\end{figure}
So if $H_{cle}$ is chosen as $G$, then the robot will try to move towards a configuration that maximizes the distance from the obstacles. The problem is that $H_{cle}$ is not differentiable, since the nearest object to the robot can change during the motion. For example, let's consider a toy example of a robot with one revolut joint, and two obstacles in the workspace.

These jumps can cause vibrations in the control if not managed with smoothing techniques or potential functions (such as the barrier functions).\bigskip

At the end, after choosing an appropriate performance metric $G$ to maximize, the control input for the velocity is\begin{equation}
    \dq=J^\#\dy+(I-J^\#J)\nabla G 
\end{equation}
this equation can be reorganized to avoid the computation of the projector operator:\begin{equation}
    \dq=\nabla G+J^\#(\dy-J\nabla G)
\end{equation}
but the computation of the pseudo inverse $J^\#$ is still required, and is intensive since it requires numerical methods for the SVD. In principle, since $m$ of the $n$ configuration variables are used to accomplish the desired task trajectory, the additional complexity of the redundancy resolution method should depend only on the remaining $n-m$ (redundancy degree) variables.\bigskip

There is another method than relies on a different constrained optimization problems that should be more efficient than the projected gradient in situations where the Jacobian has full rank.
\begin{boxproposition}{Decomposition of joint space}{}
    If $\text{rank} J=m$ there exists a \textbf{decomposition} of the joint variables (possibly after a reordering) \begin{equation}
        \mathbf q=\begin{pmatrix}
            \mathbf q_a \\
            \mathbf q_b
        \end{pmatrix}
        \begin{array}{l}
            \in \R^m \\
            \in \R^{n-m}
        \end{array}
    \end{equation}
    such that the matrix \begin{equation}
        J_a=\left(\frac{\partial  k}{\partial \mathbf q_a}\right)
    \end{equation}
    is non singular.
\end{boxproposition}
from the \textit{implicit function theorem (Dini)}, given the direct kinematics\begin{equation}
    k(\mathbf q_a,\mathbf q_b)=\mathbf y
\end{equation}
we can rewrite $\mathbf q_a$ as a function of $\mathbf y$ and $\mathbf q_b$\begin{equation}
    \mathbf q_a=g(\mathbf y,\mathbf q_b)
\end{equation}
in general, is hard to find an explicit expression for $g$, but this is not needed: we consider the partial derivative of $g$ respect to $\mathbf q_b$:\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{\mathbf q_b}g=\left(\frac{\partial  g}{\partial \mathbf q_b}\right)
\end{equation}
this is equals to\begin{equation}
    \left(\frac{\partial  g}{\partial \mathbf q_b}\right)=-\left(\frac{\partial  k}{\partial \mathbf q_a}\right)^{-1}\left(\frac{\partial  k}{\partial \mathbf q_b}\right)=-J_a^{-1}J_b\in Mat(m\times n-m).
\end{equation}
The $n-m$ variables $\mathbf q_b$ can be selected independently to optimize some performance metrics while the remaining $m$ variables $\mathbf q_a$ are used to correctly execute the task $\mathbf q_a=g(\mathbf y,\mathbf q_b)$, at the velocity level by controlling $\dq$.\bigskip 

The \textbf{Reduced Gradient} method aims to perform a step in the gradient direction just for the reduced $n-m$ variables $\dq_b$, then, the remaining velocity for $\dq_a$ is computed in function of the just found $\dq_b$ and the desired task velocity $\dy$.
\begin{equation}
    G(\mathbf q)=G(\mathbf q_a,\mathbf q_b)
\end{equation}
$\mathbf q_a$ is expressed in term of $\mathbf q_b$ and the desired $\mathbf y$ so $G$ can be written as a function of $\mathbf q_b$ only:\begin{equation}
    G(\mathbf q)=G(\mathbf q_a,\mathbf q_b)=G(g(\mathbf y,\mathbf q_b),\mathbf q_b)=G'(\mathbf q_b)
\end{equation}
we don't have to compute $g$ since at differential level we can compute $\nabla_{\mathbf q_b}G'$\begin{align}
    &\nabla_{\mathbf q_b}G'=
    \left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial \mathbf q_a}\right)
    \left(\frac{\partial g}{\partial \mathbf q_b}\right)+
    \left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial \mathbf q_b}\right)=\\ 
    &\left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial \mathbf q_a}\right)(-J_a^{-1}J_b)+
    \left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial \mathbf q_b}\right)=\\ 
    &\begin{pmatrix}\displaystyle
        \frac{\partial G}{\partial \mathbf q_a}&\displaystyle\frac{\partial G}{\partial \mathbf q_b}
    \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
        -J_a^{-1}J_b\\ I
    \end{pmatrix}=\\ 
    &\Big(
        -(J_a^{-1}J_b)^T \ \ I_{n-m}
   \Big)\nabla_{\mathbf q}G
\end{align}
At each time step, the algorithm to compute $\dq$ is the following\begin{enumerate}
    \item compute the velocities for $\mathbf q_b$ to maximize the metric $G$: $\dq_b=\nabla_{\mathbf q_b}G'$
    \item we have to solve the equation $J_a\dq_a+J_b\dq_b=\dy$ for $\dq_a$ to satisfy the $m$ task constraints 
    \item $\dq_a=J_a^{-1}(\dy-J_b\dq_b)$.
\end{enumerate}
This method is analytically simpler respect to the projected gradient since doesn't require to calculate the pseudo inverse $J^\#$, but requires to search a non-singular minor $J_a$.
\subsection{Task Augmentation}
Instead of defining a performance index $G$ and use it's gradient, in \textit{task augmentation} we add $s\le n-m$ additional task formulated as equalities, let's denote $k_1$ the direct kinematics:\begin{equation}
    k_1(\mathbf q)=\mathbf y_1\implies \begin{cases}
        k_{11}(\mathbf q)=y_{11}\\
        k_{12}(\mathbf q)=y_{12}\\ \vdots \\ 
        k_{1m}(\mathbf q)=y_{1m}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
by adding $s\le n-m$ task we consider additional equality constraints denoted $k_2$:
\begin{equation}
    k_2(\mathbf q)=\mathbf y_2\implies \begin{cases}
        k_{21}(\mathbf q)=y_{21}\\
        k_{22}(\mathbf q)=y_{22}\\ \vdots \\ 
        k_{2s}(\mathbf q)=y_{2s}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
up to a total of $s+m\le n$ constraints. The \textit{augmented} task becomes:\begin{equation}
    \mathbf y_A=\begin{pmatrix}
        k_1(\mathbf q)\\ k_2(\mathbf q)
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        \mathbf y_1\\ \mathbf y_2
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The problem is still considered at the differential level:\begin{equation}
    \mathbf y_A=\begin{pmatrix}
        J_1\\ 
        J_2
    \end{pmatrix}\dq = J_A\dq 
\end{equation}
$J_1$ is the Jacobian of the direct kinematics, $J_2$ the Jacobian of the additional tasks: $J_2=\left(\displaystyle\frac{\partial k_2}{\partial \mathbf q}\right)$. The matrix $J_A$ is called \textbf{augmented Jacobian}, and have $m+s$ rows and $n$ columns. As we said, the constraints $k_2(\mathbf q)=\mathbf y_2$ can be chosen to satisfy some desirable features for the solution. A solution for the problem can still be found in the form of the generalized inverse:\begin{equation}
    \dq = K_A\dy_A.
\end{equation}

Let's consider the following example with a 4R robot following a trajectory on a plane.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth ]{images/example_augmentedTask.eps}
\end{figure}
We use a set of variable $\mathbf q$ where $q_i$ is the angle respect to the horizontal axis. If we want to keep the last link vertical, we can add the additional constraint\begin{equation}
    k_2(\mathbf q)=q_4=\nicefrac{\pi}{2} \ \ \ \ (s=1)
\end{equation}
in the augmented problem,the degree of redundancy decreases and becomes 1.

The advantage of augmenting the task is that we get a \textit{better shaping} of the inverse kinematic solution.

There are some advantages, it might occur an \textbf{algorithmic singularity}: The rank of the augmented Jacobian is not full, despite the ranks of $J_1$ and $J_2$ are full:\begin{equation}
    \text{rank}J_1=m, \ \ \ \ \text{rank}J_2=s, \ \ \ \ \text{rank}J_A<m+s
\end{equation}
To avoid the linear dependency between the columns the following condition should be preserved:\begin{equation}
    \mathcal R(J_1^T)\cap  \mathcal R(J_2^T)=\emptyset
\end{equation}
but is (in general) difficult to obtain.\bigskip

\noindent   If we add exactly $n-m$ task we \textit{square} the problem, and we can directly use the inverse of $J_A$:\begin{equation}
    \dq = J_A^{-1}\dy_A 
\end{equation}
this scheme is \textbf{repeatable}: If no singularities are encountered during a cyclic trajectory $\dy_A(t)$, then will be executed a profile that is cyclic in the joint space.

The augmented task $k_2(\mathbf q)=0$ can be defined in a way to maximize a performance criteria $G(\mathbf q)$ as seen in Section \ref{NullSpaceMethods}.

In that context, for the reduced gradient the conditions to satisfy in the constraint optimization are\begin{equation}
    [-(J_a^{-1}J_b)^TJ]\nabla_{\mathbf q}G=\mathbf 0
\end{equation}
and are exactly $n-m$ conditions that can be chosen as the extra task $k_2(\mathbf q)$:
\begin{equation}
    k_2(\mathbf q)=[-(J_a^{-1}J_b)^TJ]\nabla_{\mathbf q}G=\mathbf 0.
\end{equation}
Since we are solving the problem at differential level, it would be required to compute $J_2=\left(\displaystyle\frac{\partial k_2}{\partial \mathbf q}\right)$, the calculation is pretty involved, so in that case the gradient might be computed numerically with a finite difference method.
In presence of algorithmic singularities, one can use the DLS method, however, both the original and the augmented task will be affected by errors.\bigskip 

In most cases, one can define a main task to execute, and one or more auxiliary tasks, with lower priority respect the first one (execute perfectly the first task, than the others). The \textbf{task priority} problem consists in assigning a priority value to each tasks, and then execute them in order to satisfy the higher priority tasks before the others. For example, let's consider two tasks\begin{align*}
    &\dy_1=J_1\dq\\ 
    &\dy_2=J_2\dq
\end{align*}
first, we address the first task as follows\begin{equation}\label{q_expression_priority}
    \dq = J_1^\#\dy_1+(I-J_1^\#J_1)\mathbf v_1
\end{equation}
the vector $\mathbf v_1\in \R^n$ can be chosen to satisfy (if possible) the second task, and will be projected on the null space of $J_1$.\begin{align}
    &\dy_2=J_2\dq=\\ 
    &J_2(J_1^\#\dy_1+(I-J_1^\#J_1)\mathbf v_1)=\\ 
    &J_2J_1^\#\dy_1+   J_2(I-J_1^\#J_1)\mathbf v_1=\\
    &J_2J_1^\#\dy_1+J_2P_1\mathbf v_1
\end{align}
We denoted $P_1$ the projection on the null space of $J_1$: $(I-J_1^\#J_1)$. The general solution for $\mathbf v_1$ in order to accomplish $\dy_2$ is:\begin{equation}
    \mathbf v_1=(J_2P_1)^\#(\dy_2-J_2J_1^\#\dy_1)+
    (I-(J_2P_1)^\#(J_2P_1))\mathbf v_2 
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf v_2\in \R^n$ is again an arbitrary vector that might be chosen to accomplish a third task of lower priority. By substituting the expression of $\mathbf v_1$ in \eqref{q_expression_priority} we get:\begin{align}
    &\dq=J_1^\#\dy_1+P_1(J_2P_1)^\#(\dy_2-J_2J_1^\#\dy_1)+P_1(I-(J_2P_1)^\#(J_2P_1))\mathbf v_2= \\
    &J_1^\#\dy_1+(J_2P_1)^\#(\dy_2-J_2J_1^\#\dy_1)+P_1(I-(J_2P_1)^\#(J_2P_1))\mathbf v_2 
\end{align}
With task priority, there will be no algorithmic singularities, the error will be restricted only on the second and lower priorities tasks.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth ]{images/task_priority.png}
\end{figure}
The concept can be generalized with an arbitrary number of tasks, but is useful to have a recursive representation. 
Let $s$ to be the number of tasks, each task is defined by a Jacobian matrix $J_i$ and a task vector $\dy_i$\begin{align}
    &\dy_i\in \R^{m_i}\\ 
    &\text{task }i: \ \dy_i=J_i\dq\\ 
    &i=1,2,\dots s
\end{align}
each task $i$ have $m_i$ components, we denote $m$ the number of total singular task\begin{equation}
    m=\sum_{i=1}^s m_i 
\end{equation}
this can be also greater than $n$ (not all task can be accomplished simultaneously). The task are indexed such that the task $i$ have greater priority than task $j$ if $i>j$, so the task $\dy_1=J_1\dq$ is the one with highest priority.

At each time step, a \textbf{stack of tasks} is defined, at that moment, the solution must be found to accomplish (if possible) the tasks ordered in the stack:\begin{equation}
    \dy_{A,k}=\begin{pmatrix}
        \dy_1\\
        \vdots\\
        \dy_k
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
up to the $k$-th task. In the same way is defined the augmented Jacobian matrix:\begin{equation}
    J_{A,k}=\begin{pmatrix}
        J_1\\
        \vdots\\
        J_k
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
that have $n$ (dof) columns and $m=\sum m_i$ rows.

The projector on the null space of $J_i$ is defined as usual\begin{equation}
    P_i=I-J_i^\#J_i
\end{equation}
The projector on the null space of $ J_{A,k}$ is defined as follows:\begin{equation}
     P_{A,k}=I- J_{A,k}^\# J_{A,k}
\end{equation}
Notice how we are defining the stack of tasks up to the $k$-th in order to define later the recursive algorithm that works for a generic $k$, and will be executed for all the task until the $s$-th one. To satisfy the first task in the stack $\dy_1$, we use the solution $\dq_1$ found through the pseudo inverse:\begin{equation}
    \dq_1=J^\#_1\dy_1
\end{equation}
we know that the pseudo inverse find the minimum norm solution, so $\dq_1$ can be seen as the solution of the following LQ problem
\begin{align}
    &\dq_1=\arg\min_{\dq\in \mathcal S_1}\frac{1}{2}\|\dq\|^2\\
    &\mathcal S_1=\left\{ \arg\min_{\dq\in \mathcal S_0}\frac{1}{2}\|J_1\dq-\dy_1\|^2\right\}
\end{align}
where $\mathcal S_0=\R^n$. So $\mathcal S_1$ is the set of all the joint velocities that satisfies the first task (if possible), or at least, minimize the error.
To satisfy also the second task, by keeping the first task accomplished, we might look for a solution among all the solution that already satisfies $\dy_1$:\begin{align}
    &\dq_2=\arg\min_{\dq\in\mathcal S_2}\frac{1}{2}\|\dq\|^2\\
    &\mathcal S_2=\left\{ \arg\min_{\dq\in \mathcal S_1}\frac{1}{2}\|J_2\dq-\dy_2\|^2\right\}
\end{align}
$\mathcal S_2$ is the subset of $\mathcal S_1$ of all the joint velocities that minimizes the error for the second task, so a solution found in $\mathcal S_2$ will be a solution\footnote{
    not necessarily a perfect solution, we are minimizing the task error, if might be impossible to perfectly realize the desired task trajectory.
} for the first task. It can be proved, that a solution for the second task can be found by considering the particular solution\begin{equation}
    \dq_2=\dq_1+(J_2P_1)^\#(\dy_2-J_2\dq_1)
\end{equation}
and a solution of the homogeneous equation, so the set of all the solution can be rewritten as\begin{equation}
    \mathcal S_2=\left\{
    \dq_2+P_{A,2}\mathbf v_2 \ : \  \mathbf v_2\in \R^n
    \right\}
\end{equation}
This procedure can be generalized: We have to find the solution up to the $k$-th task, we have found the prioritized solution $\dq_{k-1}$ and the set of all solutions up to task $k-1$ is\begin{equation}
     \mathcal S_{k-1}=\left\{
    \dq_{k-1}+P_{A,k-1}\mathbf v_{k-1} \ : \  \mathbf v_{k-1}\in \R^n
    \right\}
\end{equation}
The LQ problem for the $k$-th task is\begin{align}
    &\dq_k=\arg\min_{\dq\in\mathcal S_k}\frac{1}{2}\|\dq\|^2\\
    &\mathcal S_k=\left\{ \arg\min_{\dq\in \mathcal S_{k-1}}\frac{1}{2}\|J_k\dq-\dy_k\|^2\right\}
\end{align}
over the steps, the set of solutions is progressively reduced\begin{equation}
    \R^n=\mathcal S_0\supseteq\mathcal S_1\dots \supseteq \mathcal S_k\dots \supseteq \mathcal S_s
\end{equation}
The recursive formula to find a solution is 
\begin{equation}\label{task_priority_sol}
     \dq_k=\dq_{k-1}+(J_kP_{A,k-1})^\#(\dy_k-J_k\dq_{k-1})
\end{equation}
The solution up to the $k$-th task given in \eqref{task_priority_sol} satisfies also the previous tasks so\begin{equation}
    J_{A,k-1}\dq_k=J_{A,k-1}\dq_{k-1}
\end{equation}
So to solve the problem through an iterative method up to the $k$-th task we need to compute the null space projectors $P_{A,1},P_{A,2}\dots P_{A,k}$. There exists a recursive expression.
\begin{boxproposition}{Baerlocher, Boulic}{}
    The null space projector for the $k$-task is given by the following recursive formula\begin{equation}
        P_{A,k}=P_{A,k-1}-(J_kP_{A,k-1})^\#J_kP_{A,k-1}
    \end{equation}
    starting from $P_{A,0}=I$.
\end{boxproposition}
In presence of algorithmic singularities, for the $k$-th solution instead of the solution in Equation \eqref{task_priority_sol}, we need to apply the DLS method.
\section{Extensions to Redundancy Problems}
\subsection{Resolution at Acceleration Level}
Up to know, we have solved the redundancy problem for the joint velocities $\dq$, we know that at the lowest level, we don't control directly $\dq$, but the acceleration $\ddq$.\begin{equation}
    \mathbf y = k(\mathbf q)\implies \dy=J\dq\implies \ddy=J\ddq+\dot J\dq.
\end{equation}
\begin{boxdefinition}{Tangent Bundle}{}
    The \textbf{tangent bundle} is the set of couples $(\mathbf q,\dq)$ where $\mathbf q$ is a point of the configuration space $\mathcal C$ and $\dq$ is a point of the tangent space $T_{\mathbf q}\mathcal C$. If the points $\q$ are called configurations, the points $(\q,\dq)$ are called \textbf{states}. 
\end{boxdefinition}
In a given configuration $\mathbf q$, we know that the differential problem becomes linear in $\dq$, since $J=J(\mathbf q)$ evaluates becoming a linear operator. The same argument applies at acceleration level, in a given point of the tangent bundle $(\mathbf q, \dq)$, the Jacobian $J=J(\mathbf q)$ and it's derivative $\dot J=\dot J(\mathbf q,\dq)$ evaluates and the expression $\ddy=J\ddq+\dot J\dq$ becomes linear in $\ddq$\begin{equation}
   J\ddq = \ddy -\dot J\dq.
\end{equation}
A solution for $\ddq$ can be found with the null space method:\begin{equation}
    \ddq=J^\#\ddy+(I-J^\#J)\ddq_N
\end{equation}
where $\ddq_N$ is a preferred acceleration, usually defined as\begin{equation}
    \ddq_N=\alpha\nabla_{\mathbf q}G-K_D\dq 
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is a real number that weights the gradient of $G$, that is a performance metrics as in the projected gradient method. $-K_D\dq$ is a \textbf{damping term} to stabilize the joint motion in the null space, $K_D$ is a positive definite matrix.\bigskip 

The problem to find $\ddq_N$ in order to minimize $G(\mathbf q, \dq)$, is hard, in general $G$ is not even separable\footnote{$G(\mathbf q, \dq)$ can't be rewritten as $h(\mathbf q)+g(\dq)$ for some scalar functions $h,g$.}. This is a nonlinear optimal control problem, is hard to solve, so can be re-defined in discrete time assuming that joint accelerations are piecewise constant in time. At discrete time we have \begin{equation}
    \ddq(t)=\ddq_k, \ \ t\in[t_k,t_k+\Delta t]
\end{equation}
where $\Delta t$ is the sampling time step. $G_k$ is $G(\mathbf q_k,\dq_k)$ evaluated in $t\in[t_k,t_k+\Delta t]$. Given $\ddq_k$, the evolution of the velocities and the configuration can be approximated as\begin{align}
    &\dq_{k+1}=\dq_k+\Delta t\ddq_k\\
    &\mathbf q_{k+1}=\mathbf q_k+\Delta t\dq_k+\frac{1}{2}\Delta t^2\ddq_k
\end{align}
$G$ can be expanded locally around $(\mathbf q_k,\dq_k)$ in order to find the next value $G_{k+1}$:\begin{align}
    &G_{k+1}=G_k+\nabla_{\mathbf q}G_k(\mathbf q_{k+1}-\mathbf q_k)+
    \nabla_{\dq}G_k(\dq_{k+1}-\dq_k)=\\ 
    &G_k+\nabla_{\mathbf q}G_k\Delta t\dq_k+\Delta t\left(\frac{1}{2}\Delta t \nabla_{\mathbf q}G_k+\nabla_{\dq}G_k\right)\ddq
\end{align}
In order to get the largest decrease of $G_{k+1}-G_k$ the following preferred acceleration can be chosen:\begin{equation}
    \ddq_N=-\alpha_k\left(\frac{1}{2}\Delta t \nabla_{\mathbf q}G_k+\nabla_{\dq}G_k\right)
\end{equation}
for some real step size $\alpha_k>0$.
\subsection{Dynamic Resolution}
We can include the dynamic model of a manipulator, given by the following system of equations\begin{equation}\label{dynModel}
    M(\q)\ddq+\mathbf n(\q,\dq)=\btau
\end{equation}
where $M$ is the $n\times n$ symmetric inertia matrix, positive definite for all $\q$, $\mathbf n$ is a vector relative to centrifugal, coriolis and gravitational forces, and $\btau$ is the input torques vector. In a state $(\q,\dq)$, the matrix $M$ and the vector $\mathbf n$ evaluates and the Equation \eqref{dynModel} becomes bilinear in $\boldsymbol\tau$ and $\ddq$, $M$ is always invertible (more details in the next chapters) so there is a 1 to 1 mapping between $\btau$ and $\ddq$, in this way, we can easily choose $\btau$ to realize the desired $\ddq$.\bigskip

Since we are controlling directly the torque $\btau$, we can apply the usual methods that uses the gradient of an objective function as preferred solutions. One common choice is to minimize the torque norm:\begin{equation}\label{H_1_torque}
    H_1(\ddq)=\frac{1}{2}\|\btau\|^2=\frac{1}{2}\ddq^TM^2\ddq+\mathbf n^TM\ddq+\frac{1}{2}\mathbf n^T\mathbf n
\end{equation}
Remember that this (and the following) equations are evaluated in a given state $(\q,\dq)$, so that $M$ and $\mathbf n$ are evaluated in real numbers.
Another common choice for the objective function is the squared inverse inertia weighted norm:\begin{align}
    &H_2(\ddq)=\frac{1}{2}\|\btau\|^2_{M^{-2}}=\frac{1}{2}\btau M^{-2}\btau=\\
    &\label{H_2_torque}\frac{1}{2}\ddq^T\ddq+\mathbf n^TM^{-1}\ddq+\frac{1}{2}\mathbf n^TM^{-2}\mathbf n
\end{align}
is the weighted norm that consider the square of the inverse of the inertia matrix $M$. The last choice that we see is to use the weighted norm given by the inverse of $M$:
\begin{align}
    &H_3(\ddq)=\frac{1}{2}\|\btau\|^2_{M^{-1}}=\frac{1}{2}\btau M^{-1}\btau=\\
    &\label{H_3_torque}\frac{1}{2}\ddq^TM\ddq+\mathbf n^T\ddq+\frac{1}{2}\mathbf n^TM^{-1}\mathbf n.
\end{align}
Notice how all these Equations \eqref{H_1_torque},\eqref{H_2_torque} and \eqref{H_3_torque}, are quadratic forms in $\ddq$, so they all admit a closed form solution for $\ddq$, hence (since there is a 1 by 1 mapping) the value of the preferred torque $\btau$.\begin{itemize}
    \item The minimum torque solution is \begin{equation}
        \btau_1=(JM^{-1})^\#(\ddy-\dot J\dq+JM^{-1}\mathbf n)
    \end{equation}
    this local solution is good for short trajectories, but may leads to \textbf{whipping}: the solution minimizes only the norm of $\btau$ but doesn't try to minimize the joint velocities, if the requested trajectory is long, the speed of the joints could accumulate, the controller could require very high angular accelerations at the joints with lower inertia to compensate for the required movement, causing sudden jerks.
    \item The squared inverse inertia weighted norm solution is\begin{equation}
        \btau_2=MJ^\#(\ddy-\dot J\dq+JM^{-1}\mathbf n)
    \end{equation}
    this form prevents whipping and is to be preferred.
    \item The inverse inertia weighted norm solution is\begin{equation}
        \btau_3=J^T(JM^{-1}J^T)^{-1}(\ddy-\dot J\dq+JM^{-1}\mathbf n).
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}
\subsection{Kinematic Control and Mobile Manipulators}
So far we haven't considered in the redundancy problem the case where there is an initial task error. If the trajectory to follow is $\y_d(t)$ and the actual task position is $\y(t)$ (given by the DK and the current configuration $\q$), the error at any given time is\begin{equation}
    \mathbf e(t)=\y_d(t)-\y(t)
\end{equation} 
a non zero erro can be given by\begin{itemize}
    \item disturbances 
    \item initial mismatch 
    \item discrete-time implementation.
\end{itemize}
To solve this problem we need to close the feedback loop on task execution, instead of imposing the velocity $\dy=\dy_d$ (or the acceleration $\ddy=\ddy_d$), we the error in the control law:\begin{align}
    &\dy=\dy_d+K_P\mathbf e\\ 
    &\ddy=\ddy_d+K_D\dot{\mathbf e}+K_P\mathbf e
\end{align}
where $K,K_P$ and $K_D$ are positive definite matrices. We already seen in the Robotics 1 course that the inclusion of these terms are necessary to leads the error to zero.\bigskip

\noindent 
The next topic concerns mobile manipulators. These kinds of robots, usually have a configuration space that can be decoupled in two sets of variables:\begin{itemize}
    \item $\q_b$ are the configuration variables of the base
    \item $\q_m$ are the joint variables of the manipulator.
\end{itemize}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.3\textwidth ]{images/mobile_manipulator.eps}
\end{figure}
The task vector $\y$ is given by the direct kinematics that are a function of both the vectors $\q_m$ and $\q_b$\begin{equation}
    \y=k(\q)=k(\q_b,\q_m).
\end{equation}
The variables $\q_m$ are (as we already said) the joint angular positions. We can control the joint velocities $\dq_m$, let's denote our control input for the manipulator $\mathbf u_m$, that is exactly  $\dq_m$:\begin{equation}
    \mathbf u_m=\dq_m
\end{equation}
The variables $\q_b$ for a robot that moves on a plane, are the orientation $\phi$ and the position $x,y$ on that plane.\begin{equation}
    \q_b=\begin{pmatrix}
        x\\y\\\phi
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth ]{images/base_conf.eps}
\end{figure}
But our command for the base, denoted $\mathbf u_b$, is referred to the velocity of the wheels, in that case\begin{equation}
    \dq_b\ne \mathbf u_b 
\end{equation}
In general there is a relation that depends on the current base configuration:\begin{equation}\label{mobile_base_G}
    \dq_b=G(\q_b)\mathbf u_b.
\end{equation}
For example, assumes that we have a mobile base with two wheels. We can control the angular velocities $v_r,v_l$ of the two wheels, that are placed at distance $d$.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.3\textwidth ]{images/base_conf2.eps}
\end{figure}
we want to find the map between the linear velocity $v$ and the angular velocity $\omega=\dot \phi$ and $v_r,v_l$. There is a theory behind the definition of that map, but we will not cover it here. The linear velocity $v$ is simply given by the average velocity of the two wheels:\begin{equation}
    v=\frac{1}{2}(v_l+v_r)
\end{equation}
the angular velocity is given by\begin{equation}
    \omega = \frac{v_r-v_l}{d}
\end{equation}
given the linear velocity $v$, the components on $x$ and $y$ are\begin{align}
    &\dot x = v\cos\phi \\ 
    &\dot y=v\sin \phi
\end{align}
so the map $G(\q_b)$ is \begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \dot x\\\dot y\\ \omega
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        \nicefrac{1}{2}\cos\phi&\nicefrac{1}{2}\cos\phi\\ 
        \nicefrac{1}{2}\sin\phi&\nicefrac{1}{2}\sin\phi\\ 
        \nicefrac{1}{d}&-\nicefrac{1}{d}
    \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
        v_r\\v_l
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
We want to find the relation between $\dy$ and $\dq$ for a mobile base manipulator. Given \begin{equation}
    \y=k(\q)=k(\q_b,\q_m)
\end{equation}
we consider the derivative\begin{equation}
    \dy=\left( \frac{\partial k}{\partial\q_b }\right)\dq_b+
    \left( \frac{\partial k}{\partial\q_m }\right)\dq_m
\end{equation}
from Equation \eqref{mobile_base_G}:
\begin{equation}
    \dy=\left( \frac{\partial k}{\partial\q_b }\right)G(\q_b)\mathbf u_b+
    \left( \frac{\partial k}{\partial\q_m }\right)\mathbf u_m=\begin{pmatrix}
        J_bG&J_m
    \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
        \mathbf u_b\\ \mathbf u_m
    \end{pmatrix}=J_{NMM}\mathbf u
\end{equation}
the matrix $J_{NMM}$ is called \textbf{Nonholonomic Mobile Manipulator Jacobian}. All the results seen to resolve the redundancy problem can be applied by considering $J_{NMM}$ instead of the classic Jacobian $J$ and the available velocity commands $\mathbf u$ instead of $\dq$.
\subsection{Hard Limits in the Joint Space}
Until now, we have seen how the joint velocities solution $\dq$ that we seek during the redundancy resolution, are a solution to an optimization problem, in details, a quadratic programming problem of the kind:\begin{align}
    &\arg\min_\dq \dq^T W\dq\\ 
    &\text{s.t. }J\dq=\dy
\end{align}
where $W$ is usually positive definite. The set of solutions is defined by the $m$ \textit{equality} constraints $J\dq=\dy$, but in it's general formulation, a quadratic program have the solutions defined in a convex set given by both equality and inequality constraints:
\begin{align}
    &\dq^*=\arg\min_\dq^T \dq W\dq\\ 
    &\text{s.t. }\begin{matrix}
        J\dq=\dy\\ 
        C\dq\le \mathbf d
    \end{matrix}
\end{align}
We know that $J\dq=\dy$ is not always feasible, so in practice we search for the solution $\dq^*$ that minimizes the task error $\|J\dq-\dy\|$. 

In many cases, there is the need to add also inequality constraints to the optimization problem, the most canonical example is the inclusion of bounds of joint velocities, limits that usually comes from the specifications of the actuators.
A set of inequality constraints such as $C\dq\le \mathbf d$ identifies a convex set in $\R^n$.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth ]{images/ineq_constraints.eps}
     \caption{A convex set (in green) defined by three inequality constraints.}
\end{figure}
By adding also some equality constraints, the set of solutions restrict in the intersection between the polyhedron and the hyper plane given by the equality constraints.
\begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth ]{images/ineq_constraints_2.eps}
\end{figure}
If the constraint $\mathbf c_2^T\dq\le d_2$ have higher priority than $\mathbf c_3^T\dq\le d_3$, and the equality constraint $J\dq=\dy$ must be satisfied, the solution will be the following:
\begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth ]{images/ineq_constraints_3.eps}
\end{figure}
This solution $\dq^*$ is the feasible one closest to the inequality constraint $\mathbf c_2^T\dq\le d_2$, in particular, that solution saturate that constraint:\begin{equation}
    \mathbf c_2^T\dq^*= d_2
\end{equation}
when this happens, we say that the constraint becomes \textbf{active}. If the constraint $\mathbf c_3^T\dq\le d_3$ would have higher priority than $\mathbf c_2^T\dq\le d_2$, the solution would be the following:

\end{figure}

As we already mentioned, the robot has some ''limited'' capabilities given by the actuators, have limited joint ranges and/or limitation on the joint velocities, acceleration and torque. These constraints are ''boxes'' in the joint configuration and tangent space, that can never be violated, at most can became active and be saturated. The equality tasks $J\dq=\dy$ are usually the highest priority, but can be relaxed in case they require unfeasible performance from the actuators.\bigskip 

The \textbf{Saturation in the Null Space (SNS)} is a method to deal with these limits in the context of online resolution for the joint velocity, it consists in saturating one overdriven joint command at a time, until a feasible and better
performing solution is found. it requires some \textit{decisions}, in which joint we should saturate to not affect the task execution, for tasks that are (certainly) unfeasible, SNS embeds the selection of a task
scaling factor preserving execution of the task direction with minimal scaling. The general solution provided by the SNS is the following:\begin{equation}\label{eq:SNS}
    \dq_{SNS}=(JW)^\#s\dy+\left(
I-(JW)^\#J
    \right)\dq_N
\end{equation}
where\begin{itemize}
    \item $W$ is a diagonal matrix such that the entries are 0 or 1 and is needed to select which joint will saturate. 
    \item $s$ is the scaling factor of the required task velocity $\dy$.
    \item $\dq_N$ is the vector of the saturated (full speed) joint velocities.
\end{itemize}
This kind of solution starts from the pseudo inverse solution $J^\#\dy$ and add a ''correction'' to define a new solution that activate one constraints by remaining feasible, this correction is always in the null space of the Jacobian $\mathcal N(J)$ (up to a scaling factor, if present).
\begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=\textwidth ]{images/SNS.eps}
\end{figure}
\textbf{Example}: Let's consider a 4R planar robot with link length $l$ for all the links. The Jacobian matrix for the task of positioning in the end effector plane is\begin{equation}
    J(\q)=\begin{pmatrix}
       -ls_1-ls_{12} -ls_{123} -ls_{1234} &
        -ls_{12} -ls_{123} -ls_{1234} 
         &-ls_{123} -ls_{1234} 
          &-ls_{1234} \\ 
          lc_1+lc_{12}+lc_{123}+lc_{1234}&
         lc_{12}+lc_{123}+lc_{1234}&
         lc_{123}+lc_{1234}&
          lc_{1234}
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
there are the following bounds on the joint velocities:\begin{align*}
    &|q_i|\le V_i, \ \ i=1,2,3,4\\ 
    &V_1=V_2=2 \ \nicefrac{\text{rad}}{\text{sec}}\\ 
    &V_3=V_4=4 \ \nicefrac{\text{rad}}{\text{sec}}
\end{align*}
In the configuration\begin{equation}
    \q=\begin{pmatrix}
        \frac{\pi}{2}&-\frac{\pi}{2}&\frac{\pi}{2}&-\frac{\pi}{2}
    \end{pmatrix}^T
\end{equation}
the Jacobian evaluates in \begin{equation}
    J=\begin{pmatrix}
        -2&-1&-1&0\\ 
        2&2&1&1
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
If the desired end effector velocity is \begin{equation}
    \dy=\begin{pmatrix}
        -4&-1.5
    \end{pmatrix}^T
\end{equation}
the solution provided by the pseudo inverse is\begin{equation}
    \dq_{PS}=J^\#\dy=\frac{1}{11} \begin{pmatrix} -6 & -2 \\ 4 & 5 \\ -3 & -1 \\ 7 & 6 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
        -4\\ -1.5
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        2.4545&-2.1364&1.2273&-3.3636
    \end{pmatrix}^T
\end{equation}
The first two joints exceeds the maximum velocity, if we scale the solution with the maximum scale factor required to be under the limits, the solution becomes\begin{equation}
    s\dq_{PS}=\begin{pmatrix}
        2&
        -1.74&
        1&
        -2.74
    \end{pmatrix}^T
\end{equation}
with $s=0.8148$.
instead, if we saturate only the \textit{most violating} velocity with the SNS method, we get the solution\begin{equation}
    \dq_{SNS}=\begin{pmatrix}
        2&-1.8333&1.8333&-3.6667
    \end{pmatrix}^T
\end{equation}

\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth ]{images/SNS2.eps}
\end{figure}
As we can see by the figure, the solution provided by the \greenText{SNS procedure } is way closer to the \sapText{desired solution } respect the \blueText{scaled one}.\bigskip

Since we control the robot by velocity commands by imposing $\dq$, we can't directly impose a limits for the joint ranges $\q$ and the accelerations $\ddq$. Consider a full set of limits in the most general way:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{matrix}
        Q_{min,i}\le q_i \le Q_{max,i} \\ 
        -V_{max,i}\le \dot q_i \le V_{max,i}\\ 
        -A_{max,i}\le \ddot q_i \le A_{max,i}
    \end{matrix} \ \ \ i=1,2,\dots n
\end{equation}
During the online resolution ad discrete time, we can \textbf{convert} the acceleration and position limits in velocity constraints by making the velocity bounds $Q_{min,i},Q_{max,i}$ not constant but variable\begin{equation}
    \dot{\mathbf Q}_{min,i}(t_k) \le \dq \le \dot{\mathbf Q}_{max,i}(t_k)
\end{equation} 
where $t_k$ is the current time step, and $q_{k,i}=q_i(t_k)$. Let $T$ to be the sampling time, we define the velocity constraints (at each time step) as follows:\begin{align*}
    &
    \dot Q_{min,i}=\max\left\{
        \frac{Q_{min,i}-q_{k,i}}{T},
        -V_{max,i}, 
        -\sqrt{2A_{max,i}(q_{k,i}-Q_{min,i})}
        \right\}
    \\ 
    &
    \dot Q_{max,i}=\min\left\{
        \frac{Q_{max,i}-q_{k,i}}{T},
        V_{max,i}, 
        \sqrt{2A_{max,i}(Q_{min,i}-q_{k,i})}
        \right\}
\end{align*} 
In this way, if $q_i$ is approaching one of it's bound, the maximum velocity for $\dot q_i$ will decrease to zero.\bigskip

The Equation \eqref{eq:SNS} define how the solution will be evaluated, the control law of the SNS algorithm works to decide what are the joints to exceeds and what is the maximum scaling factor to consider, by choosing at each time step the matrix $W$ and the scalar value $s$.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
    \caption{Saturation in the Null Space}\label{alg:SNS}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Function{MainLoop}{$\dy, J$}
    \State limit\_exceeded = TRUE
    \State $W=I$, $s=1$, $s^*=0$, $\dq_N=\mathbf 0$ \greenText{//$s^*$ is the largest task scale factor found so far}
    \While{limit\_exceeded = TRUE}
    \State limit\_exceeded = FALSE
    \State $\overline\dq=\dq_N+(JW)^\#(\dy-J\dq_N)$
    \If{$\exists i \in \{1,\dots n\}$ such that $\overline{\dot {q_i}} < \dot Q_{min,i}$ \texttt{or} $\overline{\dot {q_i}} > \dot Q_{max,i}$}
    \State \greenText{//some joints exceed their maximum speed}
    \State limit\_exceeded = TRUE
    \State $\mathbf a=(JW)^\#\dy$
    \State $\mathbf b=\overline\dq-\mathbf a$
    \State  $s=$ \Call{GetTaskScalingFactor}{$\mathbf a,\mathbf b$}
    \If{$s>s^*$}
    \State $s^*=s$, $W^*=W$, $\dq_N^*=\dq_N$
    \EndIf 
    \State $j=$ the most critical joint (closest to it's bound) 
    \State $W_{jj}=0$
    \State $\dot q_{N,j}=\begin{cases}
        \dot Q_{max,j} \ \text{if }\overline{\dot q_j} > \dot Q_{max,j}\\ 
        \dot Q_{min,j}  \ \text{if }\overline{\dot q_j} < \dot Q_{min,j}
    \end{cases}$
    \If{rank($JW<m$)}
    \State $s=s^*$, $W=W^*$, $\dq_N=\dq_N^*$
    \State $\overline\dq=\dq_N+(JW)^\#(s\dy-J\dq_N)$ 
    \State limit\_exceeded = FALSE \greenText{ //the algorithm ends}
    \EndIf
    \EndIf
    \EndWhile
    \State \Return $\dq_{SNS}=\overline\dq$
    \EndFunction
    \vspace{0.5cm}
    \Function{GetTaskScalingFactor}{$\mathbf a, \mathbf b$}
    \State \greenText{//at this point $\dq_{SNS}=s\mathbf a+\mathbf b$}
    \For{$i = 1 \to n$}
        \State $S_{min,i} = (\dot{Q}_{min,i} - b_i) / a_i$
        \State $S_{max,i} = (\dot{Q}_{max,i} - b_i) / a_i$
        \If{$S_{min,i} > S_{max,i}$}
            \State swap $S_{min,i}$ and $S_{max,i}$
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \State $s_{max} = \min_i \{S_{max,i}\}$
    \State $s_{min} = \max_i \{S_{min,i}\}$
    \State the most critical joint $= \arg \min_i \{S_{max,i}\}$
    \If{$s_{min} > s_{max}$ \texttt{or} $s_{max} < 0$ \texttt{or} $s_{min} > 1$}
        \State\Return 0
    \Else
        \State\Return $s_{max}$
    \EndIf
    \EndFunction
    \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\chapter{Dynamics}
The dynamics concerns the causes of the motion, and consider the generalized forces $\mathbf u$ that acts on the mechanical structure. Typically, this relation is given by a system of second order differential equation:\begin{equation}
    \Phi(\q,\dq,\ddq)=\mathbf u.
\end{equation}
The problem of direct dynamics consists in finding the trajectory $\q(t)$ over time, by knowing the profile of the forces $\mathbf u(t)$. There are experimental and simulation solutions, the latter consists in integrating over time the differential equations (solve numerically).

The inverse problem consists in finding the required forces $\mathbf u(t)$ to realize a desired motion $\q(t)$. Can be done experimentally by \textit{iterative learning}, or can be computed algebraically at every time instant $t$ by knowing the analytical model of the manipulator. There are two main approach to find the dynamic model:\begin{itemize}
    \item The \textbf{Euler-Lagrange} method is an \textit{energy based} approach. It provides the dynamic equations in symbolic form, and is the best method to study the dynamic properties and for the analysis of control schemes. Despite that, the terms in the model grows (more than exponentially) with the increase of the degree of freedom, so is not feasible to use it real time to control the manipulator. 
    \item The \textbf{Newton-Euler} method is based on a balance of forces and moments. The dynamic equations are provided in a numerical/recursive formulation, and is the best way to implement the dynamics in the real time control schemes. 
\end{itemize}
The following discussion will be done under the assumption that the links are rigid bodies.
\section{Lagrangian Dynamics}

The full evolution in time of a manipulator can be described by the profile $\q(t)$ of the joint variables, $\q$ is a set of generalized coordinates (also called Lagrangian coordinates), and identify completely the configuration of the system. 

This discussion can be done for any mechanical (and also non mechanical) system. Given an initial configuration $(\q(0),\dq(0))$, what will be the profile of $\q(t)$? To answer the question, we need to define the Lagrangian.

\begin{boxdefinition}{Lagrangian}{}
    Given a system of rigid bodies described by the Lagrangian coordinates $\q$, let $U(\q)$ to be the potential energy of the system, and $T(\q,\dq)$ to be the kinetic energy. The \textbf{Lagrangian} of the system is a function of the state $(\q,\dq)$ and is defined as the difference between the kinetic and the potential energy:\begin{equation}
        \mathcal L(\q,\dq)=T(\q,\dq)-U(\q).
    \end{equation}
\end{boxdefinition}

We have to define a quantity called \textbf{Action}, this is a functional that, in a given time interval $[0,t]$, maps each trajectory $\q:[0,t]\rightarrow \R^n$ to a real number, computed through an integration:\begin{equation}
    \mathcal S[\q(t)]=\int_0^t\mathcal L(\q(t),\dq(t))dt
\end{equation}
So the action is defined as the integral of the Lagrangian over time, to each trajectory $\q(t)$, is associated a value of the action. The following principle is crucial to determine which trajectory the system will follow.
\begin{boxpostulate}{Principle of Least Action}{}
The trajectory $\q(t)$ that a system of rigid bodies will follow, is the one that \textbf{minimizes the action} over time.
\end{boxpostulate}
So, the challenge of finding the trajectory in general, is a problem of calculus of variations:\begin{equation}
    \q^*(t)=\arg\min_{\q(t)} \mathcal S[\q(t)].
\end{equation}
The trajectory that minimize the action is the one that satisfies the \textbf{Euler-Lagrange Equations}:\begin{equation}
    \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot q_i}-\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial q_i}=u_i, \  \ \ i=1,\dots n
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf u=\begin{pmatrix}
    u_1&\dots & u_n
\end{pmatrix}^T$ is the set of non conservative forces acting on the joints (torques given by the motor, friction, etc..). The set of $n$ scalar equation can be rewritten as a single vector equation:\begin{equation}
    \frac{d}{dt}\nabla_{\dq}\mathcal L-\nabla_{\q}\mathcal L=\mathbf u.
\end{equation}
From these equations we will derive the dynamic model in the $n$ Lagrangian coordinates $\q$. We will also see how, the total energy of a manipulator is computed by adding together the energies of each link.\subsection{The Actuated Pendulum Example}
Now, we consider the model of an actuated pendulum, in order to find the dynamic equation with the Lagrangian approach. This simple system is one-dimensional (angular position of the pendulum), so we expect to derive a single equation. Observe the diagram in Figure \ref{fig:actuadedPendulum}:\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{Dynamic parameter}: $I_m$ is the moment of inertia of the motor around it's rotation axis. 
    \item \texttt{Control command}: $\tau_m$ is the torque provided by the actuators that makes the motor to accelerate.
    \item \texttt{Variable/configuration of the system}: $\theta_m$ is the angular position of the motor.
    \item \texttt{Dynamic parameter}: $b_m$ is the viscous friction on the motor that acts as a damping factor, and $b_l$ is the viscous friction on the link.
    \item \texttt{Kinematic parameter}: $n_r$ is the reduction ratio between the motor and the link. 
    \item \texttt{Control command}: $\tau$ is the torque on the link and depends on $\tau_m$ according to the reduction ratio.
    \item \texttt{Variable/configuration of the system}: $\theta$ is the angular position of the link and depends on $\theta_m$ according to the reduction ratio.
    \item \texttt{Dynamic parameter}: $m$ is the mass of the link and $I_l$ is the moment of inertia of the link around the axis parallel to the joint axis that pass through it's center of mass. 
    \item \texttt{Kinematic parameter}:  $d_c$ is the distance between the joint axis and the center of mass, and $l$ is the length of the link.
    \item \texttt{Control command}: $F_x$ is an external force that can be applied on the tip of the link, and is only horizontal (orthogonal to both the joint axis and the direction of the gravity).
    \item \texttt{External force}: $mg_0$ is the gravity applied on the link.
\end{itemize}
\begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth ]{images/actuated_pendulum.drawio.pdf}
     \caption{Simple model of an actuated pendulum.}
     \label{fig:actuadedPendulum}
\end{figure}
The system is one dimensional and it's configuration is described by $\theta$ or $\theta_m$ since these two are mapped one to the other:\begin{align}
   & \dot \theta_m=n_r\dot\theta\\ 
   &  \theta_m=n_r\theta\\ 
   &\tau=n_r\tau_m.
\end{align}
So, our Lagrangian coordinate will be $q=\theta$. We want to compute the kinetic energy of the system, that is given by the sum between the kinetic energy of the motor and the kinetic energy of the link. The kinetic energy of the motor can be easily computed:\begin{equation}
    T_m=\frac{1}{2}I_m\dot\theta^2_m
\end{equation}
To compute $T_l$ instead we need to consider the inertia of the link around the joint axis, since the link rotate around that axis and not around it's center of mass. From the theorem of the parallel axes, by knowing that $d_c$ is the distance between the joint axis and the center of mass, we can compute the inertia:\begin{equation}
    I_l+md_c^2
\end{equation}
the kinetic energy is \begin{equation}
    T_l=\frac{1}{2}(I_l+md_c^2)\dot\theta^2
\end{equation}
The total kinetic energy of the system is\begin{equation}
    T=T_l+T_m=\frac{1}{2}I_m\dot\theta^2_m+\frac{1}{2}(I_l+md_c^2)\dot\theta^2
\end{equation}
there is a problem: it should be expressed in the same variable, so we convert $\theta_m$ to $\theta$ by considering the reduction ratio:\begin{equation}
    T=\frac{1}{2}(
        I_l+md_c^2+I_mn_r^2
    )\dot\theta^2=\frac{1}{2}I\dot\theta^2
\end{equation}
we denote $I$ the total inertia of the system.
The potential energy is given by $mg_0y$ where $y$ is the vertical coordinate of the center of mass.
\begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth ]{images/actuated_pendulum2.eps}
\end{figure}
Since $\theta=0$ identifies the configuration where the pendulum is pointing downward (so, $\theta$ is the angle between the $y$ axis and the link), the $y$ coordinate is given by $-d_c\cos\theta$, the potential energy is\begin{equation}
    U=U_0-mg_0d_c\cos\theta
\end{equation}
where $U_0$ is the arbitrary constant. At this point, we can explicit the Lagrangian of the system:\begin{equation}
    \mathcal L=\frac{1}{2}I\dot\theta^2+mg_0d_c\cos\theta-U_0.
\end{equation}
The Euler-Lagrange equation is the following\begin{equation}
    \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot\theta}-\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \theta}=u
\end{equation}
we need to define the term $u$ that contains all the non conservative forces that adds or subtracts energy from the system. In particular, since the variable is $\theta$, all these forces will be in the form of torques applied on the link.\begin{itemize}
    \item  The first term is the torque on the link $\tau$ produced by the motor, that is $n_r\tau_m$. $\tau_m$ is one of the two control input of the system.
    \item Then, we have the friction of the link: $-b_l\dot\theta$.
    \item The friction of the motor is $-b_m\theta_m$. On the link side is given by $-n_rb_m\theta_m$ (the torque is multiplied by the reduction ratio), by converting then $\theta_m$ in order to express this term in $\theta$ we get $-n_r^2b_m\theta$.
    \item The last term is the torque on the link produced by the controlled force $F_x$.
\end{itemize}
We have to find the relation between the force $F_x$ and the torque on the link. To do so, we can proceed with a kinematic approach. The pendulum is a simple robotic manipulator, the direct kinematic for the position on the $x$ axis is given by\begin{equation}
    p_x=l\sin\theta
\end{equation}
\begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.3\textwidth ]{images/actuated_pendulum3.eps}
\end{figure}
The relation between the velocity on of the end effector and $\dot\theta$ is given by the Jacobian matrix\begin{equation}
    \dot p_x=J\dot\theta=\frac{\partial p_x}{\partial \theta}\dot\theta=l\cos\theta\cdot \dot \theta
\end{equation}
In this case the Jacobian is a scalar and his transpose is still $l\cos\theta$. We recall that the Jacobian transpose maps the forces on the end effector to the torques on the joints, so the torque produced by $F_x$ is just $JF_x=l\cos\theta F_x$.
So:\begin{align}
    &\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot\theta}=I\dot\theta\\
    &\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot\theta}=\frac{d}{dt}\left(I\dot\theta\right)=I\ddot\theta\\ 
    &\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \theta}=-mg_0d_c\sin\theta\\ 
    &\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot\theta}-\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \theta}= I\ddot\theta+mg_0d_c\sin\theta.
\end{align}
The complete Euler-Lagrange equation with all the terms is:\begin{equation}
    I\ddot\theta+mg_0d_c\sin\theta=n_r\tau_m-(b_l+b_mn_r^2)\dot\theta+l\cos\theta F_x.
\end{equation}
Is a non linear second order differential equation and can't be solved analytically.\bigskip

The  diagram in Figure \ref{fig:pendulum_DD} describes the relation between the input forces $\tau_m,F_x$ and the configuration profile $\theta$ over time.
\begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=\textwidth ]{images/DirectDynamics_simulink.drawio.pdf}
     \caption{Symulink block diagram of the direct dynamics.}
     \label{fig:pendulum_DD}
\end{figure}
\noindent This is how is compute the \textbf{direct dynamics} of the system, by isolating the acceleration term:\begin{equation}
    \ddot\theta=\frac{1}{I}\left(n_r\tau_m-(b_l+b_mn_r^2)\dot\theta+l\cos\theta F_x-mg_0d_c\sin\theta\right)
\end{equation}
and knowing the initial condition $\dot q(0),q(0)$, we can integrate over time the differential equation and estimate the time profile of $\ddot \theta$ (and consequently, of $\theta$). The \textbf{inverse dynamics} instead, have the goal to compute the control forces $\tau_m,F_x$ in order to execute the desired trajectory $\theta_d(t)$. In this case we need the assumption that the second derivative of $\theta_d(t)$ exists and is piece-wise continuous.

For this actuated pendulum we have two control forces, the motor torque $\tau_m$ and the force on the tool tip $F_x$, but there is only one variable for the configuration, so we are in a situation of \textit{redundancy of forces}. Let's compute the inverse dynamics for two case:\begin{itemize}
    \item (Case 1: $F_x=0$) we have our desired trajectory $\theta_d(t)$, we isolate the input torque:\begin{equation}
        \tau_m=\frac{1}{n_r}\left(I\ddot\theta_d+mg_0d_c\sin\theta_d+(b_l+b_mn_r^2)\dot\theta_d\right)=\frac{1}{n_r}f(\theta_d,\dot\theta_d,\ddot\theta_d)
    \end{equation}
    we denote $f(\theta_d,\dot\theta_d,\ddot\theta_d)$ the right term without the $\frac{1}{n_r}$ factor. Notice how the inverse dynamics is perfectly computable at each time step, we can always get the needed value for $\tau_m$. 
    \item (Case 2: $\tau_m=0$) we proceed in the same way by isolating $F_x$:\begin{equation}
        F_x=\frac{1}{l\cos\theta_d}\left(I\ddot\theta_d+mg_0d_c\sin\theta_d+(b_l+b_mn_r^2)\dot\theta_d\right)=\frac{1}{l\cos\theta_d}f(\theta_d,\dot\theta_d,\ddot\theta_d)
    \end{equation}
    in this case, the needed force $F_x$ is not always defined, since in some configurations, the force to execute the desired trajectory grows to infinity due to the $l\cos\theta_d$ at the denominator. This is a case of \textbf{singularity} in the dynamics context. The singular configurations are $\theta_d\in\{-\nicefrac{\pi}{2},\nicefrac{\pi}{2}\}$.
\end{itemize}
Now we consider the most general case where both the forces can be applied.
\begin{align}
    &n_r\tau_m+l\cos\theta_dF_x=f(\theta_d,\dot\theta_d,\ddot\theta_d)\\
    &\label{redundantForces}u_1+u_2=f(\theta_d,\dot\theta_d,\ddot\theta_d)
\end{align}
How to resolve the inverse dynamics in this redundant case? The equation \eqref{redundantForces} can be rewritten as a dot product between two vectors\begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        1&1
    \end{pmatrix}
      \begin{pmatrix}
        u_1\\u_1
    \end{pmatrix}=f(\theta_d,\dot\theta_d,\ddot\theta_d)
\end{equation}
As we well know, every time there is a system of equations $A\mathbf x=\mathbf b$ where $A$ have more columns than rows, we can solve the system by finding the minimum norm solution through the pseudo inverse (we abbreviate the right term with $f$):\begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        u_1\\u_1
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        1&1
    \end{pmatrix}^\#f=\begin{pmatrix}
        \nicefrac{1}{2}\\\nicefrac{1}{2}
    \end{pmatrix}f=\begin{pmatrix}
        \nicefrac{f}{2}\\\nicefrac{f}{2}
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
this solution minimizes $u_1^2+u_2^2$, but remember that these terms are our control input but not directly the forces provided by the actuators. Instead, we should rewrite the relation as follows:
    \begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        n_r&l\cos\theta_d
    \end{pmatrix}
      \begin{pmatrix}
        \tau_m\\F_x
    \end{pmatrix}=f
\end{equation}
in this case we can solve for $ \begin{pmatrix}
        \tau_m&F_x
    \end{pmatrix}^T$ in order to minimizes our actuators output.\begin{equation}
        \begin{pmatrix}
        \tau_m\\F_x
    \end{pmatrix}= \begin{pmatrix}
        n_r&l\cos\theta_d
    \end{pmatrix}^\#f=\frac{1}{n_r^2+(l\cos\theta_d)^2}\begin{pmatrix}
        n_r\\l\cos\theta_d
    \end{pmatrix}f.
    \end{equation}
\subsection{Derivation of the Dynamic Model}
In this section we explain how to derive the Lagrangian of a full robotic manipulator. The kinetic energy of the robot will be the sum of the kinetic energies of each link:\begin{equation}
   T= \sum_{i=1}^nT_i
\end{equation}
The kinetic energy of the $i$-th link will depend on previous links (we are considering only open kinematic chain manipulators). So $T_i$ will depend on the state variables restricted to the first $i$ joints: \begin{equation}
    T_i=T_i(q_j,\dot q_j), \ \ j\in\{1\dots i\}.
\end{equation}
Let's start by describing the dynamic parameters of a single link.
\begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth ]{images/kinetic_energy_link.pdf}
\end{figure}
$RF_c$ is the reference frame attached to the center of mass of the rigid body, $RF_0$ our base reference frame and the position of the center of mass in the base frame is $\mathbf r_c$. The mass of the object is given by\begin{equation}
    m=\int_B\rho(x,y,z)dxdydz=\int_B dm
\end{equation}
where $B$ is the set describing the rigid body and $\rho$ the density function. We will assume for now on, that all the rigid bodies have an homogeneous density. The center of mass is given by\begin{equation}
    \mathbf r_c=\frac{1}{m}\int_B\mathbf r(x,y,z) dm 
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf r$ is the distance between the origin and the infinitesimal point that we are considering in the integral (we drop in the notation the dependence on $x,y,z$). When all the vectors are expressed in the reference frame attached to the center of mass of the body, $\mathbf r_c$ will be null:\begin{equation}\label{rcZero}
    \mathbf r_c=\mathbf 0\implies \int_B\mathbf rdm=\mathbf 0.
\end{equation}
Since the kinetic energy of a single point is $\frac{1}{2}mv^2$ (where $m$ is the mass and $v$ the norm of the velocity), to compute the kinetic energy of the whole rigid body we can integrate all the infinitesimal energies of each infinitesimal point of $B$:\begin{equation}
    T=\frac{1}{2}\int_B\mathbf v^T\mathbf vdm
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf v$ is the velocity of a single point and depend on $x,y,z$. We can rewrite the kinetic energy by exploiting the following relation:\begin{equation}
    \mathbf v = \mathbf v_c+\boldsymbol\omega\times \mathbf r=\mathbf v_c+S(\boldsymbol\omega)\mathbf r
\end{equation}
that explain how the velocity of a single point is the sum between the velocity of the center of mass and the cross product between the angular velocity and the distance of the point from the center of mass. $S(\boldsymbol\omega)$ is the skew symmetric matrix\begin{equation}
    S(\boldsymbol\omega)=\begin{pmatrix}
        0&-\omega_z&\omega_y\\ 
        \omega_z&0&-\omega_x\\ 
        -\omega_y&\omega_x&0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
the kinetic energy can be rewritten as follows\begin{equation}
    T=\frac{1}{2}\int_B(\mathbf v_c+S(\boldsymbol\omega)\mathbf r)^T(\mathbf v_c+S(\boldsymbol\omega)\mathbf r)dm
\end{equation}
by distributing the product we can split the equation in three integrals\begin{equation}
    T=\frac{1}{2}\int_B \mathbf v_c^T\mathbf v_c+
    \int_B\mathbf v_c^TS(\bomega)\mathbf rdm+\frac{1}{2}\int_B\mathbf r^TS^T(\bomega)S(\bomega)\mathbf rdm
\end{equation}\begin{itemize}
    \item The first term$$\frac{1}{2}\int_B \mathbf v_c^T\mathbf v_c$$
    can be easily computed since $\mathbf v_c$ is the velocity of the center of mass and don't depend on the integrating variable so$$
    \frac{1}{2}\int_B \mathbf v_c^T\mathbf v_c=\frac{1}{2}\mathbf v_c^T\mathbf v_c\int_Bdm=\frac{1}{2}\mathbf v_c^T\mathbf v_cm$$
    \item in the second term 
    $$  \int_B\mathbf v_c^TS(\bomega)\mathbf rdm $$ 
    for the same reason we can carry $\mathbf v_c^TS(\bomega)$ outside the integral
    $$  \mathbf v_c^TS(\bomega)\int_B\mathbf rdm $$ 
    from the equation \eqref{rcZero}, by expressing everything in the reference frame attached to the center of mass, we can eliminate this term:
     $$  \mathbf v_c^TS(\bomega)\int_B\mathbf rdm = \mathbf v_c^TS(\bomega)\mathbf 0= 0 $$ 
     \item the third term can be transformed by using the following identity
     $$
     \mathbf r^TS^T(\bomega)S(\bomega)\mathbf r=
     \bomega^TS^T(\mathbf r)S(\mathbf r)\bomega
     $$
     so 
     $$ 
     \frac{1}{2}\int_B\mathbf r^TS^T(\bomega)S(\bomega)\mathbf rdm=
     \frac{1}{2}\int_B\bomega^TS^T(\mathbf r)S(\mathbf r)\bomega dm
     $$
     $\bomega$ can be carried out the integral:
     $$
     \frac{1}{2}\bomega^T\left(\int_BS^T(\mathbf r)S(\mathbf r) dm\right)\bomega
     $$
\end{itemize}
\begin{boxdefinition}{Inertia Matrix}{}
    The \textbf{inertia matrix} around the center of mass of a rigid body $B$ is the following:\begin{equation}
        I_c=\int_BS^T(\mathbf r)S(\mathbf r) dm.
    \end{equation}
\end{boxdefinition}
So, by the previous discussion, we get to the following result.
\begin{boxtheorem}{König}{}
    The kinetic energy of a rigid body is given by the following equation:
    \begin{equation}
    T=\frac{1}{2}\mathbf v_c^T\mathbf v_cm+\frac{1}{2}\bomega^TI_c\bomega.
\end{equation}
\end{boxtheorem}
So the kinetic energy of the $i$-th link is \begin{equation}
    T_i=\frac{1}{2}\mathbf v_{ci}^T\mathbf v_{ci}m+\frac{1}{2}\bomega_i^TI_{ci}\bomega_i
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf v_{ci}$ is the linear velocity of the $i$-th link, $\bomega_i$ the angular velocity of the $i$-th link and $I_{ci}$ is the inertia matrix of the $i$-th link. We recall that $\mathbf v_{ci}$ and $\bomega_i$ are computed through the (partial) geometric Jacobian up to the $i$-th link, so $T_i$ is a function of $q_1,\dots q_i,\dot q_1,\dots \dot q_i$.
\begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth ]{images/partialJacobians.png}
\end{figure}\begin{equation}
    \mathbf v_{ci}=J_L^i(\q)\dq=\begin{pmatrix}
         & & & 0 &  & 0\\
          J_{L1}(\q)&\dots & J_{Li}(\q) & 0 &\dots & 0\\
           & & & 0 &  & 0
    \end{pmatrix}\dq
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \bomega_i=J_A^i(\q)\dq=\begin{pmatrix}
         & & & 0 &  & 0\\
          J_{A1}(\q)&\dots & J_{Ai}(\q) & 0 &\dots & 0\\
           & & & 0 &  & 0
    \end{pmatrix}\dq
\end{equation}
$J_{Lj}$ is the $j$-th columns of the linear part of the geometric Jacobian and $J_{Aj}$ is the $j$-th columns of the rotational part of the geometric Jacobian. The matrices $J_L^i(\q),J_A^i(\q)$ are called partial Jacobians. At this point we can sum over the links and get the expression of the kinetic energy of the manipulator:\begin{equation}
    T=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^n(m_i\mathbf v_{ci}^T\mathbf v_{ci}+\bomega^T_iI_{ci}\bomega_i)
\end{equation}
by expanding the linear and angular velocities we get the following expression:\begin{equation}
    T=\frac{1}{2}\dq^T\left(
       \sum_{i=1}^n m_i{J_L^i}^T(\q){J_L^i}(\q)+{J_A^i}^T(\q)I_{ci}J_A^i(\q)
    \right)\dq
\end{equation}
$I_{ci}$ is constant if expressed in the reference frame $RF_{ci}$. Notice how $T$ is a \textit{quadratic form} in $\dq$, the term \begin{equation}
    M(\q)=\sum_{i=1}^n m_i{J_L^i}^T(\q){J_L^i}(\q)+{J_A^i}^T(\q)I_{ci}J_A^i(\q)
\end{equation}
is the \textbf{Generalized Inertia Matrix} of the robot:\begin{equation}
    T=\frac{1}{2}\dq^TM(\q)\dq 
\end{equation}
since $T$ is the kinetic energy, is always non negative, and is null if and only if $\dq$ is null, so, by definition $M(\q)$ is always \textit{positive definite}:\begin{equation}
    M(\q)\succ 0, \ \ \forall \q
\end{equation}
hence, $M(\q)$ is \textbf{always invertible}.\bigskip 

Now that we have given a full discussion about the kinetic energy, we can proceed to the potential energy. Luckily, this computation is much easier, since the potential energy of a single rigid body is given only by his mass and the position of it's center of mass. So, for the $i$-th link we have\begin{equation}
    U_i=-m_i\mathbf g_0^T\mathbf r_{0,ci}
\end{equation}
where $m_i$ is the mass, $\mathbf g_0$ is the gravity vector (depends on where the manipulator is placed) and $\mathbf r_{0,ci}$ is the position of the center of mass of the $i$-th link expressed in the base frame $RF_0$, depend only on $\q$:\begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \mathbf r_{0,ci}\\ 1
    \end{pmatrix}= {}^1A_1(q_1)
    {}^1A_2(q_2)\dots  
    {}^{i-1}A_i(q_i)\begin{pmatrix}
        \mathbf r_{i,ci}\\ 1
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
$\mathbf r_{i,ci}$ is the position of the center of mass expressed in the frame $RF_i$ of the $i$-th link and is constant. The matrices ${}^1A_1(q_1)
    {}^1A_2(q_2)\dots  
    {}^{i-1}A_i(q_i)$ are the homogeneous transformations computed with the DH parameters. Often, we will denote the potential energy of the manipulator just $U(\q)$ without writing the internal structure. The kinetic energy instead can be rewritten also in scalar form:\begin{equation}
        T=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^nm_{ij}(\q)\dot q_i\dot q_j=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j}m_{ij}(\q)\dot q_i\dot q_j
    \end{equation}
    where $m_{ij}$ is an entry of the matrix $M(\q)$. The Lagrangian of the whole system is\begin{equation}
        \mathcal L(\q,\dq)=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j}m_{ij}(\q)\dot q_i\dot q_j-U(\q).
    \end{equation}
    Now we have to write the Euler-Lagrange equations\begin{equation}
    \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot q_k}-\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial q_k}=u_k, \ \ \ 1\le k\le n
\end{equation}
Firstly, we derive the Lagrangian respect to $\dot q_k$:\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot q_k}=\sum_{j=1}^nm_{kj}(\q)\dot q_j
\end{equation}
then, we have to derive this result respect to the time, but $m_{kj}$ depends on $\q$ that depends on time so we apply the chain rule\begin{align}
    &\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot q_k}= \\
    &\sum_{j=1}^nm_{kj}(\q)\ddot q_j+
    \sum_{j=1}^n\left(\frac{\partial m_{kj}}{\partial\q}\dq\right) \dot q_j\end{align}
    $\frac{\partial m_{kj}}{\partial\q}$ is the row vector of the partial derivatives respect each component.
    \begin{align}&\sum_{j=1}^nm_{kj}(\q)\ddot q_j+ \sum_{j=1}^n\left(\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{\partial m_{kj}}{\partial q_i}\dot q_i\right) \dot q_j=\\
    &\sum_{j=1}^nm_{kj}(\q)\ddot q_j+ \sum_{i,j}\frac{\partial m_{kj}}{\partial q_i}\dot q_i\dot q_j
\end{align}
Then we compute the other term of the equation\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial q_k}=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j}\frac{\partial m_{ij}}{\partial q_k}\dot q_i\dot q_j-\frac{\partial U}{\partial q_k}
\end{equation}
The complete $k$-th Euler Lagrange equation is\begin{align}
    &\sum_{j=1}^nm_{kj}(\q)\ddot q_j+ \sum_{i,j}\frac{\partial m_{kj}}{\partial q_i}\dot q_i\dot q_j-\left(\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j}\frac{\partial m_{ij}}{\partial q_k}\dot q_i\dot q_j-\frac{\partial U}{\partial q_k}\right)=u_k\implies\\ 
    &\sum_jm_{kj}\ddot q_j+\sum_{i,j}\left(
        \frac{\partial m_{kj}}{\partial q_i}-\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial m_{ij}}{\partial q_k}
    \right)\dot q_i\dot q_j+\frac{\partial U}{\partial q_k}=u_k
\end{align}
we recall that $u_k$ is the non-conservative generalized force performing work on $q_k$. It is important to notice that, in the Euler-Lagrange equations:\begin{itemize}
    \item the accelerations $\ddq$ appears as \textbf{linear} terms
    \item the velocities $\dq$ appears as \textbf{quadratic} terms
    \item the equation is \textbf{non linear} in the configuration $\q$.
\end{itemize}
So, for each known state $(\q,\dq)$, the system of equations can be inverted and solved for $\ddq$.
\begin{boxdefinition}{Christoffel symbols}{}
    The \textbf{Christoffel symbols} of the first kind associated to the inertia matrix of the robot $M(\q)$ are a set of coefficients indexed by $i,j,k\in \{1\dots n\}$. The Christoffel symbol $c_{kij}$ is\begin{equation}
        c_{kij} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{\partial m_{kj}}{\partial q_i} + \frac{\partial m_{ki}}{\partial q_j} - \frac{\partial m_{ij}}{\partial q_k} \right)
    \end{equation}
    The symbols are symmetric: $c_{kji}=c_{kij}$.
\end{boxdefinition}
Without entering in the details, we exploit the following identity:\begin{equation}
     \sum_{i,j}\frac{\partial m_{kj}}{\partial q_i}-\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial m_{ij}}{\partial q_k}=\sum_{i,j}\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{\partial m_{kj}}{\partial q_i} + \frac{\partial m_{ki}}{\partial q_j} - \frac{\partial m_{ij}}{\partial q_k} \right)
\end{equation}
so is explicit how the Christoffel symbols appears in the $k$-th Euler Lagrange equation:\begin{align}
    &\sum_jm_{kj}\ddot q_j+\sum_{i,j}\frac{1}{2}\left( \frac{\partial m_{kj}}{\partial q_i} + \frac{\partial m_{ki}}{\partial q_j} - \frac{\partial m_{ij}}{\partial q_k} \right)\dot q_i\dot q_j+\frac{\partial U}{\partial q_k}=u_k\implies\\ 
    &\sum_jm_{kj}\ddot q_j+\sum_{i,j}c_{kij}(\q)\dot q_i\dot q_j+\frac{\partial U}{\partial q_k}=u_k
\end{align}
Let's give a physical interpretation:\begin{equation}
    \sapText{\sum_jm_{kj}\ddot q_j}+\greenText{\sum_{i,j}c_{kij}(\q)\dot q_i\dot q_j}+\blueText{\frac{\partial U}{\partial q_k}}=u_k
\end{equation}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The first are the \sapText{ Inertial Terms} 
    \item the second are the \greenText{ Centrifugal and Coriolis Terms}
    \item the third are the \blueText{ Gravity Terms}.
\end{itemize}
The gravitational term on the $k$-th equation is\begin{equation}
    g_k(\q)=-\sum_{i=1}^n m_i\mathbf g_0^T\left(\frac{\partial\mathbf r_{0,ci}}{\partial q_k}\right)
\end{equation}
About the inertia matrix:\begin{itemize}
    \item $m_{kk}(\q)$ is the inertia at joint $k$ when joint $k$ accelerates, is always greater than 0.
    \item $m_kj$ is the inertia ''seen'' at joint $k$ when joint $j$ accelerates, represents the inertia coupling between the two joints.
\end{itemize}
About the Christoffel symbols:\begin{itemize}
    \item $c_{kii}$ is the coefficient of the centrifugal effect at joint $k$ when the joint $i$ is moving. 
    \item $c_{kij}$ is the coefficient of the Coriolis effect at joint $k$ when joint $i$ and joint $j$ are moving.
\end{itemize}
Instead of writing the $n$ Euler-Lagrange equations, we can write the dynamic model in one single vector equations. The inertia terms are simply given by $$ M(\q)\ddq$$
The gravity term is a vector (the gradient of the potential energy)\begin{equation}
    \mathbf g(\q)=\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial \q}\right)^T
=\nabla_\q U
\end{equation}
for the Christoffel symbol, we define a square matrix $C(\q,\dq)$ of order $n$, where the entry $(k,j)$ is defined through the Christoffel symbols:\begin{equation}
    C[k,j]=c_{kj}(\q,\dq)=\sum_{i=1}^nc_{kij}(\q)\dq
\end{equation}
About the notation: The $c$ with two appendices is an entry of the matrix $C(\q,\dq)$, the $c$ with three appendices is a Christoffel symbol. The full dynamic model as a single vector equation is:\begin{equation}\label{dynModel}
    M(\q)\ddq+C(\q,\dq)\dq+\mathbf g(\q)=\mathbf u
    .
\end{equation}
The matrix-vector multiplication $C(\q,\dq)\dq$ is the factorization of a vector $\mathbf c(\q,\dq)$, another general way to write the model is
\begin{equation}
    M(\q)\ddq+\mathbf c(\q,\dq)+\mathbf g(\q)=\mathbf u
\end{equation} 
where the $k$-th component of is defined as follows\begin{align}
     &c_k(\q,\dq)=\dq^TC_k(\q)\dq\\ \label{ChrystoffelMat}
     &C_k(\q)=\frac{1}{2}\left(
        \frac{\partial M_k}{\partial \q}+\left(\frac{\partial M_k}{\partial \q}\right)^T-\frac{\partial M}{\partial q_k}
     \right)
\end{align}
$M_k$ is the $k$-th column vector of the inertia matrix $M(\q)$.\bigskip

On the right side of the equation we have the non conservative terms $\mathbf u$, these forces have to be expressed as components that perform work on the Lagrangian coordinate. Since for a manipulator the coordinates are the joint variables defined according to the DH convention, the terms $\mathbf u$ are expressed as torques. There are generally three kinds of non conservative torques:\begin{itemize}
    \item We denote $\btau$ the torques produced by the motors (actuation). This is usually our control input. 
    \item We denote $\btau_{int}$ the torques produced by the interaction with the environment. Let $\mathbf f$ to be the vector of generalized forces (wrench) that are applied on the end effector.
    \begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth ]{images/wrench.eps}
\end{figure}
   given the Jacobian matrix $J$ relative to the direct kinematics of the end effector pose, the torques on the joints resulting from $\mathbf f$ are\begin{equation}
    \btau_{int}=J^T\mathbf f.
   \end{equation}
   \item The dissipative torques due to the friction are denoted $\btau_B$. 
\end{itemize}
$\btau_B$ always imply a loss of the total energy of the system $\mathcal H=T+U$, while $\btau$ and $\btau_{int}$ may increase or decrease the total energy.
\subsection{Dynamic Model of a PR Robot}
In this section we show how to derive the dynamic model of a simple PR robot. Making the model of such a simple robot explicit is not trivial, this should also be proof of how the explicit definition of the equations of motion for a more complex robot is impractical and often unfeasible.
We analyze the PR planar manipulator:
 \begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth ]{images/PR_3d.eps}
\end{figure}
The first joint is prismatic, to let $d_{c1}$ constant, we define this quantity as the distance between the second joint axis and $\mathbf p_{c1}$, and $d_{c2}$ is defined as the distance between (again) the second joint axis and $\mathbf p_{c2}$. The position of the center of mass of the first link is easy to compute, since is always on the $x_w$ axis, is given by $q_1-d_{c1}$:\begin{equation}
    \mathbf p_{c1}=\begin{pmatrix}
        q_1-d_{c1}\\0\\0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
the derivative is simply
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf v_{c1}=\dot{\mathbf p}_{c1}=\begin{pmatrix}
        \dot q_1\\0\\0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
so\begin{equation}
    \| \mathbf v_{c1}\|^2=\dot q_1^2.
\end{equation}
The first link never rotate, so it's angular velocity $\bomega_1$ is always null, hence the kinetic energy is simply computed (from the König Theorem) as follows:\begin{equation}
    T_1=\frac{1}{2}m_1\dot q_1^2.
\end{equation}
For the second link, we have to compute $ \mathbf v_{c2}=\dot{\mathbf p}_{c2}$. The position of the center of mass is\begin{equation}
    \mathbf p_{c2}=\begin{pmatrix}
        q_1+d_{c2}\cos q_2\\ 
        d_{c2}\sin q_2\\0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
so\begin{equation}
    \mathbf v_{c2}=\dot{\mathbf p}_{c2}=\begin{pmatrix}
        \dot q_1-d_{c2}\sin q_2\dot q_2\\ 
         d_{c2}\cos q_2\dot q_2\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
the squared norm of the velocity is\begin{equation}
    \| \mathbf v_{c2}\|^2=\dot q_1^2+d_{c2}^2\sin^2 q_2\dot q_2^2-2\dot q_1d_{c2}\sin q_2\dot q_2+d^2_{c2}\cos^2 q_2\dot q^2_2=\dot q_1^2+d_{c2}^2\dot q_2^2-2d_{c2}\sin q_2\dot q_1\dot q_2
\end{equation}
so the linear part of $T_2$ is\begin{equation}
    \frac{1}{2}m_2(\dot q_1^2+d_{c2}^2\dot q_2^2-2d_{c2}\sin q_2\dot q_1\dot q_2)
\end{equation}
the angular part is given by\begin{equation}
    \frac{1}{2}\bomega^T_2 I_{c2}\bomega_2
\end{equation}
where $I_{c2}$ is the inertia matrix relative to the center of mass. The angular velocity of the second link is easy to compute, since it rotate on the $z_w$ according to $\dot q_2$ so:\begin{equation}
    \bomega_2=\begin{pmatrix}
        0\\0\\ \dot q_2
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
due to the two zeros the term $\bomega^T_2 I_{c2}\bomega_2$ will include only the bottom right element of the matrix:\begin{equation}
    \bomega^T_2 I_{c2}\bomega_2=
    \begin{pmatrix}
        0&0& \dot q_2
    \end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
       I_{c2,xx}&I_{c2,xy}&I_{c2,xz}\\
       I_{c2,yx}&I_{c2,yy}&I_{c2,yz}\\
       I_{c2,zx}&I_{c2,zy}&I_{c2,zz}\\
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        0\\0\\ \dot q_2
    \end{pmatrix}=I_{c2,zz}\dot q_2^2
\end{equation}
the kinetic energy of the second link is\begin{equation}
    T_2=\frac{1}{2}m_2(\dot q_1^2+d_{c2}^2\dot q_2^2-2d_{c2}\sin q_2\dot q_1\dot q_2)+\frac{1}{2}I_{c2,zz}\dot q_2^2
\end{equation}
We assume that the robot is on the horizontal plane so it does not feel the gravity: $U=0$. The Lagrangian is\begin{equation}
    \mathcal L(\q,\dq)=T-U=T=\frac{1}{2}m_1\dot q_1^2+\frac{1}{2}m_2(\dot q_1^2+d_{c2}^2\dot q_2^2-2d_{c2}\sin q_2\dot q_1\dot q_2)+\frac{1}{2}I_{c2,zz}\dot q_2^2.
\end{equation}
to compute $M(\q)$ we have to factorize $T$ since $T=\frac{1}{2}\dq^TM(\q)\dq$:\begin{align}
    &\dot q_1^2\frac{1}{2}\left(
        m_1+m_2
    \right)+\dot q_2^2\frac{1}{2}\left(
        I_{c2,zz}+ m_2d_{c2}^2
    \right)+\dot q_1\dot q_2\left(-m_2d_{c2}\sin q_2\right)
    \implies\\ &T=\frac{1}{2}\dq^T\begin{pmatrix}
        m_1+m_2 & -m_2d_{c2}\sin q_2\\ 
       -m_2d_{c2}\sin q_2& m_2d_{c2}^2+I_{c2,zz}
    \end{pmatrix}\dq
\end{align}
so\begin{equation}
    M(\q)=M(q_2)=\begin{pmatrix}
        m_1+m_2 & -m_2d_{c2}\sin q_2\\ 
       -m_2d_{c2}\sin q_2& m_2d_{c2}^2+I_{c2,zz}
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Notice how the variable $q_1$ does not appear, in fact, $M$ is only in function of $q_2$, de facto this is a \textbf{general property}. \begin{boxlemma}{}{}
The inertia matrix $M(\q)$ never depend on $q_1$: The inertia matrix represents the mass distribution seen from the base reference system. By rotating the first joint ($q_1$), you rotate the entire robot around its main axis. Since the geometry of the subsequent arms with respect to the first axis does not change, the kinetic energy of the system remains the same regardless of the value of $q_1$.
\end{boxlemma}
Another general property is the following.\begin{boxlemma}{}{}
    For the inertia matrix $M(\q)$, the bottom-right term $m_{nn}$ is \textbf{always constant}. The element $m_{nn}$ reflects the inertia of the last link (the final element or effector) with respect to its axis of rotation. Since after the last joint there are no other joints that can move masses and change the geometric configuration of the terminal, the mass distribution with respect to the $n$ axis remains fixed.
\end{boxlemma}
Now, given $M$, we can compute the centrifugal and Coriolis terms. The vector \begin{equation}
    \mathbf c(\q,\dq)=\begin{pmatrix}
        c_1(\q,\dq)\\ 
        c_2(\q,\dq)
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
have its component $c_k$ defined through a matrix $C_k$ as in equation \eqref{ChrystoffelMat}. Given\begin{equation}
    M_1=\begin{pmatrix}
        m_1+m_2 \\ 
       -m_2d_{c2}\sin q_2
    \end{pmatrix}, \ \ M_2=\begin{pmatrix}
      -m_2d_{c2}\sin q_2\\ 
      m_2d_{c2}^2+I_{c2,zz}
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
we compute\begin{align}
    &\frac{\partial M_1}{\partial \q}=\begin{pmatrix}
        0&0\\  
        0&-m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2
    \end{pmatrix}\\
     &\frac{\partial M}{\partial q_1}=\begin{pmatrix}
        0&0\\  
        0&0
    \end{pmatrix}\\
     &\frac{\partial M_2}{\partial \q}=\begin{pmatrix}
        0&-m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2\\  
        0&0
    \end{pmatrix}\\
     &\frac{\partial M}{\partial \q_2}=\begin{pmatrix}
        0 & -m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2\\ 
       -m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2& 0
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{align}
So\begin{align}
    &C_1=\frac{1}{2}\left(
        \begin{pmatrix}
        0&0\\  
        0&-m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2
    \end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}
        0&0\\  
        0&-m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2
    \end{pmatrix}-\begin{pmatrix}
        0&0\\  
        0&0
    \end{pmatrix}
    \right)=\\ &\begin{pmatrix}
        0&0\\  
        0&-m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2
    \end{pmatrix}\implies  
    c_1=
    \begin{pmatrix}
       \dot  q_1&\dot q_2
    \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
        0&0\\  
        0&-m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2
    \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
       \dot  q_1\\\dot q_2
    \end{pmatrix}=-m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2\dot q_2^2
\end{align}
and\begin{align}
    &C_2=\frac{1}{2}\left(
        \begin{pmatrix}
        0&-m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2\\  
        0&0
    \end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}
        0&0\\  
        -m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2&0
    \end{pmatrix}-\begin{pmatrix}
        0 & -m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2\\ 
       -m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2& 0
    \end{pmatrix}
    \right)=\\
    & \begin{pmatrix}
        0&0\\  
        0&0
    \end{pmatrix}\implies c_2=0.
\end{align}
The full model is\begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        m_1+m_2 & -m_2d_{c2}\sin q_2\\ 
       -m_2d_{c2}\sin q_2& m_2d_{c2}^2+I_{c2,zz}
    \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
        \ddot q_1\\ \ddot q_2
    \end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}
       -m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2\dot q_2^2 \\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        u_1\\ u_2
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
We want to answer to the following question\begin{quotation}
    Starting at rest $\q=\dq=\mathbf 0$, if we apply a torque on the first joint $u_1>0$ and zero torque on the second joint, the latter will it move?
\end{quotation}
To answer, we isolate $\ddq$:\begin{align}
    &\ddq=M^{-1}[\mathbf u-\mathbf c]
\end{align}
if $\q=\dq=\mathbf 0$ then $\mathbf c=\mathbf 0$:\begin{align}
    &\ddq=M^{-1}\mathbf u=\\ 
    &\frac{1}{\det M}\begin{pmatrix}
       m_2d_{c2}^2+I_{c2,zz} & m_2d_{c2}\sin q_2\\ 
       m_2d_{c2}\sin q_2& m_1+m_2
    \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
        u_1\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}=\\ 
    &\frac{u_1}{\det M}\begin{pmatrix}
       m_2d_{c2}^2+I_{c2,zz}\\ 
      m_2d_{c2}\sin q_2
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{align}
it will accelerate at $\frac{u_1}{\det M}( m_2d_{c2}\sin q_2)$ $\frac{\text{deg}}{\text{s}^2}$, will remain stationary if and only if $q_2=0$ or $q_2=\pi$.\bigskip

Now that we have the model we want to solve the inverse dynamics. The first desired trajectory is\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        q_{1d}(t)=k \text{ constant}\\ 
        q_{2d}(t)=\alpha t\text{ with }\alpha >0
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
we compute the desired velocity and acceleration:\begin{align}
    &\dot q_{1d}=\ddot q_{1d}=0\\ 
    &\dot q_{2d}=\alpha, \ \ \ddot q_{2d}=0
\end{align}
then we substitute these trajectory in the model:\begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        m_1+m_2 & -m_2d_{c2}\sin (\alpha t)\\ 
       -m_2d_{c2}\sin (\alpha t)& m_2d_{c2}^2+I_{c2,zz}
    \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
       0\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}
       -m_2d_{c2}\cos (\alpha t)\alpha^2 \\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        u_1\\ u_2
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        u_1(t)=-m_2d_{c2}\cos(\alpha t)\alpha^2 \\ 
        u_2(t)=0
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
understanding the dynamics is not that trivial: We need to impose a torque only on $q_1$ to make $q_2$ to moves, and let $q_1$ still. The non zero torque $u_1\ne 0$ compensate the centrifugal effect of $q_2$. Let's see another example:\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        q_{1d}(t)=A(1-\cos(\omega t))\\ 
        q_{2d}(t)=\frac{\pi}{2}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
hence 
\begin{align}
    &\dot q_{1d}=A\omega\sin(\omega t), \ \ \dot q_{1d}=A\omega^2\cos(\omega t)\\ 
    &\dot q_{2d}=\ddot q_{2d}=0
\end{align}
in this case
\begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        m_1+m_2 & -m_2d_{c2}\\ 
       -m_2d_{c2}& m_2d_{c2}^2+I_{c2,zz}
    \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
        A\omega^2\cos(\omega t)\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}
       0 \\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        u_1\\ u_2
    \end{pmatrix}\implies
\end{equation}\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        u_1=A\omega^2\cos(\omega t)(m_1+m_2)\\ 
        u_2=A\omega^2\cos(\omega t)(-m_2d_{c2})
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
the frequency of the commands is identical but the direction is inverted.\subsection{Factorization and Energy Conservation}
Now, we compute the factorization of $\mathbf c$ through a matrix $C$ in order to write the model in the form of equation \eqref{dynModel}.\begin{align}
    &\mathbf c = \begin{pmatrix}
        -m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2\dot q_2^2\\ 
        0
    \end{pmatrix} =\begin{pmatrix}
        0&-m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2\dot q_2\\ 
        0&0
    \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
        \dot q_1 \\ 
        \dot q_2
    \end{pmatrix}\implies \\ &C=\begin{pmatrix}
        0&-m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2\dot q_2\\ 
        0&0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{align}
this factorization is the one that satisfy the property of skew-symmetry for the matrix $\dot M-2C$, there exists an infinite number of factorizations, but these ones doesn't necessarily satisfies the property, for example\begin{equation}
    N=\begin{pmatrix}
        0&-m_2d_{c2}\cos q_2\dot q_2\\ 
        -\dot q_2&\dot q_1
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
\begin{boxtheorem}{Skew Symmetry of $\dot M-2C$}{}
    Let $C(\q,\dq)$ to be a factorization of the centrifugal and Coriolis terms of the dynamic model: $$\mathbf c(\q,\dq) = C(\q,\dq)\dq $$
    if $C(\q,\dq)$ is the matrix constructed through the Christoffel symbols: 
    $$ C[k,j]=c_{kj}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}c_{kij}(\q)\dq$$
    then $\dot M-2C$ is skew symmetric.
\end{boxtheorem}
\textit{Proof}: The $(k,j)$-th entry of $\dot M$ is\begin{equation}
    \dot m_{kj}=\sum{i=1}{n}\frac{\partial m_{kj}}{\partial q_i}\dot q_i
\end{equation}
and the $(k,j)$-th entry of $2C$ is\begin{equation}
    2c_{kj}=2\sum_{i=1}^{n}c_{kij}(\q)\dot q_i=\sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( \frac{\partial m_{kj}}{\partial q_i} + \frac{\partial m_{ki}}{\partial q_j} - \frac{\partial m_{ij}}{\partial q_k} \right)\dot q_i
\end{equation}
so the $(k,j)$-th entry of $\dot M-2C$ is\begin{equation}
     \dot m_{kj}- 2c_{kj}=\sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(  \frac{\partial m_{ij}}{\partial q_k} - \frac{\partial m_{ki}}{\partial q_j} \right)\dot q_i = s_{kj}
\end{equation}
We denoted $s_{kj}$ this term. From this, it follows that $s_{kj}$, the $(j,k)$-th entry of $\dot M-2C$, is 
\begin{equation}
     s_{jk}=\dot m_{jk}- 2c_{jk}=\sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(  \frac{\partial m_{ik}}{\partial q_j} - \frac{\partial m_{ji}}{\partial q_k} \right)\dot q_i 
\end{equation}
since $M$ is symmetric, we can swap the indices:
\begin{equation}
     s_{jk}=\dot m_{jk}- 2c_{jk}=\sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(  \frac{\partial m_{ki}}{\partial q_j} - \frac{\partial m_{ij}}{\partial q_k} \right)\dot q_i = -\sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(-  \frac{\partial m_{ki}}{\partial q_j} + \frac{\partial m_{ij}}{\partial q_k} \right)\dot q_i
\end{equation}
but this is exactly $-s_{kj}$, so\begin{equation}
    s_{jk}=-s_{kj}.
\end{equation}
\hfill$\blacksquare$\bigskip

\noindent
A weaker property than skew symmetry is:\begin{equation}
    \mathbf x^T(\dot M-2C)\mathbf x=\mathbf 0, \ \ \forall \mathbf x\in \R^n
\end{equation}
and holds for any factorization $N\mathbf c$
\begin{equation}\label{conditionEnergyCons}
    \mathbf x^T(\dot M-2N)\mathbf x=\mathbf 0, \ \ \forall \mathbf x\in \R^n.
\end{equation}
This relation explains the \textbf{conservation of the total energy}: Let $\mathcal H$ to be the total energy of a manipulator\begin{equation}
    \mathcal H=T+U=\frac{1}{2}\dq^T M(\q)\dq+U(\q) 
\end{equation}
the evolution over time of the energy is\begin{equation}\label{EnergyEvolution}
    \dot{\mathcal H}=\dq^TM(\q)\ddq+\frac{1}{2}\dq^T\dot M(\q)\dq+\frac{\partial U}{\partial \q}\dq
\end{equation}
From equation \eqref{dynModel}, the dynamic model\begin{equation}\label{dynModel}
    M(\q)\ddq+N(\q,\dq)\dq+\mathbf g(\q)=\mathbf u
\end{equation}
can be reorganized\begin{equation}\label{reorganization}
     M(\q)\ddq=\mathbf u-N(\q,\dq)\dq-\mathbf g(\q)
\end{equation}
by recalling that $\mathbf g=\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial \q}\right)^T$ and by putting together \eqref{EnergyEvolution} and \eqref{reorganization} we get:\begin{align}
    &\dot{\mathcal H}=
    \dq^T(\mathbf u-N(\q,\dq)\dq-\mathbf g(\q))+\frac{1}{2}\dq^T\dot M(\q)\dq+\dq^T\mathbf g(\q)\implies \\
   & \dot{\mathcal H}=\dq^T\mathbf u+\frac{1}{2}\dq^T(\dot M-2N)\dq
\end{align}
but since the relation \eqref{conditionEnergyCons} holds:\begin{equation}
    \dot{\mathcal H}=\dq^T\mathbf u
\end{equation}
When $\mathbf u=\mathbf 0$ (no dissipation or increase) the \textit{total energy is constant}, instead, when $\mathbf u\ne \mathbf 0$ the \textbf{variation of total energy} is equal to the \textit{work done by non conservative forces}. For example, all the non conservative forces  relative to  friction can be expressed in the following form\begin{equation}
    \mathbf u = -K_D\dq 
\end{equation}
where $K_D$ is a diagonal positive definite matrix. In this case, if we have only friction terms:\begin{equation}
        \dot{\mathcal H}=-\dq^TK_D\dq 
\end{equation}
due to the properties of $K_D$, $\dq^TK_D\dq >0$, hence:
\begin{equation}
        \dot{\mathcal H}=-\dq^TK_D\dq <0
\end{equation}
there is always a loss of energy. If there is no friction and we have only torque commands   $\btau$:
\begin{equation}
        \dot{\mathcal H}=\dq^T\btau
\end{equation}
in this case the torque $\tau_i$ on joint $q_i$ will add energy on the system if and only if:\begin{equation}
    \text{sign}(\tau_i)=\text{sign}(\dot q_i).
\end{equation}

    &k_0\le \|M(\q)\|\le k_1+k_2\|\q\|+k_3\|\q\|^2\\ 
    &\|S(\q,\dq)\|\le (k_4+k_5\|\q\|)\|\dq\|\\ 
    &\|\mathbf g(\q)\|\le k_6+k_7\|\q\|
\end{align}
$S(\q,\dq)$ is the matrix of a factorization of the centrifugal/Coriolis terms. If all the joins are revolute, these three conditions simplify to:\begin{align}
    &k_0\le \|M(\q)\|\le k_1\\ 
    &\|S(\q,\dq)\|\le k_4\|\dq\|\\ 
    &|\mathbf g(\q)\|\le k_6
\end{align}
in these relations we are computing the norm of the matrix $M$ and $S$. There are various ways to define the norm of a matrix, in this case, given the norm for a vector $\|\cdot \|$, the \textbf{induced norm} of a matrix $A$ is defined as follows:\begin{equation}
    \|A\|=\sup_{\mathbf x\in \R^n\backslash\{\mathbf 0\}}\frac{\|A\mathbf x\|}{\|\mathbf x\|}
\end{equation}
if we consider the L2 norm $\|\mathbf x\|_2=\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2}$, the induced norm of a matrix is given by their maximum singular value:\begin{equation}
    \|A\|_2=\sigma_{\max}(A)=\sqrt{\lambda_{\max}(A^TA)}
\end{equation}
here, $\lambda_{\max}(A^TA)$ is the maximum eigenvalue of $A^TA$. For symmetric and positive definite matrices like the inertia matrix $M$, the norm is easier to compute since it is just the highest eigenvalue:\begin{equation}
    \|M\|_2=\lambda_{\max}(M).
\end{equation}

\section{Computing the Kinetic Energy}
The complexity of the dynamic model and the number of terms involved grows more than exponentially with the degrees of freedom, it is almost impossible to derive the full inertia matrix of a 6R manipulator by hand.

In this section, a recursive method to compute symbolically the inertia matrix $M(\q)$ is presented, this matrix can characterize the full model since $\mathbf c(\q,\dq)$ is derived by $M(\q)$ and the gravity term $\mathbf g(\q)$ is easy to define. The method aims to compute recursively $\mathbf v_{ci}$ and $\bomega_{i}$ starting from the base toward the end effector considering all the link. We use the notation $^{i}\mathbf  u$ to denote the point $\mathbf u$ expressed in $RF_i$.

Recall that the vector $\mathbf r_{ci}$, that identifies the position of the center of mass of link $i$, is always constant in the reference frame $RF_i$. The algorithm take advantage of the following rigid bodies relation:\begin{equation}\label{vel_rigid_bodies}
    \mathbf v_{ci}=\mathbf v_i+\bomega_i\times\mathbf r_{ci}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf v_i$ identifies the velocity of the reference frame $RF_i$. We need an indicator $\sigma_i$ that discriminates the prismatic joints:\begin{equation}
    \sigma_i=\begin{cases}
        0\text{ if joint $i$ is revolute}\\
        1\text{ if joint $i$ is prismatic}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
The method use the relations of differential kinematics, to compute the quantities at each step, we need the transformations between the moving frames, we denote $$^{i-1}R_i(q_i) $$
the $3\times 3$ rotation matrix between the frame $i-1$ to the frame $i$:\begin{equation}
    ^{i-1}\mathbf u= ^{i-1}R_i(q_i)  ^{i}\mathbf u.
\end{equation}
Now we proceeds to describe the recursive steps. We need to compute ${}^i\bomega_i$ and ${}^i\mathbf v_{i}$ and we have already computed ${}^{i-1}\bomega_{i-1}$ and ${}^{i-1}\mathbf v_{i-1}$. We compute ${}^i\bomega_i$ as follows:
\begin{boxequation*}{Recursive Step for $ {}^i\bomega_i$}{}
\begin{equation}\label{bomega_i}
    {}^i\bomega_i=^{i-1}R^T_i(q_i)[{}^{i-1}\bomega_{i-1}+(1-\sigma_i)\dot q_i {}^{i-1}\mathbf z_{i-1}].
\end{equation}
\end{boxequation*}
First of all, ${}^{i-1}\mathbf z_{i-1}$ is always $\begin{pmatrix}
    0&0&1
\end{pmatrix}^T$, since the angular velocities sum up, ${}^{i-1}\bomega_{i-1}+(1-\sigma_i)\dot q_i {}^{i-1}\mathbf z_{i-1}$ gives the angular velocity of link $i$  in the reference frame $RF_{i-1}$\begin{itemize}
    \item if $\sigma_i=0$ and the joint is revolute, the two angular velocities are added together 
    \item if $\sigma_i=1$ and the joint is prismatic, there is no additional angular velocity produced by this joint.
\end{itemize}
 \begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth ]{images/computeKineticStep1.eps}
\end{figure}
${}^i\bomega_i$ is always $\dot q_i{}^i\mathbf z_i$ since the revolute joint contributes in the angular velocity only along its axis. 
Then, $ {}^i\mathbf v_i$ is computed using  ${}^i\bomega_i$ as follows:
\begin{boxequation*}{Recursive Step for $ {}^i\mathbf v_i$}{}
\begin{equation}\label{v_i}
    {}^i\mathbf v_i=^{i-1}R^T_i(q_i)[
         {}^{i-1}\mathbf v_{i-1}+\sigma_i\dot q_i {}^{i-1}\mathbf z_{i-1}+
         ^{i-1}\bomega_{i}\times ^{i-1}\mathbf r_{i-1,i}
    ].
\end{equation}
\end{boxequation*}
Let's analyze each component\begin{itemize}
    \item $\sigma_i\dot q_i ^{i-1}\mathbf z_{i-1}$ is the linear velocity contribution given by the prismatic joint, in fact is non zero if and only if $\sigma_i=1$
    \item $^{i-1}\bomega_{i}\times ^{i-1}\mathbf r_{i-1,i}$ is the linear (tangential) velocity given by the rotation of the previous joint. $\mathbf r_{i-1,i}$ is the vector going from $RF_{i-1}$ to $RF_i$.
    \begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth ]{images/computeKineticStep2.eps}
\end{figure}
\end{itemize}
After computing ${}^i\bomega_i$, ${}^i\mathbf v_i$ for all $i$, we compute ${}^i\mathbf v_{ci}$ using the relation \eqref{vel_rigid_bodies}, and from the König theorem we can compute the kinetic energy of the system (from which the inertia matrix is derived).
\subsection{Dynamic Model of a 2R Robot}
We give an example of the algorithm at work by considering a planar 2R manipulator, with link length $l_1$ and $l_2$.
\begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.55\textwidth ]{images/dynamic2R.eps}
\end{figure}
We assume that the robot is moving on the vertical plane so the gravity vector is\begin{equation}
    \mathbf g=\begin{pmatrix}
        0\\-g_0\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
the rotation matrices between the frames of this simple manipulator are\begin{equation}
    {}^{i-1}R_i(q_i)=\begin{pmatrix}
        \cos(q_i)&-\sin(q_i)&0\\ 
        \sin(q_i)&\cos(q_i)&0\\ 
        0&0&1
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The center of mass of link $i$ in reference frame $RF_i$ is always on the $\mathbf x_i$ axis simply given by\begin{equation}
    {}^i\mathbf r_{ci}=\begin{pmatrix}
        -l_i+d_{ci}\\ 0\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Since the method is recursive, it needs an initialization for the first step at $i=0$:\begin{equation}
    {}^0\bomega_0={}^0\mathbf v_0=\mathbf 0.
\end{equation}
As the first step, we compute  ${}^1\bomega_1$ as described in equation \eqref{bomega_i}:\begin{align}
    &{}^1\bomega_1=^{0}R^T_1(q_1)[{}^{0}\bomega_{0}+(1-\sigma_1)\dot q_1 {}^{0}\mathbf z_{0}]
    \\ 
    &=\begin{pmatrix}
        \cos(q_1)&\sin(q_1)&0\\ 
        -\sin(q_1)&\cos(q_1)&0\\ 
        0&0&1
    \end{pmatrix}\left[
        \mathbf 0 + \dot q_1\begin{pmatrix}
            0\\0\\1
        \end{pmatrix}
    \right]=\begin{pmatrix}
            0\\0\\\dot q_1
        \end{pmatrix}
\end{align}
then, as described in equation \eqref{v_i}, we compute ${}^1\mathbf v_1$:\begin{align}
    &{}^1\mathbf v_1=^{0}R^T_1(q_1)[
         {}^{0}\mathbf v_{0}+\sigma_i\dot q_1 {}^{0}\mathbf z_{0}+
         ^{0}\bomega_{1}\times ^{0}\mathbf r_{0,1}
    ]\\ 
    &=
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \cos(q_1)&\sin(q_1)&0\\ 
        -\sin(q_1)&\cos(q_1)&0\\ 
        0&0&1
    \end{pmatrix}\left[
       \mathbf 0+
        \begin{pmatrix}
            0\\0\\\dot q_1
        \end{pmatrix}\times \begin{pmatrix}
            l_1\cos(q_1)\\l_1\sin(q_1)\\ 0
        \end{pmatrix}
    \right]=\begin{pmatrix}
           0\\l_1\dot q_1\\ 0
        \end{pmatrix}
\end{align}
we have all the ingredients to compute ${}^1\mathbf v_{c1}$ using the relation \eqref{vel_rigid_bodies}:\begin{equation}
    {}^1\mathbf v_{c1}=\begin{pmatrix}
           0\\l_1\dot q_1\\ 0
        \end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}
            0\\0\\\dot q_1
        \end{pmatrix}\times \begin{pmatrix}
        -l_1+d_{c1}\\ 0\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        0\\ d_{c1}\dot q_1\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
the kinetic energy of the first link is\begin{align}
    &T_1=
    \frac{1}{2}m_1{}^1\mathbf v_{c1}^T{}^1\mathbf v_{c1}+\frac{1}{2}{}^1\bomega_1^TI_{c1}{}^1\bomega_1\\
    &=\frac{1}{2}m_1 d^2_{c1}\dot q^2_1+
    \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}
            0&0&\dot q_1
        \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
       I_{c1,xx}&I_{c1,xy}&I_{c1,xz}\\
       I_{c1,yx}&I_{c1,yy}&I_{c1,yz}\\
       I_{c1,zx}&I_{c1,zy}&I_{c1,zz}\\
    \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
            0\\0\\\dot q_1
        \end{pmatrix}\\ 
        &=
        \frac{1}{2}m_1 d^2_{c1}\dot q^2_1+
        \frac{1}{2}I_{c1,zz}\dot q_1^2=\frac{1}{2}(I_{c1,zz}+m_1 d^2_{c1})\dot q^2_1.
\end{align}
Now, we proceed to the second step with $i=2$, by computing ${}^2\bomega_2$:\begin{align}
    &{}^2\bomega_2=^{1}R^T_2(q_2)[{}^{1}\bomega_{1}+(1-\sigma_2)\dot q_2 {}^{1}\mathbf z_{1}]\\
    &=\begin{pmatrix}
        \cos(q_2)&\sin(q_2)&0\\ 
        -\sin(q_2)&\cos(q_2)&0\\ 
        0&0&1
    \end{pmatrix}
    \left[
        \begin{pmatrix}
            0\\0\\\dot q_1
        \end{pmatrix}+\dot q_2 \begin{pmatrix}
            0\\0\\1
        \end{pmatrix}\right]= \begin{pmatrix}
            0\\0\\\dot q_1+\dot q_2
        \end{pmatrix}
\end{align}
and then ${}^2\mathbf v_{2}$\begin{align}
    &{}^2\mathbf v_2=^{1}R^T_2(q_2)[
         {}^{1}\mathbf v_{1}+\sigma_2\dot q_2 {}^{1}\mathbf z_{1}+
         ^{1}\bomega_{2}\times ^{1}\mathbf r_{1,2}
    ]\\ 
    &=\begin{pmatrix}
        \cos(q_2)&\sin(q_2)&0\\ 
        -\sin(q_2)&\cos(q_2)&0\\ 
        0&0&1
    \end{pmatrix}
    \left[
        \begin{pmatrix}
           0\\l_1\dot q_1\\ 0
        \end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}
            0\\0\\\dot q_1+\dot q_2
        \end{pmatrix}\times \begin{pmatrix}
            l_2\cos( q_2)\\ 
            l_2\sin (q_2)\\ 0
        \end{pmatrix}
    \right]\\
    &=\begin{pmatrix}
        l_1\sin(q_2)\dot q_1\\l_1\cos(q_2)\dot q_1\\0
    \end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}
        0\\0\\\dot q_1+\dot q_2
    \end{pmatrix}\times \begin{pmatrix}
        l_2\\0\\0
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        l_1\sin (q_2)\dot q_1\\l_1\cos(q_2)\dot q_1 + l_2(\dot q_1+\dot q_2)
        \\0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{align}
the velocity of the center of mass of the second link is\begin{align}
    &{}^2\mathbf v_{c2}=\begin{pmatrix}
        l_1\sin (q_2)\dot q_1\\l_1\cos(q_2)\dot q_1 + l_2(\dot q_1+\dot q_2)
        \\0
    \end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}
        0\\0\\\dot q_1+\dot q_2
    \end{pmatrix}\times \begin{pmatrix}
        -l_2+d_{c2}\\ 0\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}\\
    &=\begin{pmatrix}
        l_1\sin(q_2)\dot q_1\\ 
        l_1\cos(q_2)\dot q_1+d_{c2}(\dot q_1+\dot q_2)
        \\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{align}
the kinetic energy of the second link is\begin{equation}
    T_2=\frac{1}{2}m_2\big ( l_1^2\dot q_1^2+d_{c2}^2(\dot q_1+\dot q_2)^2+
    2l_1d_{c2}\cos(q_2)\dot q_1(\dot q_1+\dot q_2)\big)+\frac{1}{2}I_{c2,zz}(\dot q_1+\dot q_2)^2.
\end{equation}
The kinetic energy of the whole system is\begin{equation}
    T=T_1+T_2=\dq^TM(\q)\dq 
\end{equation}
without entering in the detailed steps of the factorization, we give directly the elements $m_{ij}$ of the matrix $M$:\begin{equation}
    m_{11}(\q)=I_{c1,zz} + m_1 d_{c1}^2 + I_{c2,zz} + m_2 d_{c2}^2 + m_2 l_1^2 + 2m_2 l_1 d_{c2} \cos(q_2)
\end{equation}
it is convenient to regroup the constant terms and denote it with a single coefficient:
\begin{align}
   & m_{11}(\q)=\rho_1 + 2\rho_2 \cos q_2\\ 
   &\rho_1=I_{c1,zz} + m_1 d_{c1}^2 + I_{c2,zz} + m_2 d_{c2}^2 + m_2 l_1^2\\ 
   &\rho_2=m_2l_1d_{c2}
\end{align}
\begin{boxdefinition}{Dynamic Coefficients}{}
    The dynamic coefficients $\rho_i$ are coefficients that group constant terms in a dynamic model (such as mass or joint length). They are useful for making the inertia matrix more readable, and are considered in the context of linear parametrization.
\end{boxdefinition}
Then we have\begin{align}
    &m_{12}(\q)=m_{21}(\q)=I_{c2,zz} + m_2 d_{c2}^2 + m_2 l_1 d_{c2} \cos q_2\implies \\
    &m_{12}(\q)=m_{21}(\q)=\rho_3+\rho_2\cos q_2\\
    &\rho_3=I_{c2,zz} + m_2 d_{c2}^2\\
    &m_{22}(\q)=I_{c2,zz} + m_2 d_{c2}^2=\rho_3.
\end{align}
The inertia matrix is rewritten in the following form:\begin{equation}
    M(\q)=\begin{pmatrix}
        \rho_1 + 2\rho_2 \cos q_2&\rho_3+\rho_2\cos q_2\\ 
        \rho_3+\rho_2\cos q_2&\rho_3
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
notice how it doesn't depend on $q_1$. Now that we have the symbolic expression of $M$, we can compute the centrifugal and Coriolis terms:\begin{align}
    &C_1(\q)=\frac{1}{2}\left(
        \frac{\partial M_1}{\partial \q}+\left(
         \frac{\partial M_1}{\partial \q}
    \right)^T+ \frac{\partial M}{\partial \q_1}
    \right)=\begin{pmatrix}
        0&-\rho_2\sin q_2\\
        -\rho_2\sin q_2&-\rho_2\sin q_2
    \end{pmatrix}\\ 
    &\implies \mathbf c_1(\q,\dq)=\dq^T C_1(\q)\dq=-\rho_2\sin q_2(\dot q_2^2+2\dot q_1\dot q_2)\\[1.5ex]
    &C_2(\q)=\frac{1}{2}\left(
        \frac{\partial M_2}{\partial \q}+\left(
         \frac{\partial M_2}{\partial \q}
    \right)^T+ \frac{\partial M}{\partial \q_2}
    \right)=\begin{pmatrix}
       \rho_2\sin q_2&0\\ 0&0
    \end{pmatrix}\\ 
    &\implies \mathbf c_2(\q,\dq)=\dq^T C_2(\q)\dq=\rho_2\sin q_2\dot q_1^2
\end{align}
Now, we have to compute the gravity terms. The potential energy of the links is\begin{align}
    &U_1=-m_1\mathbf g^T\mathbf r_{0,c1}=m_1g_0d_{c1}\sin q_1\\ 
    &U_2=-m_2\mathbf g^T\mathbf r_{0,c2}=m_2g_0(l_1\sin q_1+d_{c2}\sin(q_1+q_2))
\end{align}
now we have to compute the gradient of $U=U_1+U_2$:\begin{align}
    &\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial \q}\right)^T=\begin{pmatrix} g_0(m_1 d_{c1} \cos(q_1)+ m_2 l_1 \cos(q_1) + m_2 d_{c2} \cos(q_1+q_2)) \\ g_0 m_2 d_{c2} \cos(q_1+q_2) \end{pmatrix}\\ & =\begin{pmatrix}
    \rho_4\cos q_1+\rho_5 \cos (q_1+q_2)\\ \rho_5 \cos (q_1+q_2)
    \end{pmatrix}\end{align}where\begin{align} 
    &\rho_4=g_0(m_1 d_{c1}+ m_2 l_1)\\ 
    &\rho_5= g_0 m_2 d_{c2}.
\end{align}
The full dynamic model of the 2R robot is\begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \rho_1 + 2\rho_2 \cos q_2&\rho_3+\rho_2\cos q_2\\ 
        \rho_3+\rho_2\cos q_2&\rho_3
    \end{pmatrix}\ddq+\begin{pmatrix}
        -\rho\sin q_2(\dot q_2^2+2\dot q_1\dot q_2)\\ 
        \rho_2\sin q_2\dot q_1^2
    \end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}
    \rho_4\cos q_1+\rho_5 \cos (q_1+q_2)\\ \rho_5 \cos (q_1+q_2)
    \end{pmatrix}=\mathbf u
\end{equation}
we rewrite it in scalar form as a system of two equations:\begin{align}
&(\rho_1 + 2\rho_2 c_2)\ddot{q}_1 + (\rho_2 c_2 + \rho_3)\ddot{q}_2 - \rho_2 s_2(\dot{q}_2^2 + 2\dot{q}_1 \dot{q}_2)
+ \rho_4 c_1 + \rho_5 c_{12} = u_1 \\
&(\rho_2 c_2 + \rho_3)\ddot{q}_1 + \rho_3 \ddot{q}_2 + \rho_2 s_2 \dot{q}_1^2 + \rho_5 c_{12} = u_2
\end{align}
we used the notation $c_1=\cos(q_1)$, $c_{12}=\cos(q_1+q_2)$ etc\dots

The values of $\rho_i$ depends on the dynamic parameters, the construction of the mechanical structure of the manipulator can take place in such a way as to cancel some of these parameters and make the dynamic model simpler, for example, if $\rho_2$ is null, the model became: 
\begin{align}
&\rho_1\ddot{q}_1 + \rho_3\ddot{q}_2 -  2\dot{q}_1 \dot{q}_2
+ \rho_4 c_1 + \rho_5 c_{12} = u_1 \\
&\rho_3\ddot{q}_1 + \rho_3 \ddot{q}_2  + \rho_5 c_{12} = u_2
\end{align}
is it possible to make $\rho_2=0$? $\rho_2$ is equal to $m_2l_2d_{c2}$, $m_2$ is the mass of the second link so it can't be zero, $l_2$ is the link length and can't be zero too. $d_{c2}$ is the distance between the joint axis of link two and the center of mass of the second link: \textbf{It can be zero} if the center of mass is exactly on the joint axis, this condition can be satisfied by a link construction that adequately distributes the weight (for example, adding a counterweight on the other side of the link).

\end{figure}
\section{Closed Kinematic Chain}

In some cases, the gravity term in the dynamic model of a manipulator is identically zero, for example, if the motion of the robot is on an horizontal plane, making the potential energy $U$ constant, or for manipulators placed in remote space where the gravity can be considered absent. There are other cases when the term $\frac{\partial U}{\partial \q}$ is null, such for \textit{closed kinematic chain}.

\begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth ]{images/closed_chain.png}
\end{figure}
In this section we analyze a particular closed kinematic chain manipulator, a robot with a \textit{parallel structure}. Observe the figure \ref{parallel}.
\begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth ]{images/parallel_new.drawio.pdf}
     \caption{Parallel structure manipulator, 3D view.}
     \label{parallel}
\end{figure}
The green joints are active and actuated, the grey one are passive, hence, despite the 5 total joints, the system have two degrees of freedom, and the end effector always lies on the $(x,y)$ plane. The planar view of the manipulator is the following:
\begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth ]{images/parallel_2.eps}
     \caption{Parallel structure manipulator, planar view.}
\end{figure}
The coordinate of the system are respectively $q_1$ as the angle between the first link and the $x$ axis and $q_2$ as the angle between the second link and the $x$ axis. Notice how the angle between the fifth link and the $x$ axis is exactly $q_2-\pi$. The direct kinematics for the position of the end effector on the $(x,y)$ plane is \begin{equation}
    \mathbf p_{EE}=k(\q)=\begin{pmatrix}
        l_1\cos q_1\\ 
        l_1\sin q_1
    \end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}
        l_5\cos(q_2-\pi)\\ 
        l_5\sin(q_2-\pi)
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        l_1\cos q_1-l_5\cos q_2\\ 
        l_1\sin q_1-l_5\sin q_2
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
Now we consider the centers of mass of the links:
\begin{figure}[H]    
\centering 
     \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth ]{images/parallel_3.eps}
\end{figure}
The direct kinematics for the centers of mass is easy to compute (from now we use the short hand notation for the trigonometric terms):

\begin{align}
    &\mathbf p_{c1}=\begin{pmatrix}
        l_{c1}c_1\\ 
        l_{c1}s_1
    \end{pmatrix}\\ 
    &\mathbf p_{c2}=\begin{pmatrix}
        l_{c2}c_2\\ 
        l_{c2}s_2
    \end{pmatrix}\\ 
    &\mathbf p_{c3}=\begin{pmatrix}
        l_{2}c_2+l_{c3}c_1\\ 
        l_{2}s_2+l_{c3}s_1
    \end{pmatrix}\\ 
    &\mathbf p_{c4}=\begin{pmatrix}
        l_1c_1-l_{c4}c_2\\ 
        l_1s_1-l_{c4}s_2
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{align}
The fourth and fifth link are considered as a single rigid body and they always have the same angular velocity. The angular velocity of the first link is $\bomega_1=(0,0,\dot q_1)^T$. The third link is ''constrained'' and always rotate with the first one, so $\bomega_3=\bomega_1$. The same reasoning applies for the second and fourth link:\begin{equation}
    \bomega_2=\bomega_4=(0,0,\dot q_2)^T.
\end{equation}
The velocity of the centers of mass is obtained by differentiating $\mathbf p_{ci}$:
    \begin{align}
    &\mathbf v_{c1}=\dot q_1\begin{pmatrix}
        -l_{c1}s_1\\ 
        l_{c1}c_1
    \end{pmatrix}\\ 
    &\mathbf v_{c2}=\dot q_2\begin{pmatrix}
       - l_{c2}s_2\\ 
        l_{c2}c_2
    \end{pmatrix}\\ 
    &\mathbf v_{c3}=\begin{pmatrix}
        -\dot q_2l_{2}s_2-\dot q_1l_{c3}s_1\\ 
        \dot q_2l_{2}c_2+\dot q_1l_{c3}c_1
    \end{pmatrix}\\ 
    &\mathbf v_{c4}=\begin{pmatrix}
        -\dot q_1l_1s_1+\dot q_2l_{c4}s_2\\ 
        \dot q_1l_1c_1-\dot q_2l_{c4}c_2
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{align}
We have all the ingredients to compute the kinetic energy for each link, we denote $I_{ci,zz}$ the bottom right entry of the inertia matrix $I_{ci}$ of the $i$-th link respect its center of mass:\begin{align}
    &T_1=\frac{1}{2}m_1l_{c1}^2\dot q_1^2+\frac{1}{2}I_{c1,zz}\dot q_1^2\\[1.5ex]
    &T_2=\frac{1}{2}m_2l_{c2}^2\dot q_2^2+\frac{1}{2}I_{c2,zz}\dot q_2^2\\[1.5ex]
    &T_3 = \frac{1}{2} m_3 (l_2^2 \dot{q}_2^2 + l_{c3}^2 \dot{q}_1^2 + 2l_2 l_{c3} c_{2-1} \dot{q}_1 \dot{q}_2) + \frac{1}{2} I_{c3,zz} \dot{q}_1^2 \\[1.5ex]
    &T_4 = \frac{1}{2} m_4 (l_1^2 \dot{q}_1^2 + l_{c4}^2 \dot{q}_2^2 - 2l_1 l_{c4} c_{2-1} \dot{q}_1 \dot{q}_2) + \frac{1}{2} I_{c4,zz} \dot{q}_2^2
\end{align}
the kinetic energy of the whole system is $T=\frac{1}{2}\dq^TM(\q)\dq$ where\begin{equation}
    M(\q)=\begin{pmatrix}
I_{c1,zz} + m_1 l_{c1}^2 + I_{c3,zz} + m_3 l_{c3}^2 + m_4 l_1^2 & (m_3 l_2 l_{c3} - m_4 l_1 l_{c4}) c_{2-1}  \\[2ex]
(m_3 l_2 l_{c3} - m_4 l_1 l_{c4}) c_{2-1} & I_{c2,zz} + m_2 l_{c2}^2 + I_{c4,zz} + m_4 l_{c4}^2 + m_3 l_2^2
\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
The diagonal of this matrix is constant, notice how, we can design the mechanical structure of the manipulator in order to accomplish the following equality:\begin{equation}
    m_3l_2l_{c3}=m_4l_1l_{c4}
\end{equation}
if so, the inertia matrix becomes \textbf{diagonal and constant}:
\begin{equation}
    M=\begin{pmatrix}
    m_{11} & 0  \\
    0 & m_{22}
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
this is another example of how the mechanical design can simplify the dynamic model and leading to a \textbf{big advantage} for the \textbf{design of motion control laws}, in fact, since the matrix is constant, the centrifugal and Coriolis terms $\mathbf c(\q,\dq)$ are identically zero (they are computed through the partial derivatives of the columns in $M$), and the dynamic model simplifies in\begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
    m_{11} & 0  \\
    0 & m_{22}
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
    \ddot q_1\\ 
    \ddot q_2
    \end{pmatrix}
    +\mathbf g(\q)=\begin{pmatrix}
    u_1\\ u_2
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
if the robot is placed on an horizontal plane and $U$ is constant, then:\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        m_{11}\ddot q_1=u_1\\ 
         m_{22}\ddot q_2=u_2
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
the dynamic model is \textbf{mechanically decoupled} and \textbf{linear}.

Now, let's take in account the gravity, directed on the $y$ axis. The potential energy of each component can be easily computed by considering the $y$ coordinate of the position of center of mass:\begin{align}
    &U_1=m_1g_0l_{c1}s_1\\ 
    &U_2=m_2g_0l_{c2}s_2\\ 
    &U_3=m_3g_0(l_{2}s_2+l_{c3}s_1)\\
    &U_4=m_4g_0( l_1s_1-l_{c4}s_2)
\end{align}
given $U=\sum U_i$, the gravity contribution in the dynamic model is\begin{equation}
    \mathbf g(\q)=\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial \q}\right)^T=\begin{pmatrix}
        g_0(m_1l_{c1}+m_3l_{c3}+m_4l_1)c_1\\
        g_0(m_2l_{c2}+m_3l_{2}-m_4l_{c4})c_2
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
      g_1(q_1)\\ g_2(q_2)
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
these gravity components are decoupled, the model becomes\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        m_{11}\ddot q_1+g_1(q_1)=u_1\\ 
         m_{22}\ddot q_2+g_2(q_2)=u_2
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
notice how we can still impose additional mechanical conditions on the structure to nullify the gravity terms, we have to satisfy:\begin{align}
   & m_1l_{c1}+m_3l_{c3}+m_4l_1=0\\
   & m_2l_{c2}+m_3l_{2}-m_4l_{c4}=0
\end{align}
with the previous condition, the choice of the dynamics parameters have to satisfy the system of three equations:\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        m_1l_{c1}+m_3l_{c3}+m_4l_1=0\\
        m_2l_{c2}+m_3l_{2}-m_4l_{c4}=0\\
        m_3l_2l_{c3}-m_4l_1l_{c4}=0
    \end{cases}.
\end{equation}
