\chapter{Introduction}
\section{An Overview in Dynamics}
Previously, in the \href{https://github.com/CasuFrost/university-notes-AIRO/raw/main/Robotics%201/LatexSourceFile/Robotics1.pdf}{Robotics 1} course, we described the kinematic behavior of a robotic manipulator, assuming that no force are involved, and that we can control directly the angular velocity of each joint, without any kind of resistance.
We consider the following example\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth ]{images/no_dyn_example.eps}
\end{center}
it is clear that, considering the positive angular velocity $v>0$, in the next configuration the second link will rotate counter clock wise, while the first joint remain fixed. If we have to take in account the dynamics, we should also consider\begin{itemize}
    \item the center of mass of the first and the second link
    \item the mass of the first and the second link
    \item the inertia the first and the second link
\end{itemize}
and instead of describing the control trough angular velocities on the two joints, we define the torques $\tau_1,\tau_2$ applied on them. In this case (we ignore the effects of gravity), by applying a zero torque $\tau_1=0$ on the first joint, and a positive torque $\tau_2>0$ on the second joint, both joints will move, according with the laws of rigid body dynamics.\bigskip

Let's consider the following example of a 2R planar robot, with the joint axis orthogonal respect to the direction of gravity.
\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth ]{images/2R_example.eps}
\end{center}
where $I_1,I_2$ are the moment of inertia of the links around their center of mass, $d_{c_{1}},d_{c_{2}}$ is the distance along the links between the joint and the center of mass, $m_1,m_2$ are the mass of the links, and $l_1,l_2$ the lengths. The initial configuration at $t=0$ is \begin{equation}
    \mathbf q_0 = \begin{pmatrix}
        0\\\frac{\pi}{2}
    \end{pmatrix} \ \ \ \  
    \dq_0 = \begin{pmatrix}
        0\\0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
Consequently, also $\dot {\mathbf p}=\mathbf 0$ (the velocity of the effector end). We want to know, what will be $\ddot {\mathbf q}$ (and consequently $\ddot {\mathbf p}$) in the initial configuration at $t=0$, by considering the dynamics and the forces. The Jacobian matrix of this simple manipulator is\begin{equation}
    J=\begin{pmatrix}
        -l_1s_1-l_2s_{12} & -l_2s_{12}\\ 
        l_1c_1+l_2c_{12} & l_2c_{12}
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
in the initial configuration $\mathbf q(0)=\mathbf q_0$ we have\begin{equation}
    J(\mathbf q_0)=\begin{pmatrix}
        -l_2&-l_2\\ 
        l_1&0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
from the differential relation\begin{equation}
    \ddot{\mathbf p}=J\ddot{\mathbf q}+\dot J\dq
\end{equation}
since $\dq_0=\mathbf 0$
\begin{equation}
    \ddot{\mathbf p_0}=\begin{pmatrix}
        -l_2&-l_2\\ 
        l_1&0
    \end{pmatrix}\ddot{\mathbf q_0}
\end{equation}
we know that, by finding the initial joint acceleration $\ddot{\mathbf q_0}$ we can predict the next configuration and where the end effector will move. The full dynamic model is a system of differential equation and is the following\begin{equation}
    M(\mathbf q)\ddot{\mathbf q}+\mathbf c(\mathbf q,\dq)+\mathbf g(\mathbf q)=\mathbf 0
\end{equation} 
where $M$ is the  $2\times 2$ inertia matrix, and depends only on the current configuration, $\mathbf c$ is a term representing the centrifugal and coriolis forces, and depends also on the velocities $\dq$. The last term $\mathbf g$ is the gravity term, and depends on the current configuration. We are not explaining in details how the model is derived, since this is an introductory section, and all these topics will be described and expanded in the following chapters.  

Since the robot is at rest, the $\mathbf c$ vector is null, so\begin{equation}
    \ddot{\mathbf q}=M^{-1}(\mathbf q)\mathbf g(\mathbf q)
\end{equation}
the inertia matrix in the initial configuration (that for now we get for granted) is\begin{equation}
    M(\mathbf q_0)=\begin{pmatrix}
        I_1+m_1d_{c_1}^2+I_2+m_2d_{c_2}^2+m_2l_1^2 &I_2+m_2d_{c_2}^2\\ 
        I_2+m_2d_{c_2}^2&I_2+m_2d_{c_2}^2
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
\begin{boxtheorem}{Steiner}{}
    If $I$ is the moment of inertia around an axis of a rigid body, and $d$ is the distance between this axis and the center of mass, the inertia around the parallel axis passing trough the center of mass will be $\bar I = I+md^2$, where $m$ is the mass of the body.
\end{boxtheorem}
In the inertia matrix we see the terms $I_i+m_id_{c_i}^2$, that is exactly the moment of inertia around the parallel axis passing trough the center of mass, that we denote $\bar I_i$.
\begin{equation}
    M(\mathbf q_0)=\begin{pmatrix}
        \bar I_1+\bar I_2+m_2l_1^2 &\bar I_2\\ 
       \bar I_2&\bar I_2
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
This inertia matrix is \textit{always} positive definite, so it is invertible, in particular\begin{align}
    &M^{-1}(\mathbf q_0)=\frac{1}{\det M(\mathbf q_0)}\begin{pmatrix}
        \bar I_2&-\bar I_2\\ 
       -\bar I_2&\bar I_1+\bar I_2+m_2l_1^2 
    \end{pmatrix}=\\ &\frac{1}{(\bar I_1+m_2l_1^2)\bar I_2}\begin{pmatrix}
        \bar I_2&-\bar I_2\\ 
       -\bar I_2&\bar I_1+\bar I_2+m_2l_1^2 
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{align}
the gravity term is the following\begin{equation}
    \mathbf g(\mathbf q_0)=\begin{pmatrix}
        m_1d_{c_1}g_0+m_2l_1g_0\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        (m_1d_{c_1}+m_2l_1)g_0\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
the first component $ m_1d_{c_1}g_0+m_2l_1g_0$ represents the total gravitational torque acting on the first joint.\begin{itemize}
    \item $m_1 d_{c_1} g_0$: This is the torque generated by the weight of the first link.
    \item $m_2 l_1 g_0$: This is the torque generated by the weight of the second link acting at the end of the first link.
\end{itemize}
due to the vertical alignment of the initial configuration, the gravity does not exerts any torque on the second joint. By putting together the equations we get:\begin{equation}
    \ddot{\mathbf q_0}=\frac{(m_1d_{c_1}+m_2l_1)g_0}{(\bar I_1+m_2l_1^2)\bar I_2}\begin{pmatrix}
        -\bar I_2\\ \bar I_2
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        \star<0\\ \star > 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
so the first joint will rotate clockwise while the second counter clockwise. We want to know if the end effector will fall to the left or to the right. We compute \begin{align}
    &\ddot{\mathbf p_0}=\begin{pmatrix}
        -l_2&-l_2\\ 
        l_1&0
    \end{pmatrix}\ddot{\mathbf q_0}=\\ 
    &\ddot{\mathbf p_0}=\begin{pmatrix}
        -l_2&-l_2\\ 
        l_1&0
    \end{pmatrix}\frac{(m_1d_{c_1}+m_2l_1)g_0}{(\bar I_1+m_2l_1^2)\bar I_2}\begin{pmatrix}
        -\bar I_2\\ \bar I_2
    \end{pmatrix}=\\ 
    &\ddot{\mathbf{p}}_0 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ -\frac{l_1 (m_1 d_{c_1} + m_2 l_1)g_0}{\bar{I}_1 + m_2 l_1^2} \end{pmatrix}
\end{align}
so the end effector will accelerato downward without moving on the $x$ axis.
\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.27\textwidth ]{images/2R_example2.eps}
\end{center}
Now, we consider a simpler example from a dynamic point of view, but we will show the process to derive the dynamic model. We consider a planar 2P robot, where the joint's are orthogonal, in this simple case the Jacobian matrix is the identity. For this manipulator there will be no angular terms, since all the joint's are prismatic, we still refers with torques to the linear forces $\tau_1,\tau_2$ applied on the prismatic joints.\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.35\textwidth ]{images/2P_example.eps}
\end{center}
We consider two different methods to derive the dynamic model (both these methods we will further explained in details in the their relative sections).
\subsubsection{Newton-Euler}
This methods perform a balance of forces acting on the system of rigid bodies. We start by the last link, backtracking towards the first. The forces acting on the second link are the torque $\tau_2$ (the commands) and the gravity, so the dynamic equation for the second link (according to the Newton laws) is:\begin{equation}
    \tau_2-m_2g_0=m_2\ddot q_2
\end{equation}
on the first link, there is no force exerts from the gravity (since this link is constrained to move only along the $x$ axis), so is affected only by the command torque $\tau_1$ and the inertia of the second link (which opposes its motion):\begin{equation}
    \tau_1-m_2\ddot q_1=m_1\ddot q_1
\end{equation}
by putting the two equations together we obtain the following system\begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        m_1m_2&0\\ 0&m_2
    \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
        \ddot q_1\\ \ddot q_2
    \end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}
        0\\ m_2g_0
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        \tau_1\\\tau_2
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
the first matrix $M$ is the inertia matrix while the vector $\begin{pmatrix}
        0\\ m_2g_0
    \end{pmatrix}$ is the gravity term.
\subsubsection{Euler-Lagrange}
This methods is based on the energy of the system. The kinetic energy $T$ is given by the sum of the kinetic energies of the two links $T_1+T_2$, the same applies for the potential energy $U=U_1+U_2$ (that in this case, is given by the gravity field). The kinetic energy of the first link (that can move only along the $x$ axis) is\begin{equation}
    T_1=\frac{1}{2}m_1\dot q_1^2 
\end{equation}
since the second link can move in both directions, and their velocity is given by $\mathbf v_{c_2}=\begin{pmatrix}
    \dot q_1\\ \dot q_2
\end{pmatrix}$, the kinetic energy is\begin{equation}
    T_2=\frac{1}{2}m_2\|\mathbf v_{c_2}\|^2=\frac{1}{2}m_2(\dot q_1^2+ \dot q_2^2)
\end{equation}
the potential energy of the first link is constant, since it can't move along the $y$ axis, we denote this constant energy just $U_1$. The potential energy of the second link, given by the gravity field, is $U_2=m_2g_0q_2$.\bigskip 

The \textit{Lagrangian} is a function describing the dynamics of the system (will be explained in the next chapters), is given by the difference between the kinetic and the potential energy\begin{equation}
    \mathcal L=T-U
\end{equation}
depends on the current configuration $\mathbf q$ and the velocity $\dq$. In this case we have\begin{equation}
    \mathcal L(\mathbf q,\dq)=\frac{1}{2}m_1\dot q_1^2 +\frac{1}{2}m_2(\dot q_1^2+ \dot q_2^2)-U_1-m_2g_0q_2
\end{equation}
there is a simple principle in Physics, the trajectories of the system (the evolution over time of $\mathbf q$ and $\dq$) are the one that \textit{minimizes the action over time}, that is the following scalar function\begin{equation}
    \mathcal S=\int_0^t \mathcal L(\mathbf q,\dq)dt
\end{equation}
this is a problem of Calculus of Variations (the minimization of a functional over a set of functions) and is proved to be solved by the trajectories that satisfies the \textbf{Euler-Lagrange Equations}:\begin{equation}
    \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot q_i}-\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial q_i}=\tau_i, \ \ \ 1\le i\le n
\end{equation}
for our 2P robotic system, we have the two following equations\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        (m_1+m_2)\ddot q_1=\tau_1\\ 
        m_2\ddot q_2+m_2g_0=\tau_2
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
That fully describes the model.
\section{Planning vs Feedback Control}
We review some concepts already explored in the course of Robotics 1 about the differences between planning and feedback control. We recall that a good controller in every robotics application, should incorporate the offline planning procedure with an online closed loop system.\bigskip

We take as an example a simple body with mass $m$ that lies on a plane with zero friction. This body can move only along the direction parallel to the plane, and it's position is defined by the variable $x$, and it is applied an horizontal force $F$, the equation that describes the dynamics of the object is of course $F=m\ddot x$.

We want to plan a motion, starting from the initial condition \begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        x\\\dot x
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        0\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
and reaching the final condition  \begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        x\\\dot x
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        x_d\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
We can plan the motion by integrating over time $\ddot x = \frac{F}{m}=u$. We already seen the simple bang-bang profile, in that case, the displacement in position reached in $T$ seconds by applying a command input of amplitude $u$ is \begin{equation}
    x=\frac{T^2}{4}u
\end{equation}
if $u$ is limited by $F_{max}$ the minimum time to reach $x=x_d$ starting from $x=0$ is\begin{equation}
    T=\sqrt{\frac{4 m x_d}{F_{max}}}
\end{equation}
where $F_{max}=mu_{max}$.
\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth ]{images/bang_bang.eps}
\end{center}
This approach execute the desired motion in nominal condition in the minimum time, but it requires some information about the dynamical model (in this case, the value of $m$). Of course, in real situations, there are always some disturbance forces that we can't control or predict, so this approach will eventually leads to a configuration that is not exactly the desired one, in some cases, with an high error.

The feedback approach consists in using the state variables in the commands, it doesn't necessary requires the dynamic model, but some sensors to measure the position, velocity, and other quantities. In this simple example, the general approach is the following\begin{equation}
    m\ddot x = u(x,\dot x)
\end{equation}
where $u$ is the control rule. We consider the following PD law:\begin{equation}
    m\ddot x=k_P(x_d-x)-k_D \dot x
\end{equation}
the system is still linear. We track the error and it's derivatives\begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        e=x_d-x\\ 
        \dot e = -\dot x\\ 
        \ddot e = -\ddot x
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
We express the dynamics in term of the errors and we get the second order equation:\begin{equation}
    m\ddot e+k_Pe+k_D\dot e = 0
\end{equation}
this homogeneous equation can be solved analytically by knowing $m,k_P,k_D$ and the initial condition. The value of $e(t)$ converges to zero if $m,k_D$ and $k_P$ have the same sign, since $m$ is the mass (and is always positive), it is sufficient to choose positive values for $k_P,k_D$. This law works even if there are some disturbance, we can combine the feed forward action with the feedback loop by choosing a force command\begin{equation}
    F=F_f+k_P(x_f-x)+k_D \dot (\dot x_f-\dot x)
\end{equation}
where $F_f(t)$ realizes the desired trajectory in nominal condition and $x_f(t)$ is the evolution over time of $x$ in the nominal condition.\bigskip

This simple PD control law doesn't work in situations where the system is subject to undamped persistent forces. Let's consider a body of mass $m$ subject to the gravity, that can move only along the axis orthogonal to the ground. We call $y$ the position of the body, and we can apply a force $F$ as the command input. The dynamic model is described by the equation\begin{equation}
    m\ddot y = F-mg_0.
\end{equation}
If we choose a PD control law for $F$ the dynamic equation is \begin{equation}
    m\ddot y = k_P(y_d-y)-k_D\dot y-mg_0.
\end{equation}
where $y_d$ is the target position and 0 is the target velocity. The error is $e=y_d-y$, the temporal evolution of the error is described by the equation\begin{equation}
     k_Pe+k_D\dot e+m\ddot e -mg_0=0
\end{equation}
Will $e(t)$ converge to zero? Let's decide some values for the data just to give an example, let $m=k_P=k_D=1$.
\begin{equation}
   e+\dot e+\ddot e -9.8=0
\end{equation}
if the initial conditions are $e(0)=y_d, \ \dot e(0)=0$ the solution is 
\begin{equation}
    e(t) = \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}t\right) \left[ (y_d - 9.8) \cos\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}t\right) + \frac{y_d - 9.8}{\sqrt{3}} \sin\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}t\right) \right] + 9.8
\end{equation}
since $\lim_{t\rightarrow \infty}\exp(-\frac{1}{2}t)=0$ we have \begin{equation}
    \lim_{t\rightarrow \infty} e(t)=9.8\ne 0 
\end{equation}
so the desired state will be never reached. It is sufficient to add an integral term in the control law by making it a PID controller:\begin{equation}
    k_I\int ed\tau
\end{equation}
the final dynamic equation with the inclusion of this term is \begin{equation}
    k_Pe+k_D\dot e+k_I\int_0^t ed\tau+m\ddot e -mg_0=0
\end{equation}

\chapter{Advanced Topics on Kinematics}
\section{Calibration}
A measurement system have an high repeatability if different measurements of the same quantity provides similar results, and is accurate if the measured quantity is near to the ground truth. A robot can be seen as a measurement system, the DH parameters are used in the direct kinematics to compute the task vector from a given configuration. If the predicted position of the end effector is far from the real position, the parameters are wrong, there is a lack in accuracy and the \textbf{calibration procedure} is needed. 

For simplicity, we consider a manipulator with $n$ (all revolut) joints. The  DH parameters are\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\alpha }= \begin{pmatrix}
        \alpha_1\\\vdots \\\alpha_n
    \end{pmatrix}, \ \ 
    \mathbf a=\begin{pmatrix}
        a_1\\\vdots \\a_n
    \end{pmatrix}, \ \ 
    \mathbf d=\begin{pmatrix}
        d_1\\\vdots \\d_n
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
from these parameters we can compute the \textit{nominal} direct kinematics\begin{equation}
    \mathbf y_{e,nom}=k_e(\boldsymbol\alpha,\mathbf a,\mathbf d,\btheta)
\end{equation}
$\btheta$ is the set of joint angles measured by the encoders. The calibration procedure is essential to have a perfect direct kinematics, especially in the industrial context. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item due to the tolerances in the mechanical construction and in assembly of the links and the joints, there is a small errors in the actual end effector pose respect to the one given by the direct kinematics.
    \item the errors distributed along the are are amplified, due due to
the open kinematic chain structure of most robots
    \item the goal of the calibration procedure is to recover as much as possible this end effector position error by adjusting the DH parameters.
\end{itemize}
The calibration procedure needs a sampling operations, where the robot is set in different configuration, when an external device measure the real end effector pose, in such way we can compare this ground truth with the pose predicted by the direct kinematics.

Let $\mathbf y_e$ to be the real end effector pose measured by the external system, and $\mathbf y_{e,nom}$ the one given by the direct kinematics, we consider the error\begin{equation}
    \Delta \mathbf y=\mathbf y_e-\mathbf y_{e,nom}
\end{equation}
from the first order Taylor expansion of the direct kinematics, we can write the following linear relation
\begin{equation}
    \Delta \mathbf y=
    \frac{\partial k_e}{\partial\boldsymbol \alpha}\Delta\boldsymbol \alpha+
     \frac{\partial k_e}{\partial\mathbf a}\Delta\mathbf a+
     \frac{\partial k_e}{\partial\mathbf d}\Delta\mathbf d+
     \frac{\partial k_e}{\partial \btheta}\Delta\btheta
\end{equation}
The small vectors $\Delta\boldsymbol \alpha,\Delta\mathbf a,\Delta\mathbf d,\Delta\btheta$ are the small errors in the DH parameters. The relation can be rewritten in matrix form\begin{equation}
    \Delta\mathbf y_e=\Phi\Delta\boldsymbol\varphi
\end{equation}
where\begin{align}
    &\Phi=\begin{pmatrix}\displaystyle
         \frac{\partial k_e}{\partial\boldsymbol \alpha}&
         \displaystyle\frac{\partial k_e}{\partial\mathbf a}&
     \displaystyle\frac{\partial k_e}{\partial\mathbf d}&
     \displaystyle\frac{\partial k_e}{\partial \btheta}
    \end{pmatrix}\in Mat(6\times 4n)\\
    &\Delta\boldsymbol\varphi=\begin{pmatrix}
        \Delta\boldsymbol \alpha\\ 
        \Delta\mathbf a\\ 
        \Delta\mathbf d\\
        \Delta\btheta
    \end{pmatrix}\in \R^{4n}
\end{align}
notice how $ \Delta \mathbf y$ is given by the measurement, $\Phi$ is evaluated with the nominal parameters and $\Delta\boldsymbol\varphi$ is the unknown error in the DH parameters. 

For example, with a 2R planar robot, where the direct kinematics is \begin{equation}
    k(\mathbf a,\btheta)=\begin{pmatrix}
        a_1\cos\theta_1+a_2\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2)\\ 
        a_1\sin\theta_1+a_2\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2)
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
if the we assume that the parameters $\mathbf \alpha,\mathbf d$ are correct, the $\Phi$ matrix to consider is\begin{equation}
    \Phi=\begin{pmatrix}
        \displaystyle\frac{\partial k}{\partial \mathbf a}&   \displaystyle\frac{\partial k}{\partial \btheta}
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        c_1&c_{12}&-a_1s_2-a_2s_{12}&-a_2s_{12}\\ 
        s_1&s_{12}&a_1c_1+a_2c_{12}&a_2c_{12}
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
Instead of considering one simple point to measure the error, one can sample $l>>n$ points, to get $l$ matrices $\Phi_i$ and $l$ cartesian errors $\Delta\mathbf y_{e,i}$. In this way we can construct the matrix\begin{equation}
    \overline \Phi = \begin{pmatrix}
        \Phi_1 \\ \vdots \\ \Phi_l
    \end{pmatrix}\in Mat(6l\times 4n)
\end{equation}
and the vector \begin{equation}
        \overline{\Delta\mathbf y}_e=\begin{pmatrix}
            \Delta\mathbf y_{e,1} \\ \vdots \\ 
            \Delta\mathbf y_{e,l}
        \end{pmatrix}\in \R^{6l}
\end{equation}
the system of equation to solve in order to find the errors in the DH parameters is \begin{equation}\label{CalSys}
    \overline \Phi \Delta\boldsymbol\varphi= \overline{\Delta \mathbf y}_e 
\end{equation}
$\overline\Phi$ is called \textbf{regressor matrix}. The system in \eqref{CalSys} is an overdetermined system of equations, and can be solved by considering the pseudo inverse of $\overline\Phi$\begin{align}
    &\Delta\boldsymbol{\varphi}=\overline\Phi^{\#}\overline{\Delta \mathbf y}_e \\
    & \overline\Phi^{\#}=(\Phi^T\Phi)^{-1}\Phi^T
\end{align}
Instead of finding the pseudo inverse, an iterative algorithm can be applied to find $\Delta\boldsymbol\varphi$ that considers sequentially the measurements.\bigskip

\section{Kinematic Redundancy}
The direct kinematics $\mathbf y=k(\mathbf q)$ is the map between the task space and the configuration space\begin{equation}
     k:\mathcal C\rightarrow\mathcal T
\end{equation}
we usually denote $n=\dim\mathcal C$ the dimension of the configuration space and $m=\dim\mathcal T$ the dimension of the task space. A robot is \textbf{kinematically redundant} if $n>m$ (there are more degrees of freedom than strictly needed for executing the task), and $n-m$ is the \textbf{redundancy degree}. 
The redundancy of a robot is a relative concept, doesn't depend on the number of joints, but on the number of dimension of the task space, a 6R robot is not redundant for the task of positioning and orientating the end effector, but is redundant for the task of just positioning.

A redundant robot have an infinite number of solution for the inverse kinematics in the regular case, the redundancy can be used in various ways, for example\begin{itemize}
    \item avoid collision with obstacles in the cartesian space 
    \item avoid kinematic singularities in the joint space 
    \item increase manipulability in specified directions
    \item uniformly distribute/limit joint velocities and/or accelerations
    \item optimize energy consumption or needed motion torques
    \item \dots
\end{itemize}\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth ]{images/redundancy.eps}
\end{center}
We define a \textbf{self motion} of a redundant robot, a motion in the joint space (arm reconfiguration) that doesn't change the task vector $\mathbf y$. In a given configuration, one may perform a self motion to get to a configuration that improve/optimize in
some way the behavior of the robotic system.

The solution to the inverse kinematics to perform a self motion are mainly sought at differential level, by considering the Jacobian matrix, due to the linearity, we recall that the Jacobian is the linear mapping between the tangent spaces of $\mathcal C$ and $\mathcal T$.\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth ]{images/tangent_space2.eps}
\end{center}
In practice, the configuration that are in the self motion on a given position $\mathbf p$ satisfies\begin{equation}
    \mathbf q \in \mathcal C \  : \ k(\mathbf q)=\mathbf p
\end{equation}
the following image represents the points on the configuration space of a planar 2R that are mapped on the same task vector.\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth ]{images/selfmotion.png}
\end{center}

Without entering in the details (for now), the \textit{general redundancy resolution} is an optimization problem that can be solved offline (globally) or online (locally). In both methods, the objective function is a general function of the state space $H(\mathbf q,\dq)$, and the goal is to find a trajectory for $\mathbf q(t)$ and $\dq(t)$, thus, redundancy resolution is a calculus of variations problem.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Global methods}: Given a desired trajectory $\mathbf y(t)$ and an initial configuration $\mathbf q(t_0)$, to find an evolution over $[t_0,t_0+T]$, is involved a functional of the type\begin{equation}
     \int_{t_0}^{t_0+T}H(\mathbf q,\dq)dt   
    \end{equation}
    that have to be minimized (or maximized) over $\mathbf q(t), t\in [t_0,t_0+T]$. This problem is usually too difficult to solve. 
    \item \textbf{Local methods}: Usually at each time step $t=kT_s$ ($T_s$ is the sampling time) given a task velocity $\dot{\mathbf y}$ to realize we solve the problem of finding the joint velocity $\dq$ that realizes such $\dot{\mathbf y}$ while optimizing a given function $H(\mathbf q,\dq)$. Then the discrete step of update is \begin{equation}
        \mathbf q((k+1)T_s)=\mathbf q(kT_s)+T_s\dq(kT_s)
    \end{equation}
    this is more easy to solve and usually requires the resolution of a linear quadratic problem.
\end{itemize}
There are three classes of methods to solve $J\dq=\dot{\mathbf y}$:\begin{itemize}
    \item Jacobian-based: Among the infinite solutions, the one that minimizes a suitable norm is chosen 
    \item null-space methods: a term is added to the previous solution so as not to affect execution
of the task trajectory, i.e., belonging to the null-space $\mathcal N(J)$
    \item task augmentation: redundancy is reduced/eliminated by adding $s\le n-m$ further auxiliary tasks to make the problem ''squared''.
\end{itemize}
\subsection{Jacobian Based Methods}
We have to solve the redundant problem from a differential level\begin{equation}
    J\dq=\dot{\mathbf y}
\end{equation}
where $J$ have $m$ rows and $n$ columns, with $n>m$. We will consider solutions of the form\begin{equation}
    \dq = K\dot{\mathbf y}
\end{equation}
where $K$ is a matrix with $n$ rows and $m$ columns. We want $K$ to satisfy the following condition\begin{equation}
    JKJ=J
\end{equation}
such a matrix is called a \textbf{generalized inverse} of $J$ (depends on the current configuration $\mathbf q$), there are infinite matrix that satisfies that condition. A generalized inverse can realize the desired task velocity $\dot{\mathbf y}$ if it is in the range of $J$.\begin{align}
    \forall \dot{\mathbf y}\in\mathcal R(J) \ : \ J(K\dot{\mathbf y})=JKJ\dq=J\dq=\dot{\mathbf y}
\end{align}
An example of a generalized inverse is the \textit{right inverse}, let $J$ to be a Jacobian of the form\begin{equation}
    J=\begin{pmatrix}
        J_a&J_b
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
where $\det J_a\ne0$, then, the right inverse is \begin{equation}
    K_r=\begin{pmatrix}
        J_a^{-1}\\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
this is equivalent to ''freezing'' some degrees of freedom. \bigskip 

Another example of a generalized inverse is the \textbf{pseudo inverse matrix}, that is the matrix $J^\#$ that satisfies the four following properties:\begin{align}
    &JJ^\#J=J\\ 
    &J^\#JJ^\#=J^\#\\ 
    &(JJ^\#)^T=JJ^\#\\ 
    &(J^\#J)^T=J^\#J
\end{align}
if the matrix $J$ is full rank, and $n>m$, then the pseudo inverse can be computed in closed form:\begin{equation}
    J^\#=J^T(JJ^T)^{-1}.
\end{equation}
\textbf{Example}: if $$J=\begin{pmatrix}
    1&2&3
\end{pmatrix} $$
the pseudo inverse is 
$$ 
\begin{pmatrix}
    1\\2\\3
\end{pmatrix}\left(
\begin{pmatrix}
    1&2&3
\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
    1\\2\\3
\end{pmatrix}
\right)^{-1}=\frac{1}{14}\begin{pmatrix}
    1\\2\\3
\end{pmatrix}.
$$
\textbf{Example}: Now we consider a square singular matrix. First of all, let $J_\epsilon$ to be the following parametrized matrix
$$J_\epsilon=\begin{pmatrix}
    3&0\\ 0&\epsilon
\end{pmatrix}
$$
we know that the inverse of this matrix is$$
    J_\epsilon^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix}
        \frac{1}{3}&0\\0&\frac{1}{\epsilon}
    \end{pmatrix}
$$
with $\epsilon\rightarrow 0$ (the matrix becomes singular), the right-bottom element of the inverse $\frac{1}{\epsilon}$ goes to infinity. So the matrix 
$$J=\begin{pmatrix}
    3&0\\ 0&0
\end{pmatrix}
$$
isn't invertible and the pseudo inverse just replace the ''infinite-becoming'' term with 0:$$
J^\#=\begin{pmatrix}
        \frac{1}{3}&0\\0&0
    \end{pmatrix}.
$$
\textbf{Example}: Let's consider the following redundant full rank matrix $$J=\begin{pmatrix}
    1&0&2\\ 
    2&1&-1
\end{pmatrix}$$
we consider a generalized inverse, in particular, the right inverse:\begin{align*}
    &J=\begin{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
    1&0\\ 
    2&1
\end{pmatrix}& \begin{pmatrix}
    2\\-1
\end{pmatrix}
\end{pmatrix}\\ 
&K_r=\begin{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
    1&0\\ 
    2&1
\end{pmatrix}^{-1}\\\begin{pmatrix}
    0&0
\end{pmatrix}
\end{pmatrix}\\
&K_r=\begin{pmatrix}
    1&0\\
    -2&1\\
    0&0
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{align*}
We already seen in the Robotics 1 course that the pseudo inverse realizes the desired task velocity by choosing the joint's velocity $\dq$ of minimum norm. In particular, given the problem $J\dq=\dot{\mathbf y}$, even if $\dot{\mathbf y}$ is not in the range space of $J$, the pseudo inverse finds the minimum norm solution among the $\dq$ that minimizes\begin{equation}
    \|\dot{\mathbf y}-J\dq\|^2.
\end{equation}
If $J$ is singular and $JJ^T$ cannot be inverted, the formula $J^\#=J^T(JJ^T)^{-1}$ can't be used, and to find $J^\#$ we need numerical methods that relies on the \textit{singular value decomposition} of $J$.

\begin{boxdefinition}{SDV}{}
    For any matrix $J$, there exists a decomposition\begin{equation}
        J=U\Sigma V^T 
    \end{equation}
    where $U$ and $V$ are orthonormal matrices, and $\Sigma$ is a diagonal matrix.
\end{boxdefinition}
This decomposition have special properties, the columns of $U$ are the eigen vectors of $JJ^T$, and the columns of $V$ are the eigen vectors of $J^TJ$. The matrix $\Sigma$ have the following form:\begin{equation}
    \Sigma=\begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_1 & & & \\ 
        & \ddots & & 0_{m\times (n-m)}\\ 
        & & \sigma_m & 
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
it follows that\begin{align}
    &\sigma_1\ge\sigma_2\dots \ge \sigma_\rho>0\\
    &\sigma_{\rho+1}= \sigma_{\rho+2}\dots = \sigma_{m}=0
\end{align}
where $\rho$ is the rank of $J$. The numbers $\sigma_1\dots ,\sigma_\rho$ are the \textbf{singular values} of $J$. The eigen vectors of $JJ^T$ (columns of $U$) are associated to the eigen values $\sigma_1^2\dots,\sigma_\rho^2$. The last $n-\rho$ columns of $V$ are a basis for the null space of $J$.\begin{align}
    &J\mathbf v_i=\sigma_i\mathbf u_i, \ \ \ (i=1\dots, \rho)\\ 
    &J\mathbf  v_i=0, \ \ \ (i=\rho+1\dots n)
\end{align}
we denote $\mathbf u_i$ the $i$-th column vector of $U$ and $\mathbf v_i$ the $i$-th column vector of $V$. 
\begin{boxproposition}{}{}
    The column vectors of $U$ are the eigenvectors of $JJ^T$ associated to the eigen values $\sigma_1^2\dots,\sigma_\rho^2$.
\end{boxproposition}
\textit{Proof}: We want to show that a  vector $\mathbf u_i$ is an eigen vector of $JJ^T$. Since $J=U\Sigma V^T$ we have\begin{equation}
    JJ^T=U\Sigma V^T V \Sigma^T U^T
\end{equation}
since $V$ is orthonormal, we have that $V^T=V^{-1}$ so $V^TV=I$:\begin{align}
    &JJ^T=U\Sigma \blueText{V^T V}\Sigma^T U^T\\
    &JJ^T=U\Sigma \blueText{I}\Sigma^T U^T\\
    &JJ^T=U\Sigma\Sigma^T U^T
\end{align}
by considering the structure of $\Sigma$, we can easily see how $\Sigma\Sigma^T$ will be:\begin{equation}
    \Sigma\Sigma^T=\begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_1 & & & \\ 
        & \ddots & & 0_{m\times (n-m)}\\ 
        & & \sigma_m & 
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_1 & &  \\ 
        & \ddots & \\ 
        & & \sigma_m \\
        &0_{m\times (n-m)}&
    \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_1^2 & &  \\ 
        & \ddots &   \\ 
        & & \sigma_m^2 
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
so\begin{equation}
JJ^T=U\begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_1^2 & &  \\ 
        & \ddots &   \\ 
        & & \sigma_m^2 
    \end{pmatrix} U^T
\end{equation}
now we consider a vector column of $U$ and we multiply $JJ^T$ by that:\begin{equation}
    JJ^T\mathbf u_i=U\begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_1^2 & &  \\ 
        & \ddots &   \\ 
        & & \sigma_m^2 
    \end{pmatrix} U^T\mathbf u_i
\end{equation}
the result of $U^T\mathbf u_i$ is the column vector where each entry is the dot product between the column vectors of $U$ and $\mathbf u_i$:\begin{equation}
    U^T\mathbf u_i=
        \begin{pmatrix}
            \mathbf u_1^T\\ 
            \mathbf u_2^T\\ 
            \vdots \\ 
            \mathbf u_m^T
        \end{pmatrix}\mathbf u_i=\begin{pmatrix}
            \mathbf u_1^T\mathbf u_i\\ 
            \vdots \\ 
             \mathbf u_i^T\mathbf u_i\\ 
             \vdots \\ 
            \mathbf u_m^T\mathbf u_i\\ 
        \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
since $U$ is orthonormal, the column vectors are orthogonal and have unitary norm so\begin{equation}
    U^T\mathbf u_i=\begin{pmatrix}
        0 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}=\boldsymbol\delta_i
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol\delta_i$ is the column vector with $m$ component, all zeros except for the $i$-th entry that is 1. Now we can go back to  $JJ^T$:\begin{align}
    &JJ^T\mathbf u_i=U\begin{pmatrix}
        \sigma_1^2 & &  \\ 
        & \ddots &   \\ 
        & & \sigma_m^2 
    \end{pmatrix} \boldsymbol\delta_i=
    JJ^T\mathbf u_i=U\sigma_i^2\boldsymbol\delta_i\\=\sigma_i^2 U \boldsymbol\delta_i
\end{align}
clearly $U \boldsymbol\delta_i$ is the $i$-th column vector of $U$ so\begin{equation}
    JJ^T\mathbf u_i=\sigma_i^2\mathbf u_i
\end{equation}
\hfill$\blacksquare$\bigskip

\noindent As we already mention, the pseudo inverse (that we can compute with the SVD) is important since it gives the solution that minimizes the norm of the joint velocity $\dq$, this is a good property from an energetic point of view, since we always pursue the same task velocity by imposing less torque (hence, less electrical current).
\begin{boxtheorem}{}{}
    Consider the system $J\dq=\dy$ in the variables $\dq$, where $J$ is a full rank matrix with $n$ columns and $m$ rows ($n>m$). The solution given by $J^\#\dy=J^T(JJ^T)^{-1}\dy$ denoted $\dq_{PS}$ is the minimum norm solution that realizes $\dy$. 
\end{boxtheorem}
\textit{Proof}: let $\dq^*$ to be any other solution to $J\dq^*=\dy$. Since $J\dq^*=J\dq_{PS}$ it follows:\begin{align}
    &J\dq^*=J\dq_{PS}\implies \\
    &J\dq^*-J\dq_{PS}=\mathbf 0\implies \\
    &J(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})=\mathbf 0.
\end{align}\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth ]{images/PS_solution_1.eps}
\end{center}
Now we show that the vector $\dq^*-\dq_{PS}$ is orthogonal to $\dq_{PS}$:
\begin{align}
    &(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})^T\dq_{PS}=\\ &(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})^TJ^\#\dy=\\
    &\blueText{(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})^TJ^T}(JJ^T)^{-1}\dy=\\
    &\blueText{(J(\dq^*-\dq_{PS}))^T}(JJ^T)^{-1}\dy=\\
    &\blueText{(J\dq^*-J\dq_{PS})^T}(JJ^T)^{-1}\dy=\\
    &\blueText{(\dy-\dy)^T}(JJ^T)^{-1}\dy=\\
     &\blueText{\mathbf 0^T}(JJ^T)^{-1}\dy=0.
\end{align}
\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth ]{images/PS_solution_2.eps}
\end{center}
Now let's compute the norm of $\dq^*$:\begin{equation}
    \|\dq^*\|^2=\|\dq_{PS}+(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})\|^2
\end{equation}
since $\dq^*$ and $(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})$ are orthonormal, the norm of the sum is the sum of the two norms:\begin{equation}
    \|\dq_{PS}+(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})\|^2=\|\dq_{PS}\|^2+\|(\dq^*-\dq_{PS})\|^2\ge\|\dq_{PS}\|^2 
\end{equation}
so it is always grater or equal than the norm of $\dq_{PS}$. The proof is complete.\hfill$\blacksquare$\bigskip

We mentioned how the SVD is useful to compute the pseudo inverse when the matrix is not full rank. This is true due to the following relation:\begin{equation}
    J=U\Sigma V^T\implies J^\#=V\Sigma^\# U^T 
\end{equation}
and $\Sigma^\#$ is easy to compute:\begin{equation}\label{SigmaDiecis}
    \Sigma^\#=\begin{pmatrix}
        \nicefrac{1}{\sigma_1}& & &\\ 
        &\ddots &  & 0_{(m-\rho)\times (m-\rho)}\\ 
        & &  \nicefrac{1}{\sigma_\rho} &\\ 
         & &  0_{(n-m)\times m} &
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
so to compute $J^\#$:\begin{enumerate}
    \item We use numerical methods to compute the singular value decomposition of $J$ by finding $U,V,\Sigma$
    \item we compute $\Sigma^\#$ by using the form in equation \eqref{SigmaDiecis}
    \item we compute $J^\#=V\Sigma^\# U^T$.
\end{enumerate}

There is another way to solve the problem $J\dq=\dy$ by finding a solution that realizes $\dy$ but not minimize the norm of $\dq$, but a weighted norm given by a positive definite matrix $W$.
\begin{boxdefinition}{Weighted Pseudo Inverse}{}
    Given the problem $J\dq=\dy$, and a positive definite matrix $W$, the solution \begin{equation}
        \dq_W=J^\#_W\dy
    \end{equation}
    where\begin{equation}
        J^\#_W=W^{-1}J^T(JW^{-1}J^T)^{-1}
    \end{equation}
    minimizes the weighted norm\begin{equation}
        \|\dq\|^2_W=\dq^TW\dq.
    \end{equation}
\end{boxdefinition}
This kind of solution is used to give more emphases to some joints, for example, if $W$ is diagonal\begin{equation}
    W=\begin{pmatrix}
        W_1&\dots & 0\\ 
        \vdots &\ddots&\vdots \\ 
        0&\dots & W_n
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
a large weight $W_i$ will impose to find a solution where $\dot q_i$ is small. This can be used to find a solution that uses more joint velocity for the last joints that are near the end effector, since they have to support a smaller weight (and not that of the entire structure) and therefore less torque is required to move them compared to the joints at the base of the structure.\bigskip

There is a last Jacobian based method, called \textbf{Damped Least Squares (DLS)}. It can be proved that, the solution given by the pseudo inverse $J^\#$ is equivalent to the solution $\dq$ of the following optimization problem:\begin{equation}
    \begin{matrix}
        \displaystyle\min_{\dq} \|\dq\|^2\\ \\
        \text{such that }\|\dy-J\dq\|^2=0
    \end{matrix}
\end{equation}
this problem can be \textit{relaxed}: We might add a weighting term $\mu\ge 0$ that creates a compromise between large joint velocity and task accuracy: In practice, we allow a solution that achieves a task velocity that is not identical to the desired one, but with a lower norm. The optimization problem becomes unconstrained:\begin{equation}
    \displaystyle\min_{\dq} \frac{\mu}{2}\|\dq\|^2+\frac{1}{2}\|\dy-J\dq\|^2
\end{equation}
this induces a robust behavior when crossing singularities and applies equally to square and non-square matrices. Since it is a quadratic form, it admits an analytical solution:\begin{equation}
    \dq=J_{DLS}\dy=J^T(JJ^T+\mu I_m)^{-1}\dy
\end{equation}
the more the factor $\mu$ is big, the more importance will be given to the norm respect to the task error. The task error is\begin{equation}
    \dot{\mathbf e}=\mu(JJ^T+\mu I_m)^{-1}\dy
\end{equation}
of course, $\mu$ can be variable: Since when crossing a singularity the required joint velocity $\dq$ grows, so we can make $\mu$ inversely proportional to the minimum singular value $\sigma_\rho$ of $J$, that can be seen as a measure of distance from a singularity.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth ]{images/DLS}
    \caption{DLS solution near a singularity for a $2\times 2$ Jacobian.}
\end{figure}
We have seen four different Jacobian based methods:\begin{itemize}
    \item pseudo inverse: $\dq=J^\#\dy$
    \item weighted pseudo inverse: $\dq=J_W^\#\dy$
    \item right side inverse: $\dq=\begin{pmatrix}
        J_a^{-1}\\0
    \end{pmatrix}\dy$
    \item DLS: $\dq=J_{DLS}\dy$, this last one is not using a generalized inverse of $J$.
\end{itemize}
These solutions locally tries to avoid singularities, but there is no guarantees that that singularities are globally avoided, despite we are trying to minimize the joint velocity norm. Another issue of these local methods, is that leads to non repeatable motion: A periodic trajectory in the task space is not necessary mapped in the joint space as a periodic evolution of the joint's trajectory. This makes harder to predict if a given task movement will leads to a collision of the manipulator with obstacles in the workspace.
\subsection{Null Space Methods}
This method will exploit joint's movements that lies in the null space of the Jacobian matrix. Before entering in the details, let's recall the following theorem.
\begin{boxtheorem}{}{}
    Given a system of linear equations $A\mathbf x=\mathbf b$, any solution (if any) can be written in the form:\begin{equation}
        \mathbf x_p+\mathbf x_h 
    \end{equation}
    where $\mathbf x_p$ is a particular solution, and $\mathbf x_h$ is a solution of the homogeneous system $A\mathbf x=\mathbf 0$.
\end{boxtheorem}
since we are trying to solve $\dy=J\dq$, we will consider a general solution of the following form:\begin{equation}
    \dq = J^\#\dy + (I-J^\#J)\dq_N
\end{equation}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $J^\#\dy$ is a particular solution (we know that is the solution with minimum norm)
    \item $(I-J^\#J)\dq_N$ is a solution to the system $J\dy=\mathbf 0$.
\end{itemize}
Notice how $(I-J^\#J)$ is the \textbf{projection operator} on the null space of $J$. $\dq_N$ is an arbitrary joint velocity vector, that will be projected on the null space, in such way, this term will not influence the resulting task vector $\dy$.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth ]{images/qN.eps}
\end{figure}
\noindent In particular, $(I-J^\#J)$ is the \textit{orthogonal} projection operator, and have the following properties:\begin{itemize}
    \item is symmetric
    \item is idempotent: $(I-J^\#J)^2=(I-J^\#J)$
    \item $(I-J^\#J)^\#=(I-J^\#J)$
    \item $J^\#\dy$ is orthogonal to $(I-J^\#J)\dq_N$.
\end{itemize}
This kind of solution can be generalized by considering any couple of generalized inverse $K_1,K_2$:\begin{equation}
    \dq=K_1\dy+(I-K_2J)\dq_N 
\end{equation}
but will not have all of the four properties just presented. In this way, $\dq_N$ can be chosen arbitrarily as a ''preferred'' direction, and the null space projection of this desired velocity will be accomplished. Before entering in the details of how to choose this $\dq_N$, we will give a general presentation (not only related to Robotics) of an optimization problem.
\begin{boxdefinition}{Linear Quadratic Problem}{def:LQ}
    A \textbf{LQ problem} is the following optimization problem\begin{align}
        &\min_{\mathbf x\in \R^n}H(\mathbf x)=\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf x-\mathbf x_N)^TW(\mathbf x-\mathbf x_N)\\
        &\text{subject to } \ J\mathbf x=\mathbf b 
    \end{align}
    where\begin{itemize}
        \item $W\in Mat(n\times n)$, symmetric
        \item $J\in Mat(m\times n)$
        \item $\mathbf b\in \R^m$
        \item $\mathbf x_N\in \R^n$.
    \end{itemize}
\end{boxdefinition}
In our Robotics context, the matrix $W$ is also positive definite\begin{equation}
    \mathbf x^T W \mathbf x >0, \ \ \forall \mathbf x.
\end{equation}
This condition is sufficient to make the problem to have a minimum solution. A general LQ problem can be solved by adding $m$ additional variables $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ called \textbf{Lagrangian multipliers} and by considering the following function called \textbf{Lagrangian}:\begin{equation}
    \mathcal L(\mathbf x,\boldsymbol{\lambda})=H(\mathbf x)+\boldsymbol{\lambda}^T(J\mathbf x-\mathbf b).
\end{equation}
These Lagrangian multipliers works as a way to ''violate'' the constraints to find a solution. To find a solution, we have to find the critical points of $\mathcal L$, by considering the gradient respect to $\mathbf x$ and $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ and putting it equals to zero.\begin{align}
    &\nabla_{\mathbf x}\mathcal L=\begin{pmatrix}
        \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial x_1}&\dots & \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial x_n}
    \end{pmatrix}=W(\mathbf x-\mathbf x_N)+J^T\boldsymbol{\lambda}=\mathbf 0\\ 
    &\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\lambda}}\mathcal L=\begin{pmatrix}
        \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial \lambda_1}&\dots & \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial \lambda_m}
    \end{pmatrix}=J\mathbf x-\mathbf b=\mathbf 0
\end{align}
The sufficient condition to have a minimum is to have the Hessian matrix respect to $\mathbf x$ to be positive definite, as we already said, since:\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{\mathbf x}^2\mathcal L=W\succ 0
\end{equation}
We can solve the system of equations given by \begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        \nabla_{\mathbf x}\mathcal L=\mathbf 0\\ 
        \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\lambda}}\mathcal L=\mathbf 0
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
the first equation can be solved for $\mathbf x$:\begin{equation}\label{LQ1}
    \mathbf x=\mathbf x_N-W^{-1}J^T\boldsymbol{\lambda}
\end{equation}
by substituting in the second equation we get\begin{equation}
    J(\mathbf x_N-W^{-1}J^T\boldsymbol{\lambda})-\mathbf b=\mathbf 0
\end{equation}
hence\begin{equation}\label{LQ2}
    \boldsymbol{\lambda}=(JW^{-1}J^T)^{-1}(J\mathbf x_N-\mathbf b)
\end{equation}
By combining \eqref{LQ1} and \eqref{LQ2} we can find the general solution for $\mathbf x$:\begin{equation}
    \mathbf x=\mathbf x_N+W^{-1}J^T(JW^{-1}J^T)^{-1}(\mathbf b-J\mathbf x_N).
\end{equation}
here $\mathbf x_n$ acts as a preferred solution, in fact, if it satisfies the constraint $J\mathbf x_n=\mathbf b$, will be the solution. Let's switch back to the Robotics context. The problem to find a solution to $J\dq=\dy$ that minimizes the weighted squared norm $\|\dq\|_W^2$ is exactly an LQ problem.\begin{align*}
    &\min_{\dq} \frac{1}{2}(\dq-\dq_N)W(\dq-\dq_N)\\ 
    &\text{s.t. } J\dq=\dy
\end{align*}
and $\dq_N$ is the preferred solution. We know that the solution is exactly\begin{equation}
   \dq= \dq_N+W^{-1}J^T(JW^{-1}J^T)^{-1}(\dy-J\dq_N)
\end{equation}
notice how $W^{-1}J^T(JW^{-1}J^T)^{-1}$ is the weighted pseudo inverse $J^\#_W$:
\begin{equation}
   \dq= \dq_N+J^\#_W(\dy-J\dq_N)
\end{equation}
can be reorganized as follows
\begin{equation}
   \dq= J^\#_W\dy+(I-J^\#_WJ)\dq_N 
\end{equation}
that is exactly the sum of a particular solution ($J^\#_W\dy$) and the projection (not orthogonal) of $\dq_N$ on the null space of $J$.
\bigskip 

\noindent At this point, we have to define a way to choose (at each time step) the preferred direction $\dq_N$.\begin{quote}
    \sapText{Idea}: We can choose $\dq_N$ as the gradient of some performance metrics in function of the configuration.
\end{quote}
We define a scalar function $G(\mathbf q)$ that measures how desirable a configuration is, the gradient of this function will push the configuration towards the maximization of that function. So we define:\begin{equation}
    \dq_N=\nabla G
\end{equation}
In this way, while executing the desired trajectory $\mathbf y(t)$, we will let the robot to perform a self motion by trying to increase $G(\mathbf q)$. Note how, in the configuration space $\mathcal C$, that is a differentiable manifold, there is a \textbf{self motion manifold}, an embedded subspace of the configurations that are mapped in the same task vector $\mathbf y$. This manifold is given by all the configurations $\mathbf q$ that satisfies $k(\mathbf q)=\mathbf y$, where $ k$ is the direct kinematics.\bigskip

We know that $\mathcal C$ is a $n$-manifold, and $\mathcal T$ (the task space) is a $m$-manifold. When $n>m$ the dimension of the self motion manifold embedded in $\mathcal C$ is of course $n-m$.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth ]{images/nablaG.eps}
    \caption{The vector $\nabla G$ is pointing where $G$ is greater than the actual configuration value $G(\mathbf q^*)$. In particular, the preferred direction will be the projection of the gradient on the null space of $J$.}
\end{figure}
\begin{boxdefinition}{Projected Gradient}{}
    The \textbf{projected gradient} method is the procedure to use the gradient of the performance metric $G$ as the preferred direction $\dq_N$.
\end{boxdefinition}
Now let's see some example of metrics. One important choice, is to consider the \textit{distance from singularities} defined as follows\begin{equation}
    G_{man}(\mathbf q)=\sqrt{\det(J(\mathbf q)J^T(\mathbf q))}
\end{equation}
this measure is related to the volume of the manipulability ellipsoid, so, by trying to maximize it, the robot will go away from singular configurations.\bigskip 

$G_{man}(\mathbf q)$ is quite difficult to compute at each time step, since it require to compute the determinant of $JJ^T$, when dealing with a specific kinematic structure, we can find other metrics that are suitable and less expensive to compute.

For example, consider a 3R planar robot, we know that a singular configuration occurs when $q_2\in \{0,\pi\}$ or $q_3\in \{0,\pi\}$. A good choice of metrics can be\begin{equation}
    G(\mathbf q)=\sin^2q_2+\sin^2q_3
\end{equation}
it is true that $G$ and $G_{man}$ (for the 3R planar robot) shares the zeros: $G(\mathbf q)=0\iff G_{man}(\mathbf q)=0$, but $G$ is easier to compute.

Another useful choice for $G$ comes from the midpoints of the joints ranges, let \begin{align}
    &q_i\in[q_{im},q_{iM}]\\
    &\bar q_i=\frac{q_{im}+q_{iM}}{2}
\end{align}
to be the ranges for each joint $i$. The following function will be greater when the value of each joint is near to their mid point $\bar q_i$:\begin{equation}
    G_{mid}(\mathbf q)=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(\frac{q_i-\bar q_i}{q_{iM}-q_{im}}\right)^2
\end{equation}
These choice of $G$ are all differentiable, so it's always possible to compute the gradient $\nabla G$. The following function is not differentiable.\begin{boxdefinition}{Clearance}{}
    The \textbf{clearance} is the minimum distance between a point of the robot and an obstacles in the workspace.
\end{boxdefinition}
Let $robot\subset\R^3$ to be the set of all points on the surface of the robot structure, and let $obstacles\subset\R^3$ to be the set of points on the surface of all the obstacles in the workspace. Clearly, the points in $robot$ change with the configuration $\mathbf q$ according to the direct kinematics.

The clearance function is defined as follows:\begin{equation}
    H_{cle}(\mathbf q)=\min_{\begin{matrix}
    \mathbf p\in robot\\ \mathbf p' \in obstacles
    \end{matrix}}\|\mathbf p(\mathbf q)-\mathbf p'\|^h
\end{equation}
where $h$ is usually 1 or 2. Of course, this function is not practically computable if we consider the real shapes of the objects, that are composed by an infinite and continuous number of points displaced in a non formally describable way. To exploit this, usually the robot structure and the obstacles are modeled with simple shapes like cubes,parallelepiped or spheres, which surround the true shapes of the objects, so as to be conservative in terms of coverage (if such shapes do not collide, then the true objects do not collide either). \begin{boxproposition}{Decidability of the Distance between Two Polytopes}{}
    For any couple of convex shapes (polytopes) in the three dimensional space, it is possible to find the minimum distance between them.
\end{boxproposition}
Given these results, if we model the robotic structure and the obstacles as convex shapes, we can always compute $H_{cle}(\mathbf q)$ in any configuration.

So if $H_{cle}$ is chosen as $G$, then the robot will try to move towards a configuration that maximizes the distance from the obstacles. The problem is that $H_{cle}$ is not differentiable, since the nearest object to the robot can change during the motion. For example, let's consider a toy example of a robot with one revolut joint, and two obstacles in the workspace.

These jumps can cause vibrations in the control if not managed with smoothing techniques or potential functions (such as the barrier functions).\bigskip

At the end, after choosing an appropriate performance metric $G$ to maximize, the control input for the velocity is\begin{equation}
    \dq=J^\#\dy+(I-J^\#J)\nabla G 
\end{equation}
this equation can be reorganized to avoid the computation of the projector operator:\begin{equation}
    \dq=\nabla G+J^\#(\dy-J\nabla G)
\end{equation}
but the computation of the pseudo inverse $J^\#$ is still required, and is intensive since it requires numerical methods for the SVD. In principle, since $m$ of the $n$ configuration variables are used to accomplish the desired task trajectory, the additional complexity of the redundancy resolution method should depend only on the remaining $n-m$ (redundancy degree) variables.\bigskip

There is another method than relies on a different constrained optimization problems that should be more efficient than the projected gradient in situations where the Jacobian has full rank.
\begin{boxproposition}{Decomposition of joint space}{}
    If $\text{rank} J=m$ there exists a \textbf{decomposition} of the joint variables (possibly after a reordering) \begin{equation}
        \mathbf q=\begin{pmatrix}
            \mathbf q_a \\
            \mathbf q_b
        \end{pmatrix}
        \begin{array}{l}
            \in \R^m \\
            \in \R^{n-m}
        \end{array}
    \end{equation}
    such that the matrix \begin{equation}
        J_a=\left(\frac{\partial  k}{\partial \mathbf q_a}\right)
    \end{equation}
    is non singular.
\end{boxproposition}
from the \textit{implicit function theorem (Dini)}, given the direct kinematics\begin{equation}
    k(\mathbf q_a,\mathbf q_b)=\mathbf y
\end{equation}
we can rewrite $\mathbf q_a$ as a function of $\mathbf y$ and $\mathbf q_b$\begin{equation}
    \mathbf q_a=g(\mathbf y,\mathbf q_b)
\end{equation}
in general, is hard to find an explicit expression for $g$, but this is not needed: we consider the partial derivative of $g$ respect to $\mathbf q_b$:\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{\mathbf q_b}g=\left(\frac{\partial  g}{\partial \mathbf q_b}\right)
\end{equation}
this is equals to\begin{equation}
    \left(\frac{\partial  g}{\partial \mathbf q_b}\right)=-\left(\frac{\partial  k}{\partial \mathbf q_a}\right)^{-1}\left(\frac{\partial  k}{\partial \mathbf q_b}\right)=-J_a^{-1}J_b\in Mat(m\times n-m).
\end{equation}
The $n-m$ variables $\mathbf q_b$ can be selected independently to optimize some performance metrics while the remaining $m$ variables $\mathbf q_a$ are used to correctly execute the task $\mathbf q_a=g(\mathbf y,\mathbf q_b)$, at the velocity level by controlling $\dq$.\bigskip 

The \textbf{Reduced Gradient} method aims to perform a step in the gradient direction just for the reduced $n-m$ variables $\dq_b$, then, the remaining velocity for $\dq_a$ is computed in function of the just found $\dq_b$ and the desired task velocity $\dy$.
\begin{equation}
    G(\mathbf q)=G(\mathbf q_a,\mathbf q_b)
\end{equation}
$\mathbf q_a$ is expressed in term of $\mathbf q_b$ and the desired $\mathbf y$ so $G$ can be written as a function of $\mathbf q_b$ only:\begin{equation}
    G(\mathbf q)=G(\mathbf q_a,\mathbf q_b)=G(g(\mathbf y,\mathbf q_b),\mathbf q_b)=G'(\mathbf q_b)
\end{equation}
we don't have to compute $g$ since at differential level we can compute $\nabla_{\mathbf q_b}G'$\begin{align}
    &\nabla_{\mathbf q_b}G'=
    \left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial \mathbf q_a}\right)
    \left(\frac{\partial g}{\partial \mathbf q_b}\right)+
    \left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial \mathbf q_b}\right)=\\ 
    &\left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial \mathbf q_a}\right)(-J_a^{-1}J_b)+
    \left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial \mathbf q_b}\right)=\\ 
    &\begin{pmatrix}\displaystyle
        \frac{\partial G}{\partial \mathbf q_a}&\displaystyle\frac{\partial G}{\partial \mathbf q_b}
    \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
        -J_a^{-1}J_b\\ I
    \end{pmatrix}=\\ 
    &\Big(
        -(J_a^{-1}J_b)^T \ \ I_{n-m}
   \Big)\nabla_{\mathbf q}G
\end{align}
At each time step, the algorithm to compute $\dot q$ is the following\begin{enumerate}
    \item compute the velocities for $\mathbf q_b$ to maximize the metric $G$: $\dq_b=\nabla_{\mathbf q_b}G'$
    \item we have to solve the equation $J_a\dq_a+J_b\dq_b=\dy$ for $\dq_a$ to satisfy the $m$ task constraints 
    \item $\dq_a=J_a^{-1}(\dy-J_b\dq_b)$
\end{enumerate}
this method is analytically simpler respect to the projected gradient since doesn't require to calculate the pseudo inverse $J^\#$, but requires to search a non-singular minor $J_a$.
